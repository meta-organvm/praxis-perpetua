## ABSTRACT

This dissertation presents the design, implementation, and theoretical grounding of a *self-governing evaluative authority* — a domain-agnostic system that can be attached to any structured operational pipeline to provide continuous, multi-perspective quality assessment with statistical agreement measurement and recursive self-correction.

The authority comprises five architectural layers: (1) a constitutional rubric defining quality dimensions with objective/subjective partition and weighted scoring guides; (2) a deliberative panel of AI evaluators operating under distinct personas with explicit scoring biases; (3) a judicial consensus mechanism using ICC(2,1), Cohen's kappa, and Fleiss' kappa with outlier detection and minority-opinion preservation; (4) an executive feedback system coupling evaluative findings back into operational parameters; and (5) a meta-evaluative layer enabling the authority to assess its own governance integrity.

Drawing on organizational cybernetics (Beer, 1972, 1979, 1985), autopoietic systems theory (Maturana & Varela, 1980; Luhmann, 1995), psychometric inter-rater reliability (Shrout & Fleiss, 1979), and the emerging LLM-as-judge literature (Zheng et al., 2023), the dissertation argues that these five layers constitute the minimal viable governance architecture for any AI-augmented institution operating at scale beyond a single operator's direct evaluative capacity.

The authority was first instantiated against a career application pipeline (133 modules, 2,000+ tests, 9 quality dimensions, 4-rater panel spanning 2 model families), achieving ICC = 1.0 ("almost perfect" agreement). The dissertation then demonstrates — through formal specification, adapter interface design, and theoretical case analysis — how the same authority generalizes to software governance across 105 repositories, essay quality evaluation, generative art assessment, academic peer review, and CI/CD quality gating.

The central contribution is the reframing of AI-model evaluative diversity: not as noise to be minimized (the dominant framing in the LLM-as-judge literature) but as a *governance signal* analogous to productive disagreement in human deliberative bodies. When diverse evaluators agree, the quality assessment is robust. When they disagree, the disagreement pattern reveals which quality dimensions are contested and from which institutional perspective — information that no single evaluator, however capable, can produce.

**Keywords:** organizational cybernetics, viable system model, inter-rater agreement, autopoiesis, LLM-as-judge, institutional governance, self-referential systems, recursive evaluation, persona-driven assessment
