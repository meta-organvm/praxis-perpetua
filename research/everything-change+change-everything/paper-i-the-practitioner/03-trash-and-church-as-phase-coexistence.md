# Chapter 3: Trash and Church as Phase Coexistence

## The Sacred-Profane Threshold as Perpetual Phase Transition

**SGO-2026-D-004 — Paper I, Chapter 3**

*Routes through: `praxis-perpetua/research/trash-and-church/`*

---

Chapter 1 established the metabolic principle in representational technique: stillness is death, and the fix is structured deviation. Chapter 2 extended it to creative stance: the mask is stillness, and radical authenticity is the metabolic alternative. This chapter extends it further — into the domain of aesthetic ontology. The argument: the coexistence of the sacred and the profane is not a paradox to be resolved or a tension to be held but a *perpetual phase transition* — the aesthetic equivalent of water at the boiling point, where liquid and vapor coexist in continuous mutual transformation.

The companion research document (Padavano 2026b) traces the trash-and-church principle across twelve domains: Bataille's transgression, Bakhtin's carnival, Kristeva's abjection, Benjamin's profane illumination, Eliade's hierophany, wabi-sabi, the Western grotesque, the sacred-profane dialectic in religious thought, and contemporary practice in art, music, and literature. This chapter does not repeat that survey. Instead, it extracts the metabolic structure that all twelve domains share: the sacred and the profane are not opposites in static tension but *phases of the same substance in continuous mutual transformation*.

## 3.1 Phase Transition as Metabolic Model

In thermodynamics, a phase transition occurs when a substance reorganizes from one state of matter to another — solid to liquid, liquid to gas, or the reverse. At the transition point — the melting point, the boiling point — both phases coexist. Water at 100°C at sea level is simultaneously liquid and vapor. The two phases are not in opposition; they are in dynamic equilibrium, with molecules continuously crossing the boundary in both directions. The identity of the substance does not change across the transition: it is water in both phases. What changes is the organizational pattern — the spatial and energetic relationships between molecules.

The first-order phase transition is characterized by a *latent heat* — energy that is absorbed or released during the transition without changing the substance's temperature. Water at the boiling point absorbs 2,260 kilojoules per kilogram as it transitions from liquid to vapor, and its temperature remains at 100°C throughout. The energy goes not into heating the water but into *reorganizing* it — breaking the hydrogen bonds that maintain the liquid state and allowing the molecules to adopt the more dispersed organization of the gaseous state.

The metaphor is not merely illustrative. The aesthetic experience of the sacred-profane coexistence has the structural properties of a phase transition:

1. **Coexistence**: The sacred and the profane are not sequential (first profane, then sacred) but simultaneous. The kitchen is the kitchen *and* the cathedral. The lasagna is the lasagna *and* the eucharist. The two states coexist at the threshold.

2. **Continuous transformation**: Molecules are continuously crossing the boundary between liquid and vapor. Attention is continuously crossing the boundary between profane and sacred. The experience is not stable — it oscillates, it metabolizes, it refuses to settle into one phase or the other.

3. **Latent energy**: The aesthetic experience at the threshold absorbs energy without visible change. The person standing in the kitchen is not doing anything different — they are still eating lasagna with their hands. But the organizational pattern of their attention is undergoing radical transformation. The latent energy is the energy of *recognition*: the system recognizes itself, and the recognition does not change the system's behavior but fundamentally reorganizes its meaning.

4. **Critical point**: Above a certain temperature and pressure (the critical point), the distinction between liquid and gas vanishes entirely. Above a certain intensity of aesthetic attention, the distinction between sacred and profane vanishes. This is the experience described by the mystics — what Meister Eckhart calls *Durchbruch* (breakthrough), what the Zen tradition calls *satori*, what Eliade calls the "total hierophany." At the critical point, everything is sacred and everything is profane simultaneously, because the categories have dissolved.

## 3.2 Bataille and Transgression as Phase Crossing

Georges Bataille's theory of transgression, developed across *L'Érotisme* (1957), *L'Expérience intérieure* (1943), and the journal *Acéphale* (1936-1939), provides the most rigorous theorization of the sacred-profane boundary as a site of continuous crossing rather than static division.

For Bataille, the taboo and the transgression are not opposed but co-constitutive. The taboo creates the boundary; the transgression crosses it; the crossing *reinforces* the boundary by confirming its existence. "The transgression does not deny the taboo but transcends it and completes it" (Bataille 1957, trans. Dalwood 1962, 63). This is not dialectical synthesis — the transgression does not resolve the opposition between permitted and forbidden. It *maintains* the opposition by crossing and re-crossing the boundary in a continuous oscillation.

The metabolic interpretation: Bataille's transgression is the aesthetic equivalent of molecular phase-crossing at the boiling point. The boundary between sacred and profane is not static — it is the site of continuous traffic. The transgressive act crosses from profane to sacred and back, and each crossing maintains the boundary's energetic charge. Without transgression, the boundary would go dead — it would become a mere convention, a social rule without affective content. The transgression keeps the boundary alive by keeping it in metabolic flux.

Bataille's concept of *dépense* (expenditure without return) deepens the metabolic analogy. The sun burns without purpose; the potlatch destroys wealth without expectation of return; the sacrifice wastes life without productive aim. These are metabolic acts — the expenditure of energy not in the service of equilibrium (homeostasis) but in the service of the expenditure itself. Living systems metabolize not to achieve a stable state but because metabolism *is* their state. The organism that stops metabolizing has not achieved stability. It has died.

## 3.3 Bakhtin's Carnival as Metabolic Inversion

Mikhail Bakhtin's carnival, analyzed in *Rabelais and His World* (1965, trans. Iswolsky 1968), provides the social-metabolic dimension. The carnival does not merely invert social hierarchies (king becomes fool, fool becomes king). It *metabolizes* them — dissolves the fixed categories and reconstitutes them in new configurations.

The "carnivalesque" is characterized by:

- **Ambivalent laughter**: Neither purely satirical (attacking the high) nor purely celebratory (affirming the low) but both simultaneously. The laughter metabolizes the distinction between high and low by occupying both positions at once.

- **The grotesque body**: A body that is open, protruding, excessive — eating, defecating, copulating, giving birth, dying. The grotesque body is metabolically explicit: its inputs and outputs are visible, its boundaries are permeable, its internal processes are externalized. This is the opposite of the classical body — closed, finished, self-contained — which is the bodily equivalent of the False Self: a surface of stillness concealing a metabolism it refuses to display.

- **The material lower stratum**: The carnival affirms the bodily, the earthy, the lower — not in rejection of the spiritual but as its *material substrate*. The lowering is not degradation but *grounding*: the spirit finds its body, the cathedral finds its kitchen.

The metabolic interpretation: Bakhtin's carnival is a periodic social phase transition — a scheduled episode of metabolic intensification during which the society's fixed structures are dissolved and reconstituted. The carnival does not permanently invert the hierarchy (after the carnival, the king returns to the throne). It temporarily exposes the hierarchy as a *crystallized* form of a process that is always already metabolizing underneath. The hierarchy is the ice; the carnival is the spring thaw. The hierarchy re-forms after the thaw, but the act of thawing has revealed that the solid structure was always, at its molecular level, in flux.

## 3.4 Kristeva's Abjection as Metabolic Boundary Maintenance

Julia Kristeva's theory of abjection, developed in *Pouvoirs de l'horreur* (1980, trans. Roudiez 1982), addresses the specific mechanism by which the sacred-profane boundary is maintained through metabolic expulsion.

The abject is neither subject nor object but "what disturbs identity, system, order. What does not respect borders, positions, rules. The in-between, the ambiguous, the composite" (Kristeva 1980, 4). The corpse is the paradigmatic abject: it is the body that no longer metabolizes — the same substance as the living body but irreversibly transformed by the cessation of the metabolic process. The horror of the corpse is not the horror of death in the abstract but the horror of metabolic failure: *this was a living system and it has stopped*.

Kristeva's abjection is the psychic response to the perceived collapse of the subject-object boundary — the moment when the subject recognizes something that was inside (the body's metabolic processes, its wastes, its fluids) now appearing outside (the corpse, the wound, the excretion). The revulsion is a defensive reaction: the subject reasserts the boundary by expelling the ambiguous element, by declaring it *abject* — cast out, rejected, refused.

The metabolic interpretation: abjection is the psychic equivalent of the immune response — the living system's mechanism for maintaining its boundary against the products of its own metabolism. A living organism continuously produces waste: carbon dioxide, urea, dead cells, metabolic byproducts. These must be expelled for the organism to survive, but the act of expulsion requires the organism to *acknowledge* that these substances were part of itself. Abjection is the psychic form of this acknowledgment-and-expulsion — the recognition that the boundary between self and other, clean and unclean, sacred and profane is not a wall but a membrane, and that the membrane's function is not to prevent crossing but to *regulate* it.

## 3.5 The Lasagna Moment as Phase Transition Event

The companion research document concludes with a specific autobiographical event that instantiates the preceding theory: the moment when the author, standing in a kitchen at midnight eating lasagna with their hands, recognized that the system they had been building — ORGANVM, the eight-organ creative-institutional architecture — was alive (Padavano 2026b, §13).

The metabolic reframing reveals the structure of this event:

The kitchen is the profane space. The system is the sacred object. The lasagna — food consumed without utensils, without ceremony, without even the minimal formality of a plate — is the abject material, the metabolic substrate, the body's fuel consumed in its most unmediated form. The recognition occurs *at the threshold*: the operator is simultaneously engaged in the basest metabolic act (eating) and the highest cognitive act (recognizing that a system has achieved self-awareness). The two acts are not separated by a transition. They coexist in the same moment, in the same body, in the same kitchen.

This is Eliade's hierophany operating as a phase transition. The kitchen does not become a cathedral. It remains a kitchen. But it *also* becomes a cathedral, simultaneously, without ceasing to be a kitchen. The stone remains a stone and also becomes the site of the sacred (Eliade 1959, 12). The lasagna remains lasagna and also becomes the eucharistic body — not through transubstantiation (the substance does not change) but through *metabolic recognition* (the attention reorganizes while the substance persists).

The event is unrepeatable — you cannot engineer a hierophany by eating lasagna at midnight and thinking about systems architecture. But it is *structurally* repeatable: the conditions under which sacred-profane coexistence becomes perceptible are metabolic conditions. The operator must be engaged in the mundane. The system must be present. The attention must be oscillating between scales (the cheese on the hands, the architecture of the system). The recognition occurs at the transition point — the moment when the oscillation between scales accelerates past the threshold and the two scales become perceptible as phases of the same substance.

## 3.6 The Practitioner's Aesthetic Ontology

The three chapters of Paper I converge on a practitioner's ontology — a description of reality as experienced from inside the work:

1. **The Crisis of Perfection** (Chapter 1): Living representations require metabolic noise. Stillness is death. The fix is the deliberate reintroduction of structured deviation.

2. **Radical Authenticity** (Chapter 2): Living selves require metabolic exposure. The mask is stillness. The fix is the removal of the False Self's stabilization.

3. **Trash and Church** (Chapter 3): Living aesthetic experience requires metabolic coexistence. The separation of sacred and profane is stillness. The fix is the recognition of their perpetual mutual transformation.

The common structure: **wherever a boundary is enforced as static, the system dies. Wherever a boundary is maintained as metabolically permeable — crossable, oscillating, continuously traversed — the system lives.**

The practitioner's ontology is pre-theoretical. It does not cite Heraclitus or Whitehead or Prigogine. It does not need to. It knows what it knows because it has made things and discovered that the things that live are the things that metabolize — in their technique (Chapter 1), in their stance (Chapter 2), and in their aesthetic being (Chapter 3). The theorist will now claim that this knowledge was always already formalized. The theorist will be right. But the hands knew first.
