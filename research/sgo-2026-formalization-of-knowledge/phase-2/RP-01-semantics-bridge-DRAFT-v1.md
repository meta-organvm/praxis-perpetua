# Toward a Grand Unified Semantics: Compositionality, Lambda Calculus, and the Structural Unity of Meaning Across Logic, Computation, and Natural Language

**Studium Generale ORGANVM -- Research Program RP-01**

---

## Abstract

The word "semantics" names three distinct academic traditions: logical semantics, in which meaning is truth-in-a-structure (Tarski, Kripke); programming language semantics, in which meaning is mathematical denotation, operational behaviour, or axiomatic specification (Scott, Strachey, Hoare); and formal natural language semantics, in which meaning is truth-conditional content compositionally assigned to syntactic structures (Montague, Heim, Kratzer). Each tradition claims to study "meaning," yet each operates with different formalisms, different standards of adequacy, and different philosophical presuppositions. This dissertation investigates whether these three traditions are unified by deep structural bridges or whether they merely share a homonymous label. Through systematic analysis of eight identified bridge structures -- compositionality, lambda calculus, the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence, type theory, model theory, game semantics, dynamic semantics, and proof-theoretic semantics -- we argue that the three traditions are not merely analogous but are instances of a single mathematical framework, with category theory providing the most perspicuous unifying metalanguage. For each bridge, we assess whether the cross-domain parallel constitutes genuine isomorphism, homomorphism, or mere analogy. We identify the precise points at which unification breaks down -- concurrency, pragmatics, distributional meaning, and compositionality failures -- and argue that these breakdowns are instructive rather than fatal, revealing the boundary conditions under which a unified semantics is achievable. The central finding is that the classical, compositional, typed fragment of each tradition admits genuine structural unification, while extensions beyond this fragment (context-dependence, non-determinism, statistical meaning) require weaker but still informative cross-domain correspondences.

**Keywords:** formal semantics, compositionality, lambda calculus, Curry-Howard correspondence, type theory, category theory, game semantics, dynamic semantics, model theory, proof-theoretic semantics, distributional semantics, Montague grammar

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Homonymy Problem

The term "semantics" occupies a peculiar position in the landscape of formal inquiry. It appears, with apparently equal legitimacy, in at least three distinct disciplinary contexts. In mathematical logic, semantics is the study of truth, validity, and logical consequence relative to mathematical structures -- models, in the technical sense of Tarski (1936). In computer science, semantics is the rigorous mathematical study of the meaning of programming languages, encompassing denotational, operational, and axiomatic approaches (Scott and Strachey 1971; Plotkin 1981; Hoare 1969). In linguistics and the philosophy of language, semantics is the study of the meaning of natural language expressions, typically pursued through truth-conditional analysis, compositional type-theoretic methods, and model-theoretic interpretation (Montague 1973; Heim and Kratzer 1998).

This terminological convergence raises a question that is at once philosophical, mathematical, and practical: do these three traditions study the same thing? Is there a single, coherent notion of "meaning" that the three traditions address from different angles, or does the shared label conceal fundamentally different enterprises? The question is not merely taxonomic. If the three traditions are genuinely unified, then results in one domain should transfer to others; formalisms developed for one purpose should illuminate the others; and the apparent convergences that have been remarked upon by researchers in all three fields should admit of principled explanation. If, on the other hand, the shared label is merely homonymous, then the apparent convergences are accidental, and attempts to import insights across domains are likely to produce confusion rather than clarity.

The present dissertation takes this question as its central problematic. It proceeds not by philosophical argument alone, but by systematic identification and formal analysis of eight structural bridges -- mathematical constructs, principles, and frameworks that appear, with varying degrees of structural fidelity, in all three traditions. For each bridge, the dissertation asks: is this a genuine isomorphism (a structure-preserving bijection), a homomorphism (a structure-preserving map that may lose information), an analogy (a suggestive but informal parallel), or a mere metaphor (a terminological coincidence with no structural content)? The resulting taxonomy of bridge strengths constitutes, we argue, the most systematic assessment to date of the prospects for a unified theory of meaning.

### 1.2 Research Questions

Three research questions guide the investigation:

**RQ1.** To what extent does the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence provide a genuine structural unification of meaning across logic, computation, and natural language, and where does this unification break down?

**RQ2.** Is dynamic semantics -- the treatment of meaning as context-change potential -- a more natural unifying principle than truth-conditional semantics for bridging the three traditions, and what formal evidence supports or undermines this claim?

**RQ3.** Can distributional semantics -- the statistical and geometric theory of meaning underlying modern neural language processing -- be reconciled with the formal semantic traditions, or does it constitute an irreducibly different theory of meaning?

### 1.3 Contribution and Scope

The principal contribution of this dissertation is a systematic taxonomy of cross-domain semantic bridges, ranked by structural strength on a four-point scale: isomorphism, homomorphism, analogy, and metaphor. This taxonomy is grounded in precise mathematical characterizations of each bridge and explicit identification of the conditions under which each bridge holds and fails.

The scope is necessarily broad. A dissertation that spans mathematical logic, programming language theory, and formal linguistics cannot achieve the depth of a monograph focused on any one of these fields. The compensating virtue is synoptic: by surveying all three traditions simultaneously, the dissertation reveals structural connections that are invisible from within any single tradition. The ambition is not to produce new results within any of the three fields, but to produce a new understanding of the relationships among them.

The remainder of the dissertation is organized as follows. Section 2 surveys the three traditions of semantics, establishing the terminology and formalisms that subsequent sections presuppose. Section 3, the core of the dissertation, presents the eight bridges in detail. Section 4 examines the role of category theory as a candidate unifying metalanguage. Section 5 identifies the points at which unification breaks down. Section 6 draws implications for philosophy, computer science, linguistics, and system design. Sections 7 and 8 offer discussion and conclusions.

## 2. Three Traditions of Semantics

### 2.1 Logical Semantics

The logical tradition of semantics has its modern origins in the work of Gottlob Frege, whose *Begriffsschrift* (1879) and subsequent writings established the framework of quantificational logic and introduced the distinction between sense (*Sinn*) and reference (*Bedeutung*) that would shape all subsequent theorizing about meaning. Frege's insight that the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts -- the principle of compositionality, to which we return at length in Section 3.1 -- provided the foundational axiom for formal approaches to meaning in all three traditions.

The decisive technical development was Alfred Tarski's semantic theory of truth (1936), which provided a rigorous mathematical definition of truth for formalized languages. Tarski's central device was the notion of *satisfaction*: a formula is satisfied by a sequence of objects just in case the formula, under the assignment of those objects to its variables, holds in a given structure. The recursive definition of satisfaction proceeds compositionally through the logical connectives and quantifiers: a conjunction is satisfied if and only if both conjuncts are satisfied; an existential quantification is satisfied if and only if some extension of the given assignment satisfies the quantified formula; and so on. The result is a precise mathematical account of what it means for a sentence to be true in a structure -- a *model* -- and thereby a precise account of logical consequence: a sentence follows from a set of premises just in case every model that satisfies the premises also satisfies the sentence.

Tarski's framework inaugurated *model-theoretic semantics* as the dominant paradigm in logical semantics. Model theory, as developed by Tarski, Abraham Robinson, and subsequently by Saharon Shelah and others, studies the relationship between formal theories (collections of sentences in a formal language) and their models (mathematical structures in which those sentences hold). The fundamental concepts are satisfaction, truth-in-a-structure, elementary equivalence (two structures satisfying exactly the same sentences), and categoricity (a theory having, up to isomorphism, exactly one model of a given cardinality). Model theory provides the mathematical substrate upon which both natural language semantics (Section 2.3) and certain approaches to programming language semantics (Section 2.2) are constructed.

Saul Kripke's possible-worlds semantics (1959, 1963) extended the model-theoretic framework to modal logic. A Kripke frame consists of a set of possible worlds and an accessibility relation between them; a modal formula is evaluated not at a single world but across worlds related by the accessibility relation. The formula "necessarily *p*" is true at a world *w* just in case *p* is true at every world accessible from *w*. This framework provided rigorous semantics for the modal operators of necessity and possibility and was subsequently extended to epistemic logic (knowledge and belief), deontic logic (obligation and permission), temporal logic, and other non-classical logics. For natural language semantics, possible-worlds semantics proved especially consequential: Montague (1973) employed Kripke-style intensional models as the interpretive structures for natural language, treating the intension of an expression as a function from possible worlds to extensions.

A distinct tradition within logical semantics, *proof-theoretic semantics*, locates meaning not in truth conditions or models but in inferential role. On this view, associated with Gerhard Gentzen, Michael Dummett, and Dag Prawitz, the meaning of a logical connective is given by its introduction and elimination rules in natural deduction. The conjunction introduction rule, for example, says that from *A* and *B* one may infer *A* and *B*; the conjunction elimination rules say that from *A* and *B* one may infer *A* and, separately, *B*. The meaning of conjunction *is* the pattern of inference it licenses. This approach connects naturally to computer science through the Curry-Howard correspondence (Section 3.3), under which introduction rules correspond to constructors and elimination rules correspond to destructors in a typed programming language.

A third approach, *game semantics* in logic, originated in the work of Paul Lorenzen and Kuno Lorenz in the 1950s and 1960s and was independently developed by Jaakko Hintikka. On this view, the truth of a formula is cashed out in terms of the existence of a winning strategy for a designated player (the "Proponent" or "Verifier") in a game whose rules are determined by the formula's logical structure. Universal quantification corresponds to a move by the Opponent (who chooses a witness); existential quantification corresponds to a move by the Proponent (who chooses a witness); conjunction and disjunction are similarly distributed. A formula is logically valid if the Proponent has a winning strategy regardless of the Opponent's choices. This game-theoretic perspective provides an interactive, dynamic alternative to the static model-theoretic approach, and it has found important applications outside logic, as we shall see in Sections 2.2 and 3.6.

Finally, *algebraic semantics* in logic represents logical systems by algebraic structures: Boolean algebras for classical propositional logic, Heyting algebras for intuitionistic logic, modal algebras for modal logics, and MV-algebras for many-valued logics. The Lindenbaum-Tarski construction associates to any logical theory an algebra whose elements are equivalence classes of formulae (under provable equivalence) and whose operations correspond to the logical connectives. Algebraic semantics provides an alternative to model-theoretic semantics using algebraic rather than set-theoretic tools, and it connects to computer science through the algebraic specification of abstract data types.

### 2.2 Programming Language Semantics

The semantics of programming languages became a subject of rigorous mathematical investigation in the late 1960s, motivated by the need for precise specifications of what programs mean -- specifications that could ground claims of compiler correctness, support formal verification, and inform language design. Three approaches emerged, each embodying a different philosophical stance on what it means to assign meaning to a program.

**Denotational semantics**, developed by Dana Scott and Christopher Strachey beginning around 1969 and first published systematically in 1971, assigns meaning to programs by mapping them to mathematical objects -- *denotations* -- in suitably structured mathematical domains. The meaning of an expression is a mathematical entity (a function, a set, an element of a lattice) that abstracts away from the mechanics of execution and captures what the expression "denotes." The central technical innovation was Scott's development of *domain theory*: the identification of appropriate mathematical structures -- complete lattices, continuous lattices, and more generally directed-complete partial orders (dcpos) -- in which to interpret programs. Domain theory formalizes the intuitive notion of *approximation*: a partial computation approximates a total computation, and the meaning of a recursive program is obtained as the least fixed point of a continuous operator on a domain. Scott's fixed-point theorem guarantees the existence of such least fixed points under appropriate continuity conditions.

The hallmark of denotational semantics is *compositionality*: the denotation of a compound expression is built from the denotations of its subexpressions according to the rules of syntactic composition. This principle, which Scott and Strachey adopted from mathematical logic (and ultimately from Frege), is the same principle that governs Montague's approach to natural language semantics. Indeed, the parallel is not accidental: Montague and Scott were colleagues at UCLA in the late 1960s, and the intellectual cross-pollination between the two programs has been noted by historians of both fields.

**Operational semantics** assigns meaning to programs by specifying how they execute. There are two principal styles. *Structural operational semantics* (small-step), formalized by Gordon Plotkin (1981), defines a transition relation on configurations (pairs of a program fragment and a state or environment), specifying how each syntactic construct transforms one configuration into another. The meaning of a program is the sequence (or tree, or graph) of transitions it induces. *Natural semantics* (big-step), associated with Gilles Kahn, defines a relation between initial and final configurations, specifying what overall result each program produces without specifying intermediate steps. The lambda calculus, introduced by Alonzo Church in the 1930s as a foundation for mathematical logic, served as the first formal operational semantics avant la lettre: beta-reduction (the substitution of an argument for a bound variable in a function body) defines the computational steps of functional programs.

**Axiomatic semantics**, originated by Robert Floyd (1967) and formalized by C.A.R. Hoare (1969), defines the meaning of a program not by what it computes or how it executes, but by what is *true* before and after its execution. The central device is the *Hoare triple* {*P*} *C* {*Q*}, which asserts: if the precondition *P* holds before the execution of command *C*, then the postcondition *Q* will hold after execution (assuming termination, in the case of partial correctness). The meaning of a program construct is given by a proof rule that relates preconditions to postconditions. For example, the rule for sequential composition states that if {*P*} *C*_1 {*R*} and {*R*} *C*_2 {*Q*}, then {*P*} *C*_1; *C*_2 {*Q*}. Axiomatic semantics is the most explicitly logic-adjacent of the three approaches: it imports the apparatus of logical assertion, entailment, and proof directly into the specification of program meaning. The preconditions and postconditions are logical formulae; the proof rules are instances of logical inference; and program correctness is a theorem to be proved.

The three approaches are not competitors but complementary perspectives. Soundness and completeness results relate them: a denotational semantics is *adequate* with respect to an operational semantics if denotational equality implies observational equivalence (programs with the same denotation behave the same in all contexts); it is *fully abstract* if the converse also holds (observationally equivalent programs have the same denotation). The problem of full abstraction -- constructing denotational models that are fully abstract with respect to operational semantics -- has been one of the driving problems of the field, and its solution for the language PCF by means of game semantics (Abramsky, Jagadeesan, and Malacaria 1994; Hyland and Ong 2000) constitutes one of the great achievements of the discipline.

### 2.3 Natural Language Semantics

The formal study of natural language meaning has its roots in the philosophical tradition of Frege, Russell, and the early Wittgenstein, but it became a systematic enterprise with the work of Richard Montague in the late 1960s and early 1970s. Montague's radical thesis was stated with characteristic directness: "I reject the contention that an important theoretical difference exists between formal and natural languages" (Montague 1970b, p. 222). On this view, natural language is amenable to the same rigorous semantic analysis as the formal languages of logic, and the tools of mathematical logic -- higher-order predicate logic, lambda calculus, type theory, and model theory -- can be applied to the interpretation of English (and, by extension, any natural language) without essential modification.

**Montague grammar** implements this program by means of an intensional logic based on Kripke models. The syntax of English is described by a categorial grammar in which expressions are assigned syntactic categories (e.g., noun phrase, verb phrase, sentence). Each syntactic category corresponds to a semantic type: entities have type *e*, truth values have type *t*, and functions have types built from these base types (e.g., an intransitive verb has type *e* -> *t*, taking an entity and returning a truth value). The semantic interpretation of an expression is a function from possible worlds and times to extensions of the appropriate type -- an *intension*. Lambda calculus provides the notation for combining meanings: the meaning of "every man walks" is derived by lambda-abstracting over the subject position in the verb phrase meaning and applying a generalized quantifier. The crucial feature is that the semantic interpretation is *compositional*: the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts and the syntactic rule by which they are combined. This is the same compositionality principle that governs denotational semantics in computer science and recursive satisfaction in model-theoretic logic.

The Montague paradigm, despite its elegance, faced significant challenges from phenomena that resist sentence-level, truth-conditional treatment. **Discourse representation theory** (DRT), developed independently by Hans Kamp (1981) and Irene Heim (1982), addressed the problem of *cross-sentential anaphora* -- the phenomenon whereby a pronoun in one sentence can refer back to an indefinite noun phrase introduced in a preceding sentence (as in "A man walked in. He sat down."). DRT introduces an intermediate level of representation -- *discourse representation structures* (DRSs) -- that track the referents introduced in a discourse and the conditions predicated of them. The meaning of a sentence is not its truth conditions in isolation but its contribution to an evolving DRS. This shift from static truth conditions to dynamic information update was revolutionary: it reconceived meaning not as a property of individual sentences but as a function from input contexts (information states) to output contexts.

**Dynamic semantics**, as the broader program came to be known, generalizes DRT's insight: the meaning of a sentence is its *context-change potential* -- the function by which it transforms the information state of a rational interpreter. On this view, to know the meaning of a sentence is to know "the change it brings about in the information state of anyone who accepts the news conveyed by it" (Heim 1982). Dynamic semantics has been extended to handle presupposition, modal subordination, plural anaphora, and other phenomena. From the perspective of the present dissertation, the most striking feature of dynamic semantics is its convergence with programming language semantics: both treat meaning as state transformation. A dynamically interpreted sentence is, in a precise sense, a program -- it takes an input state and produces an output state.

**Situation semantics**, developed by Jon Barwise and John Perry in the early 1980s, offers yet another departure from classical possible-worlds semantics. Where possible worlds are complete and maximal -- specifying the truth value of every proposition -- situations are *partial*: they represent limited aspects of the world. The basic objects of the theory are *infons* -- units of information consisting of a relation, arguments, and a polarity (positive or negative). Meaning is a relation between situation types, constrained by the information-carrying regularities of the world. The emphasis on partiality connects situation semantics to domain theory in computer science, where partial information is the fundamental concept: a partially computed result is an element of a domain that approximates, but does not fully determine, the final result. This parallel between partial situations and partial computations is one of the less-explored but potentially significant bridges between natural language and programming language semantics.

The truth-conditional tradition, associated with Donald Davidson's programme (1967) of constructing Tarski-style truth theories for natural language, remains influential. Davidson's proposal was to treat a theory of truth for a language as a theory of meaning: to know the meaning of a sentence is to know its truth conditions. The Davidsonian schema -- "'Snow is white' is true if and only if snow is white" -- transfers Tarski's Convention T from formal languages to natural language, making the logical notion of truth-in-a-model the foundation of linguistic meaning. Truth-conditional semantics has been criticized by proponents of dynamic, inferential, and use-based theories, but it remains the default framework in much formal semantics and the implicit foundation of Montague grammar.

## 3. The Eight Bridges

This section constitutes the core of the dissertation. For each of the eight identified bridges, we provide: (a) a characterization of the bridge's manifestation in each tradition; (b) a formal analysis of its structural strength; and (c) an assessment of the conditions under which the bridge holds and fails. Structural strength is assessed on a four-point scale:

- **Isomorphism**: a structure-preserving bijection; the cross-domain parallel is exact and invertible.
- **Homomorphism**: a structure-preserving map; the parallel is exact in one direction but may lose information.
- **Analogy**: a suggestive structural parallel that can be made precise in some formulations but not in full generality.
- **Metaphor**: a terminological or conceptual similarity without substantive structural content.

### 3.1 Bridge 1: Compositionality (Frege's Principle)

**Statement of the principle.** The principle of compositionality, attributed to Frege (though its precise formulation postdates him), states that the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its constituent expressions and the rules used to combine them. In algebraic terms, compositionality asserts the existence of a *homomorphism* from a syntactic algebra (whose elements are expressions and whose operations are syntactic combination rules) to a semantic algebra (whose elements are meanings and whose operations are semantic combination functions).

**In logical semantics.** Tarski's recursive definition of satisfaction is the paradigmatic compositional semantics. The satisfaction of a conjunction A and B is defined in terms of the satisfaction of A and the satisfaction of B; the satisfaction of an existential quantification (exists x)(Fx) is defined in terms of the satisfaction of Fx under suitable variable assignments. The recursion follows the syntactic structure of formulae, and the semantic values (truth values in a structure under an assignment) are computed bottom-up from the semantic values of subformulae. Compositionality is not merely a feature of Tarskian semantics; it is its constitutive principle. Without it, the recursive definition cannot proceed, and the semantic theory collapses.

**In programming language semantics.** Denotational semantics is explicitly and self-consciously compositional. Scott and Strachey's foundational design decision was that the denotation of a compound program expression must be defined solely in terms of the denotations of its subexpressions. The denotation of a sequential composition (C_1 ; C_2) is the functional composition of the denotations of C_1 and C_2; the denotation of a conditional (if B then C_1 else C_2) is defined in terms of the denotation of B (a boolean-valued function) and the denotations of C_1 and C_2. Compositionality in this setting is not merely a methodological preference; it is the property that distinguishes denotational from operational semantics, which may refer to intermediate states of computation that are not syntactic subexpressions.

**In natural language semantics.** Montague grammar elevates compositionality to its founding axiom. Each syntactic rule of the grammar is paired with a corresponding semantic rule, and the meaning of a complex expression is computed by applying the semantic rule to the meanings of the expressions combined by the syntactic rule. The pairing is so tight that it has been formalized as a *homomorphism* from the syntactic algebra (the free algebra generated by the grammar's syntactic operations) to the semantic algebra (the type-theoretic domain of meanings). Janssen (1983, 1997) and others have shown that Montague's framework can be precisely characterized as an algebraic homomorphism, making compositionality a rigorous mathematical property rather than an informal desideratum.

**Structural strength: Isomorphism (for the algebraic formulation).** In all three traditions, compositionality can be formalized as a homomorphism from a syntactic algebra to a semantic algebra. Moreover, the algebraic formulation is identical across traditions: it is the *same mathematical concept* -- a structure-preserving map between algebras -- instantiated in three different domains. The bridge is not merely analogical; it is a case of shared mathematical identity. The syntactic and semantic algebras differ across traditions (formulae versus programs versus English sentences; truth values versus denotations versus intensions), but the *relationship* between syntax and semantics -- the homomorphism -- is the same in each case.

**Limitations.** Compositionality is challenged in all three traditions. In natural language, idioms ("kick the bucket"), context-dependent expressions (indexicals, demonstratives), and conversational implicature resist straightforward compositional treatment. In programming language semantics, non-local constructs (exceptions, continuations, reflection) and concurrency complicate compositional analysis. In logic, self-referential constructions and contextual operators (the provability predicate, the truth predicate) strain compositionality. The responses to these challenges -- covert variables, monadic effects, substructural logics -- are themselves instructively parallel across traditions, a point to which we return in Section 5.

### 3.2 Bridge 2: Lambda Calculus

**Statement of the bridge.** The lambda calculus, introduced by Alonzo Church in the 1930s as a foundation for mathematical logic, is the formal system for expressing computation through function abstraction (lambda-binding a variable in a term to create a function) and application (applying a function to an argument). It is, simultaneously and non-metaphorically, the foundation of functional programming, the notation of Montague grammar, and a proof term calculus for intuitionistic logic.

**In computer science.** The lambda calculus is the theoretical foundation of functional programming languages. LISP (1958), the first functional programming language, was directly inspired by Church's lambda calculus; its core mechanism of function definition and application is a concrete implementation of lambda abstraction and beta-reduction. Modern functional languages -- Haskell, ML, OCaml, and the functional cores of multi-paradigm languages such as Scala and Rust -- are, in their theoretical essence, typed lambda calculi with various extensions (algebraic data types, pattern matching, type classes, effects). The simply typed lambda calculus corresponds to the core of these languages; System F (the polymorphic lambda calculus) corresponds to languages with parametric polymorphism; and the calculus of constructions (Coquand and Huet 1988) corresponds to dependently typed languages and proof assistants such as Coq and Lean.

**In natural language semantics.** Montague's formal apparatus for natural language semantics uses lambda calculus as its compositional glue. The meaning of a transitive verb such as "loves" is represented as a lambda term: lambda y . lambda x . love(x, y), a function that takes two entity arguments and returns a truth value. Semantic composition proceeds by lambda application: combining the verb meaning with a noun phrase meaning by beta-reduction. The entire apparatus of Montague grammar -- quantifier raising, scope ambiguity resolution, intensionalization -- is encoded in lambda-calculus operations. When a formal semanticist writes the meaning of "every man walks" as (every')(man')(walk'), the primes denote lambda-calculus operations on typed terms. The notation is not merely reminiscent of functional programming; it *is* functional programming, applied to linguistic rather than computational data.

**In logic.** In proof theory, lambda terms serve as *proof terms* for intuitionistic natural deduction. Under the Curry-Howard correspondence (Section 3.3), a proof of an implication A -> B is a function (a lambda term) that transforms proofs of A into proofs of B. The structure of a proof -- its introduction and elimination steps, its dependencies, its normalization behaviour -- is faithfully recorded by the structure of the corresponding lambda term. Beta-reduction of lambda terms corresponds to proof simplification (cut elimination); the Church-Rosser property (confluence) of beta-reduction corresponds to the uniqueness of normal forms for proofs.

**Structural strength: Isomorphism.** The lambda calculus is not used *analogously* across the three traditions; it is *the same formal system* deployed in three contexts. The lambda terms that appear in a Montague-grammar derivation of "every man walks" are syntactically and semantically identical to the lambda terms that define a higher-order function in Haskell and the lambda terms that serve as proof terms for a natural-deduction proof of a higher-order formula. The types are drawn from the same type system (the simple type theory of Church 1940, or extensions thereof); the reduction rules are the same (beta-reduction, eta-conversion); and the algebraic laws governing the calculus (Church-Rosser, strong normalization for typed systems) apply uniformly across all three uses. This is the strongest possible form of bridge: not structural similarity, but syntactic and semantic identity.

**Limitations.** The untyped lambda calculus, while Turing-equivalent and therefore maximally expressive for computation, does not directly correspond to standard logical systems (it is inconsistent as a logic, since every type is inhabited). The typed lambda calculi used in practice differ in expressiveness: the simply typed lambda calculus is not Turing-complete; System F and the calculus of constructions extend expressiveness at the cost of decidability of type checking (in some variants). In natural language semantics, the lambda calculus is used primarily as a notational device for compositional meaning assembly; the computational properties of the calculus (evaluation strategies, complexity of normalization) are typically of secondary interest to linguists. Nevertheless, the formal identity of the system across all three uses remains undeniable.

### 3.3 Bridge 3: The Curry-Howard-Lambek Correspondence

**Statement of the bridge.** The Curry-Howard correspondence, discovered incrementally by Haskell Curry (1934), William Alvin Howard (1969), and others, establishes a direct, formally precise relationship between proofs and programs. Under this correspondence:

- Propositions correspond to types.
- Proofs correspond to programs (lambda terms).
- Proof simplification (cut elimination, normalization) corresponds to computation (beta-reduction).

Joachim Lambek (1968, 1972) extended the correspondence to include categories:

- Types correspond to objects in a category.
- Programs (proofs) correspond to morphisms.
- Composition of programs (proofs) corresponds to composition of morphisms.

The resulting three-way correspondence -- the *Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence* -- is arguably the deepest known structural bridge between logic, computation, and mathematics.

**The two-way correspondence: proofs and programs.** In the simply typed lambda calculus, the types of lambda terms can be read as propositions of intuitionistic propositional logic. The function type A -> B corresponds to the implication "if A then B"; the product type A x B corresponds to conjunction "A and B"; the sum type A + B corresponds to disjunction "A or B". Under this reading, a well-typed lambda term of type A -> B is a *proof* that A implies B: it is a constructive witness, a function that transforms any evidence for A into evidence for B. Type checking -- the algorithmic verification that a program has a given type -- corresponds to proof checking -- the verification that a purported proof actually proves its conclusion. Type inference -- the algorithmic determination of a program's type -- corresponds to proof search. The correspondence extends to richer type systems: dependent types correspond to first-order and higher-order predicate logic; linear types correspond to linear logic (Girard 1987); and homotopy type theory (HoTT) extends the correspondence into the realm of homotopy theory and higher category theory.

**The three-way correspondence: proofs, programs, and categories.** Lambek's contribution was to observe that the correspondence between proofs and programs is mirrored by a correspondence with morphisms in a category. A cartesian closed category (CCC) -- a category with finite products and exponential objects -- provides a categorical semantics for the simply typed lambda calculus. The objects of the category are types (propositions); the morphisms are programs (proofs); composition of morphisms is composition of programs (proof concatenation). The internal language of a CCC is the simply typed lambda calculus, and conversely, every simply typed lambda calculus gives rise to a CCC. The correspondence is functorial: translations between type systems correspond to functors between categories.

**The question of a fourth corner: natural language.** The Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence unifies logic, computation, and category theory but does not directly include natural language semantics. However, several lines of evidence suggest that the correspondence can be extended to encompass natural language. First, Montague grammar uses the typed lambda calculus as its semantic formalism; since the typed lambda calculus is one vertex of the Curry-Howard triangle, Montague grammar is already implicitly connected to the correspondence. Second, categorial grammar -- particularly the Lambek calculus (Lambek 1958) and combinatory categorial grammar (Steedman 2000) -- treats syntactic categories as types and syntactic combination as type-driven composition, making the grammar itself a fragment of a typed logical system. In the Lambek calculus, the syntactic type A/B ("an expression that combines with a B on its right to form an A") is formally a linear implication, and a grammatical derivation is a proof in a substructural logic. Third, the type-logical tradition in formal linguistics (Moortgat 1997; Morrill 2011) explicitly develops the Curry-Howard correspondence for natural language, treating syntactic derivations as proofs and semantic representations as proof terms.

The extension to a full four-way correspondence -- proofs = programs = morphisms = utterance-meanings -- remains, however, incomplete. The difficulties include: the treatment of lexical ambiguity (a word can have multiple types, unlike a well-defined term in a typed calculus); the role of context (natural language meaning depends on extra-linguistic context in ways that have no direct analogue in pure logic or typed lambda calculus); and the treatment of pragmatic phenomena (implicature, presupposition) that fall outside the scope of purely compositional, type-driven meaning assignment.

**Structural strength: Isomorphism (for the three-way correspondence); homomorphism (for the extension to natural language).** The three-way Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence is an isomorphism in the strongest sense: there are precise, invertible translations between proofs, programs, and categorical morphisms, and these translations preserve the relevant structural properties (composition, identity, typing). The extension to natural language is weaker: the type-logical tradition establishes a homomorphism from syntactic derivations to proof terms (and thereby to programs and morphisms), but this homomorphism is not an isomorphism because natural language exhibits phenomena (ambiguity, context-dependence, non-compositionality) that have no counterpart in the purely logical or computational vertices of the correspondence.

### 3.4 Bridge 4: Type Theory

**Statement of the bridge.** Type theory -- the formal study of type systems -- provides classification of meanings in all three traditions. Types sort entities into kinds, constrain the well-formedness of expressions, and ensure that combinations of meanings are semantically coherent. The role of types is structurally parallel across traditions, though the details and motivations differ.

**In logic.** Types in logic originate with Bertrand Russell's theory of types (1908), designed to avoid the paradoxes of naive set theory by stratifying the universe of discourse into hierarchical levels. In modern logic, types distinguish sorts of entities: individuals, propositions, truth values, sets, functions. Church's simple type theory (1940) provides the foundation for higher-order logic, in which variables can range over functions and predicates of arbitrary finite type. Martin-Lof's intuitionistic type theory (1972, 1984) goes further, introducing *dependent types* -- types that depend on values -- and identifying propositions with types (the propositions-as-types interpretation). In Martin-Lof's system, a proposition is a type, and a proof of the proposition is an element of that type. This identification is the type-theoretic expression of the Curry-Howard correspondence, and it has been enormously influential in both logic and computer science.

**In programming languages.** Types in programming languages serve a dual role: they classify data (integers, strings, booleans, records, functions) and they constrain programs (a well-typed program cannot apply an integer as if it were a function, or dereference a null pointer in a language with non-nullable types). Type systems range from the simple (the type systems of C and Java, which classify data but offer limited safety guarantees) to the sophisticated (the type systems of Haskell, Agda, Idris, and Lean, which encode logical properties and support type-driven program verification). The Milner-Hindley type system, used in ML and Haskell, provides *type inference*: the compiler can determine the types of all expressions without explicit annotations, a property that corresponds (via Curry-Howard) to automatic proof search for a fragment of intuitionistic logic.

**In natural language semantics.** In Montague grammar, types classify semantic objects: entities have type *e*, truth values have type *t*, and functions are typed by their domains and codomains. An intransitive verb has type *e* -> *t* (a function from entities to truth values -- a predicate); a transitive verb has type *e* -> (*e* -> *t*) (a curried two-place predicate); a quantifier phrase has type (*e* -> *t*) -> *t* (a function from predicates to truth values -- a generalized quantifier). The type system constrains semantic composition: only type-compatible expressions can combine, and the rules of combination (function application, primarily) are type-driven. Selectional restrictions -- the fact that "the rock slept" is semantically anomalous while "the man slept" is not -- can be modeled as type constraints, and more refined type systems (with subtypes, polymorphism, or dependent types) can capture increasingly fine-grained semantic distinctions.

**Structural strength: Homomorphism.** The role of types is structurally parallel across all three traditions: in each case, types classify meanings, constrain well-formedness, and drive composition. However, the parallel is a homomorphism rather than an isomorphism, because the type systems used in the three traditions differ in important ways. Logical type systems are typically simple (Church's simple types) or dependent (Martin-Lof); programming language type systems include features (polymorphism, type classes, linear types, effect types) that have no direct counterpart in standard logical or linguistic type systems; and linguistic type systems are relatively impoverished compared to their logical and computational counterparts, typically limited to simple functional types over a small set of base types. Moreover, the *motivations* for typing differ: in logic, types prevent paradox; in programming, types prevent runtime errors; in linguistics, types prevent semantic anomaly. These different motivations lead to different design decisions -- for example, subtyping is important in object-oriented programming and has analogues in linguistics (the noun-phrase hierarchy) but is less central to standard logical type theory.

### 3.5 Bridge 5: Model Theory

**Statement of the bridge.** In all three traditions, a central approach to semantics proceeds by defining an *interpretation*: a mapping from syntactic expressions to elements of a mathematical structure (a model). The model provides the "world" in which expressions receive their meanings, and truth (or correctness, or denotation) is defined relative to this model.

**In logical semantics.** Tarski's model-theoretic semantics defines truth relative to a structure M = (D, I), where D is a domain of individuals and I is an interpretation function that assigns extensions to non-logical symbols (individuals to constants, subsets of D to predicates, functions on D to function symbols). A formula is true in M under an assignment just in case the assignment satisfies the formula according to the recursive definition of satisfaction. Model-theoretic semantics provides the standard framework for classical logic, and model theory as a mathematical discipline studies the relationship between theories and their models at a high level of abstraction (categoricity, stability, classification of structures).

**In programming language semantics.** Denotational semantics defines the meaning of a program by mapping it to an element of a mathematical domain -- a *denotation*. The collection of domains, together with the interpretation function that maps syntactic constructs to elements of these domains, constitutes a model of the programming language. Scott's domain-theoretic models are, in a precise sense, models in the model-theoretic sense: they are mathematical structures that interpret a formal language (the programming language) by assigning mathematical objects to its expressions. The adequacy and full abstraction properties that relate denotational to operational semantics are analogues of soundness and completeness in logical model theory.

**In natural language semantics.** Montague's models are Kripke structures: they consist of a set of possible worlds, a set of individuals (possibly varying across worlds), and an interpretation function that assigns intensions (functions from worlds to extensions) to lexical items. The intensional model M = (W, D, I) interprets natural language expressions by assigning to each expression an intension of the appropriate type. Truth is relativized to a world: a sentence is true at a world *w* in model M just in case its intension, applied to *w*, yields the truth value True. This is a direct extension of Tarskian model-theoretic semantics to an intensional setting, and the formal parallels between Tarski models for first-order logic and Montague models for natural language are exact for the relevant fragments.

**Structural strength: Isomorphism (for classical, compositional fragments).** For the classical, compositional fragments of each tradition -- first-order logic, simply typed functional languages, and Montague-style extensional semantics -- the model-theoretic approach is structurally identical. In each case, meaning is a mapping from syntax to elements of a mathematical structure; truth (or denotation) is defined relative to this structure; and the mapping is compositional. The mathematical apparatus is the same: structures, interpretation functions, satisfaction relations. The bridge is an isomorphism for these fragments.

**Limitations.** The model-theoretic approach faces challenges in all three traditions that strain the bridge. In logic, the model theory of second-order logic is notoriously difficult (Henkin semantics versus full semantics; the failure of completeness for full second-order logic). In programming language semantics, the problem of full abstraction -- the existence of models that distinguish exactly the operationally distinguishable programs -- resisted solution for decades and required the development of game semantics. In natural language semantics, the model-theoretic approach struggles with context-dependence, discourse dynamics, and the relationship between intensional and extensional meaning. These challenges motivate the dynamic and game-theoretic alternatives discussed in Sections 3.6 and 3.7.

### 3.6 Bridge 6: Game Semantics

**Statement of the bridge.** Game semantics interprets meaning -- truth, validity, denotation -- in terms of games between players. A formula is valid if one player has a winning strategy; a program denotes a strategy for interacting with its environment; a sentence means what it contributes to a conversational game. Game semantics is remarkable for having been *independently* and *productively* developed in all three traditions, making it perhaps the most striking case of convergent intellectual evolution among the eight bridges.

**In logical semantics.** Paul Lorenzen and Kuno Lorenz developed *dialogical logic* in the 1950s and 1960s, in which the validity of a formula is defined in terms of a dialogue game between a Proponent (who asserts the formula) and an Opponent (who challenges it). Each logical connective determines a game rule: an assertion of a conjunction can be challenged by requesting either conjunct; an assertion of a disjunction must be defended by defending one disjunct; an assertion of a universal quantification can be challenged by the Opponent choosing a witness. A formula is valid if the Proponent has a winning strategy -- a way of responding to every possible sequence of challenges. Independently, Jaakko Hintikka developed *game-theoretical semantics* (GTS) for first-order logic, in which the truth of a formula in a model is defined by a game: existential quantifiers correspond to moves by the Verifier (who chooses witnesses), and universal quantifiers correspond to moves by the Falsifier. A formula is true if the Verifier has a winning strategy.

**In natural language semantics.** Hintikka extended GTS to natural language, providing game-theoretic interpretations of quantifiers, anaphora, and information-dependence phenomena. On this view, the meaning of "every man loves some woman" depends on the order of play: if the universal player (the Falsifier) moves first (choosing a man), and the existential player (the Verifier) moves second (choosing a woman), then the resulting interpretation allows the woman to depend on the man -- a *dependent reading* that is naturally captured by the game-theoretic framework. GTS provides elegant analyses of *branching quantifiers*, *informational independence*, and other phenomena that resist treatment in standard first-order logic. Clark's (1996) game-theoretic models of conversational interaction extend the game-theoretic perspective to pragmatics, treating dialogue as a coordination game between speakers.

**In programming language semantics.** In the 1990s, Samson Abramsky, Radha Jagadeesan, Pasquale Malacaria, Martin Hyland, and C.-H. Luke Ong developed game semantics for programming languages, most notably providing the first *fully abstract* model for the language PCF (Plotkin's computable functionals). In this framework, the meaning of a program is a *strategy* -- a specification of how the program responds to each possible interaction with its environment (its "opponent"). The environment makes moves (providing inputs, requesting outputs), and the program responds (computing outputs, requesting inputs). The resulting game-theoretic model distinguishes exactly those programs that are operationally distinguishable -- it is fully abstract -- solving a problem that had resisted denotational methods for two decades. Game semantics has since been extended to handle state, concurrency, probability, and other computational effects.

**Structural strength: Homomorphism.** The game-theoretic perspective operates in all three traditions, but the specific game structures differ. In logic, the games are typically two-player games of perfect information on finite game trees; in programming language semantics, the games are typically sequential, well-bracketed games with rules governing the order of moves; in natural language semantics, the games are less formalized and often serve as interpretive heuristics rather than fully rigorous mathematical structures. The three uses share the fundamental idea of *meaning as interaction* -- the replacement of static truth-assignment with dynamic engagement between agents -- and this shared idea can be formalized as a homomorphism between the three game-theoretic frameworks. The homomorphism is not an isomorphism because the game structures and the rules governing play differ across traditions. Nevertheless, the independent convergence of three traditions on a game-theoretic conception of meaning is powerful evidence of deep structural kinship.

### 3.7 Bridge 7: Dynamic Semantics

**Statement of the bridge.** Dynamic semantics treats meaning as *context-change potential* -- the capacity of an expression to transform an information state. In natural language semantics, this view originates with Heim (1982) and Kamp (1981); in programming language semantics, it is the default view embodied in both operational and denotational approaches; in logic, it connects to the dynamic turn in predicate logic (Groenendijk and Stokhof 1991) and to the tradition of proof-theoretic semantics.

**In natural language semantics.** Dynamic semantics revolutionized formal semantics by replacing the question "Under what conditions is this sentence true?" with the question "How does this sentence change the information state of the hearer?" The meaning of a sentence is not a proposition (a set of possible worlds) but a *context-change potential* (a function from information states to information states). In DRT, the information state is a discourse representation structure; in dynamic predicate logic (DPL), it is a set of variable assignments; in update semantics (Veltman 1996), it is a set of possible worlds. The dynamic perspective provides natural accounts of anaphora ("A man walked in. He sat down" -- the first sentence introduces a discourse referent, and the second sentence updates the context by adding a predicate on that referent), presupposition (a presupposition is a precondition on the input context), and conversational dynamics (each utterance transforms the common ground).

**In programming language semantics.** Programs are, quintessentially, state transformers. In denotational semantics, the meaning of an imperative program is a function from states to states (where a state assigns values to variables). In operational semantics, a program transforms one configuration into another. The parallel with dynamic natural language semantics is not superficial: both treat meaning as a function from input states to output states, and the mathematical formalism is the same (functions on structured state spaces). Moreover, DRT-style semantic parsers -- systems that parse natural language into discourse representation structures -- literally *compile* natural language into state-transforming programs, making the bridge between dynamic semantics and programming language semantics operational as well as theoretical.

**In logic.** Dynamic predicate logic (Groenendijk and Stokhof 1991) reinterprets first-order logic dynamically: the meaning of a formula is not its truth conditions but its *potential to change variable assignments*. An existential quantifier introduces a new variable assignment; a conjunction passes the output of its first conjunct as input to its second. This dynamic reinterpretation of logic bridges the logical and linguistic traditions directly: it shows that the dynamic semantics of natural language can be obtained by a systematic reinterpretation of the standard logical apparatus. In proof-theoretic semantics, the connection is less direct but suggestive: Brandom's inferentialism treats the meaning of an assertion as its role in updating the "score" of a conversation -- the commitments and entitlements of the participants -- which is a dynamic, state-transforming conception of meaning.

**Structural strength: Analogy tending toward homomorphism (under active formalization).** The parallel between dynamic natural language semantics and programming language semantics is more than an analogy -- both use the same mathematical framework (functions on state spaces) -- but less than a full isomorphism. The state spaces differ (discourse contexts versus program stores), the operations differ (anaphora resolution versus variable assignment), and the properties of interest differ (coherence of discourse versus correctness of computation). Recent work in computational semantics -- particularly the development of semantic parsers that translate natural language into executable code -- is actively strengthening the bridge from analogy toward homomorphism. The formalization of the dynamic semantics-programming language parallel remains an area of active research and a promising avenue for unification.

### 3.8 Bridge 8: Proof-Theoretic Semantics

**Statement of the bridge.** Proof-theoretic semantics locates the meaning of an expression not in its truth conditions or denotation but in its *inferential role* -- the patterns of inference in which it participates. In logic, this approach is associated with Gentzen, Dummett, and Prawitz; in computer science, it connects to the Curry-Howard correspondence and to the view of types as specifications; in philosophy and linguistics, it is associated with Robert Brandom's inferentialism.

**In logic.** Proof-theoretic semantics, as developed by Dummett (1991) and Prawitz (1965, 2006), holds that the meaning of a logical connective is determined by its introduction and elimination rules in natural deduction. The introduction rules for a connective specify the conditions under which an assertion involving that connective can be established; the elimination rules specify the consequences that can be drawn from such an assertion. Harmony between introduction and elimination rules -- the property that the elimination rules are exactly justified by the introduction rules, and vice versa -- is the proof-theoretic analogue of soundness and completeness. A connective is meaningful (well-defined) if and only if its rules are harmonious. This view provides an alternative to model-theoretic semantics: meaning is not correspondence to an external reality (a model) but role within a system of inference.

**In programming language semantics.** Via the Curry-Howard correspondence, the proof-theoretic approach to meaning in logic translates directly into a computational approach to meaning in programming. Introduction rules correspond to constructors (ways of building values of a type); elimination rules correspond to destructors or pattern-matching operations (ways of using values of a type). Harmony between introduction and elimination rules corresponds to the property that every value can be constructed and subsequently decomposed without loss of information -- a property that, in programming language theory, corresponds to the existence of canonical forms and the soundness of pattern matching. The Martin-Lof type theory, which serves as the foundation of proof assistants such as Coq, Agda, and Lean, makes the proof-theoretic approach to meaning its organizing principle: every type is defined by its introduction and elimination rules, and every well-typed program is a proof.

**In natural language semantics and philosophy.** Brandom's inferentialism (1994, 2000) extends the proof-theoretic approach to natural language: the meaning of a word or sentence is not its reference or truth conditions but the inferences it licenses and the inferences that license it. To understand the meaning of "red" is to know that "This is red" entails "This is coloured," is entailed by "This is scarlet," and is incompatible with "This is green." Meaning is *use in inference*, not correspondence to reality. While Brandom's programme is philosophical rather than formally mathematical, it has deep structural affinities with proof-theoretic semantics in logic and with the type-theoretic approach in computer science: in all three cases, meaning is constituted by inferential role rather than by denotation. The connection to computational linguistics is less developed but suggestive: distributional semantics (Section 5.3), which derives word meanings from patterns of co-occurrence, can be seen as an empirical approximation of inferential role.

**Structural strength: Homomorphism.** The proof-theoretic approach to meaning operates in all three traditions, and the structural parallels -- introduction/elimination rules in logic, constructors/destructors in programming, inferential articulation in natural language -- are genuine and formally precise (in the logic and programming cases) or philosophically rigorous (in the natural language case). The bridge is a homomorphism rather than an isomorphism because the natural language version (Brandom's inferentialism) is less formalized than its logical and computational counterparts and because the inferential networks of natural language are vastly more complex and context-sensitive than the proof systems of logic or the type systems of programming languages.

## 4. Category Theory as Unifying Metalanguage

### 4.1 The Categorical Perspective on Each Bridge

Category theory, the "mathematics of mathematics," provides a language in which the structures of all eight bridges can be described uniformly. A category consists of objects and morphisms (arrows between objects), satisfying associativity and identity laws. Functors map categories to categories, preserving structure; natural transformations map functors to functors. This abstract vocabulary turns out to be precisely suited to describing the relationship between syntax and semantics in all three traditions.

**Compositionality as functor.** In categorical terms, a compositional semantics is a *functor* from a syntactic category (whose objects are types and whose morphisms are syntactic derivations) to a semantic category (whose objects are domains of meaning and whose morphisms are meaning-preserving transformations). The requirement that the functor preserve composition and identity is the categorical expression of compositionality: the meaning of a compound expression (a composite morphism) is the composite of the meanings of its parts.

**Lambda calculus as internal language.** Every cartesian closed category (CCC) has an internal language that is a typed lambda calculus, and conversely, every typed lambda calculus gives rise to a CCC. This adjunction between syntax (lambda calculus) and semantics (CCCs) is the categorical expression of the lambda-calculus bridge: the lambda calculus is not merely a notation used in three traditions but the internal language of a mathematical structure (the CCC) that appears in all three.

**The Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence as adjunction.** The three-way correspondence between proofs, programs, and morphisms is, categorically, an equivalence of categories: the category of proofs in intuitionistic logic, the category of typed lambda terms, and the free CCC generated by the type signature are equivalent. Translations between these presentations are functors; the coherence of these translations is captured by natural transformations.

**Type theory as fibration.** In categorical terms, type theory corresponds to the theory of *fibrations* over a base category. A dependent type theory gives rise to a *display map category* or a *comprehension category*; the types are the fibres, and the terms are sections. This fibred perspective unifies the treatment of types across logic (where types are propositions), programming (where types are data classifications), and natural language (where types are semantic sorts).

**Model theory as functor category.** A model of a theory, in the categorical perspective, is a structure-preserving functor from the syntactic category of the theory to a semantic category (typically the category of sets, **Set**). Theories are categories; models are functors; elementary embeddings and homomorphisms between models are natural transformations. This reformulation applies uniformly to models in logic (Tarski structures), models in programming language semantics (Scott domains), and models in natural language semantics (Montague models).

**Game semantics as dialogue category.** Hyland's work on game semantics shows that games and strategies form a category -- the *game category* -- in which the objects are games and the morphisms are strategies. Composition of strategies corresponds to sequential interaction; the identity strategy corresponds to "copycat" behaviour. The categorical structure of games provides a unified framework for the logical, computational, and (potentially) linguistic uses of game semantics.

### 4.2 Genuine Unification or Redescription?

The categorical perspective raises a fundamental question: does category theory provide *genuine unification* of the three semantic traditions, or does it merely *redescribe* each tradition in a more abstract vocabulary? The concern is that category theory, by virtue of its extreme generality, can describe virtually any mathematical structure, and therefore the fact that it can describe all three semantic traditions may reflect the generality of category theory rather than the unity of semantics.

Several considerations suggest that the categorical unification is more than redescription. First, the categorical reformulations reveal *structural connections* that are invisible in the original formalisms. The equivalence of CCCs and typed lambda calculi, for example, is a non-trivial mathematical theorem that establishes a precise, invertible relationship between two independently motivated mathematical structures. This is not mere redescription; it is the discovery of a hidden structural identity. Second, the categorical framework generates *new results* by transferring constructions across domains. The development of game semantics for PCF, for example, was directly informed by the categorical analysis of linear logic and the identification of game categories as a model of multiplicative linear logic. Third, the categorical perspective identifies *limits of unification* as well as instances of it: the failure of certain categorical constructions to apply in certain domains (e.g., the difficulty of giving a categorical semantics for non-deterministic or concurrent computation) marks the precise boundaries of cross-domain analogy.

Nevertheless, the concern about vacuous generality has force. Not every categorical reformulation is illuminating; some are merely notational changes that add complexity without adding insight. The criterion for genuine unification, we suggest, is *productivity*: a categorical unification is genuine if it generates new theorems, new constructions, or new insights that were not available in the original formalisms. By this criterion, the categorical unification of logic and computation (via CCCs and the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence) is genuine; the categorical reformulation of natural language semantics is promising but less fully developed.

### 4.3 Toposes, Presheaf Semantics, and the Limits of Categorical Unification

A *topos* is a category that behaves like a generalized universe of sets, supporting internal logical reasoning. The category of sets is a topos; presheaf categories (functors from a small category to **Set**) are toposes; sheaf categories over a topological space are toposes. Every topos has an *internal logic* that is intuitionistic (constructive) and higher-order, and every topos provides a model of intuitionistic higher-order logic.

Topos theory has been proposed as the mathematical framework for a unified semantics. The idea is attractive: a topos provides a universe in which one can interpret logical formulae, define computational domains, and model linguistic structures, all within a single mathematical framework. Presheaf semantics, in particular, has been applied to programming language semantics (to model local state, name generation, and parametricity) and to natural language semantics (to model context-dependence and information flow). The sheaf condition -- the requirement that local information can be coherently glued together into global information -- has suggestive parallels with compositionality and with the coherence conditions on discourse representation structures.

However, topos-theoretic unification faces significant limitations. The internal logic of a topos is intuitionistic, not classical; this is appropriate for constructive mathematics and programming (where computability is paramount) but may be too restrictive for classical model theory and for truth-conditional natural language semantics, which typically assumes classical logic. Moreover, the topos-theoretic framework does not directly accommodate computational effects (state, input/output, exceptions, concurrency) that are central to modern programming language semantics; monads and other categorical devices are needed to model effects, and the integration of effects with topos-theoretic semantics is an area of active research. Finally, the application of topos theory to natural language semantics remains largely programmatic: while presheaf models have been used for specific linguistic phenomena, a comprehensive topos-theoretic natural language semantics does not yet exist.

### 4.4 The Relationship to the Curry-Howard-Lambek Triangle

The Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence provides the tightest known categorical unification of logic and computation. The correspondence is mediated by CCCs: the internal language of a CCC is the simply typed lambda calculus, which is simultaneously a proof system for intuitionistic propositional logic. The question is whether this tight unification can be extended to encompass natural language.

The type-logical tradition in formal linguistics (Lambek 1958; Moortgat 1997; Morrill 2011; Steedman 2000) provides the most promising avenue. In the Lambek calculus, syntactic types are formulae of a non-commutative logic (a substructural logic that lacks the structural rules of exchange, and in some variants, weakening and contraction). Syntactic derivations are proofs in this logic; semantic interpretations are proof terms (lambda terms) extracted from derivations via the Curry-Howard correspondence. The result is a system in which grammaticality is provability, meaning is proof term, and semantic composition is proof normalization -- a direct linguistic instantiation of the Curry-Howard-Lambek triangle.

Combinatory categorial grammar (CCG), developed by Mark Steedman, offers a linguistically richer variant. CCG uses a small set of combinatory rules (function application, composition, type-raising) that correspond to combinators in combinatory logic (the B, T, and S combinators). These combinators are, by Curry's original observation, the combinatory counterparts of lambda terms; and by the Curry-Howard correspondence, they correspond to proof steps in a logic. CCG thus provides a grammar formalism in which syntactic derivation, semantic composition, and logical proof are identified -- a concrete realization of the four-way correspondence sketched in Section 3.3.

The limitations of this approach are real. Natural language is not fully captured by the Lambek calculus or CCG; phenomena such as long-distance dependencies, ellipsis, and non-constituent coordination require extensions to the basic framework. Moreover, the logics underlying these frameworks are substructural (lacking some of the classical structural rules), which limits the directness of the correspondence with classical logic and with mainstream programming language type systems. Nevertheless, the type-logical tradition represents the most sustained and mathematically rigorous attempt to extend the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence to natural language, and its partial success is strong evidence that the correspondence is not limited to the logic-computation axis.

## 5. Where Unification Breaks Down

The preceding sections have argued that the three traditions of semantics are connected by genuine structural bridges, not merely by homonymous terminology. This section examines the points at which the bridges fail -- the phenomena and features that resist cross-domain unification. These breakdowns are instructive: they reveal the boundary conditions of a unified semantics and identify the areas where further theoretical work is needed.

### 5.1 Pragmatics and Context-Dependence

The most pervasive challenge to a unified semantics is *pragmatics* -- the aspects of meaning that depend on the context of use rather than the linguistic form of an expression. Natural language meaning is deeply context-dependent: indexicals ("I," "here," "now"), demonstratives ("this," "that"), and context-sensitive expressions ("local," "enemy," "ready") receive their semantic values only relative to a context of utterance. Conversational implicature (Grice 1975) -- the information conveyed by an utterance beyond its literal content -- depends on shared assumptions about cooperative communication. Presupposition, speech acts (Austin 1962; Searle 1969), and discourse structure all contribute to the meaning of an utterance in ways that go beyond truth-conditional content.

In logic, pragmatic phenomena have no direct counterpart. Formal languages are designed to eliminate context-dependence: the meaning of a formula is determined by its form and the interpretation function, without reference to who is asserting it, when, or why. While there are logics that model aspects of pragmatics -- epistemic logic for knowledge and belief, deontic logic for obligation, dynamic logic for information update -- these logics formalize pragmatic *notions* without capturing the full complexity of pragmatic *reasoning* as it occurs in natural language use.

In programming language semantics, the closest analogue of pragmatics is the distinction between *specification* and *implementation* -- between what a program is supposed to do (as specified by a requirements document, a type signature, or a contract) and what it actually does (as determined by its code). This distinction, while real and important, is much less pervasive and much more tractable than the pragmatics of natural language.

Pragmatics thus represents a genuine asymmetry among the three traditions: natural language semantics must grapple with context-dependence in a way that logical and programming language semantics do not. The eight bridges identified in Section 3 all concern *semantic* structure (compositional meaning, type-driven interpretation, model-theoretic truth); they do not extend to pragmatics. A unified theory of meaning that includes pragmatics would require bridges of a different kind -- perhaps game-theoretic models of rational communication (Lewis 1969; Clark 1996; Franke 2011) or decision-theoretic models of speaker choice (Frank and Goodman 2012).

### 5.2 Concurrency and Non-Determinism

Sequential computation maps neatly onto logical proof: the Curry-Howard correspondence relates proofs to sequential programs, and the structure of a proof (its introduction and elimination steps, its normalization) corresponds to the structure of a sequential computation (its construction and deconstruction of values, its evaluation). Concurrency -- multiple interacting processes executing simultaneously -- disrupts this correspondence.

Concurrent computation introduces *non-determinism* (the outcome of a computation may depend on the relative timing of concurrent events), *interaction* (processes communicate and synchronize), and *interference* (concurrent processes may share and mutate state). These features have no natural counterpart in classical sequential logic. Process calculi (CCS, CSP, the pi-calculus) provide formal models of concurrent computation, but their relationship to logic is more complex than the Curry-Howard correspondence for sequential computation. Linear logic (Girard 1987) has been proposed as the logical counterpart of concurrency: its resource-sensitive connectives (the multiplicative conjunction "tensor" and the exponential "bang") model the consumption and sharing of resources, and its proof nets provide a geometry of interaction that captures aspects of concurrent behaviour. However, the correspondence between linear logic and concurrency is less tight than the Curry-Howard correspondence for sequential computation, and a fully satisfactory "Curry-Howard for concurrency" remains an open problem.

In natural language, the analogue of concurrency is *multi-party discourse* -- conversation involving multiple participants who contribute simultaneously or in overlapping turns. The discourse representation structures of DRT are designed for monological discourse (a single speaker addressing a single hearer); extending them to dialogical or multi-party interaction requires additional machinery (e.g., the dialogue gameboards of Ginzburg 2012).

Concurrency thus represents a point where the logic-computation bridge weakens, and the extension to natural language is correspondingly strained. The game-theoretic perspective (Section 3.6) is perhaps the most promising approach to unification in this area, since games inherently involve multiple agents and interactive, concurrent moves.

### 5.3 The Distributional and Neural Challenge

Distributional semantics, rooted in the distributional hypothesis -- "a word is characterized by the company it keeps" (Firth 1957) -- derives the meaning of a word from statistical patterns of co-occurrence in large corpora. Words that appear in similar contexts are deemed semantically similar; the meaning of a word is represented as a point in a high-dimensional vector space (a *word embedding*). Modern neural language models (transformers, large language models) extend this approach to sentences and documents, learning contextual representations that capture rich semantic information.

Distributional semantics is, in some respects, the antithesis of the formal traditions surveyed in this dissertation. It is *sub-symbolic* (meanings are points in a continuous vector space, not structured logical forms); *non-compositional* in the classical sense (the meaning of a sentence is not built up recursively from the meanings of its words by rule-governed combination, but is computed by a learned function that may be opaque); and *empirical* (meanings are derived from data, not from a priori logical analysis). It has no obvious counterpart in logic or in classical programming language semantics.

Yet the relationship between distributional and formal semantics is not one of simple opposition. Several lines of research seek to reconcile the two:

First, *compositional distributional semantics* (Coecke, Sadrzadeh, and Clark 2010; Baroni, Bernardi, and Zamparelli 2014) combines the distributional approach to lexical meaning with the compositional structure of formal semantics. In the categorical model of Coecke et al., words are vectors, grammatical types are spaces, and composition is a tensor operation governed by the pregroup grammar (a variant of the Lambek calculus). This framework provides a categorical bridge between distributional and formal semantics, using the categorical machinery discussed in Section 4. The bridge is, however, a homomorphism rather than an isomorphism: the compositional distributional models approximate, but do not fully reproduce, the truth-conditional interpretations of formal semantics.

Second, *grounded semantics* approaches embed distributional representations in model-theoretic structures, providing a denotational interpretation of word vectors. The idea is that a word vector represents not the meaning of a word directly but the distribution of its uses, from which a formal denotation (an extension in a model) can be recovered by mapping vectors to model-theoretic objects.

Third, the *probing* paradigm in neural NLP investigates the extent to which neural language models learn formal semantic structure (syntax trees, semantic roles, entailment relations) from distributional data alone. The finding that neural models encode substantial formal structure suggests that the distributional and formal approaches may be complementary rather than contradictory: distributional learning may be a mechanism by which formal structure is acquired from data.

Nevertheless, the reconciliation of distributional and formal semantics remains incomplete, and it is not clear that a full unification is possible. The distributional approach captures aspects of meaning (graded similarity, contextual modulation, associative structure) that are absent from formal semantics; the formal approach captures aspects of meaning (entailment, scope, quantification, compositionality) that are absent from (or at best implicit in) distributional models. The relationship between the two may be one of complementarity rather than unification -- two perspectives on meaning that illuminate different facets of the same phenomenon.

### 5.4 Sub-Sentential Meaning and Compositionality Failures

Compositionality is the foundational axiom of all three formal semantic traditions (Section 3.1), but it is challenged by phenomena in all three domains. In natural language, the most familiar challenges are *idioms* ("kick the bucket" does not mean what its parts mean), *coercion* ("begin the book" requires a type-shifting operation to interpret "the book" as an event), and *co-predication* ("The book is heavy and interesting" predicates physical and informational properties of an object that must be simultaneously physical and abstract). These phenomena do not refute compositionality -- there are compositional analyses of idioms (as stored complex entries), coercion (as type-shifting operators), and co-predication (as dot-type objects in the sense of Pustejovsky) -- but they demonstrate that compositionality is a *theoretical commitment* that must be actively maintained through the postulation of additional mechanisms, not a simple observation about how meaning works.

In programming language semantics, compositionality is challenged by *non-local effects*: exceptions, continuations, mutable state, and concurrency all introduce dependencies between program fragments that are not captured by the local syntactic structure. Monads (Moggi 1989, 1991), algebraic effects (Plotkin and Power 2003), and continuation-passing style are techniques for *restoring* compositionality in the presence of effects, but they require rethinking what the "meanings" of subexpressions are (not values, but computations; not functions, but Kleisli arrows).

In logic, compositionality is challenged by *context-sensitive* and *self-referential* constructions: the liar paradox, Kripke's theory of truth, and the behaviour of the provability predicate all involve semantic phenomena that do not respect the compositional structure of formulae.

The parallel between compositionality challenges across traditions is itself a bridge, albeit of a different kind from the bridges of Section 3: the *failure modes* of compositionality are structurally similar across domains, and the *repair strategies* (type-shifting, monadic encapsulation, substructural restriction) share a common pattern of enriching the semantic framework to accommodate the recalcitrant phenomena.

### 5.5 Can Distributional Semantics Be Reconciled with Formal Traditions?

Returning to RQ3, the evidence surveyed in this section suggests that distributional semantics cannot be *fully* reconciled with the formal traditions in the sense of being reduced to or derived from them. The distributional approach embodies a fundamentally different ontology of meaning: meanings are not structured logical forms or model-theoretic denotations but points in a continuous geometric space, defined by statistical regularities in language use. This ontological difference resists elimination.

However, partial reconciliation is achievable and, indeed, is being actively pursued. The categorical framework of Coecke et al. provides a principled interface between distributional and compositional semantics; grounded semantics provides a denotational interpretation of distributional representations; and the probing paradigm reveals that distributional models implicitly encode formal structure. The emerging picture is one of *complementarity*: distributional semantics captures the associative, graded, context-sensitive aspects of meaning that are neglected by formal semantics, while formal semantics captures the logical, compositional, truth-conditional aspects that distributional semantics does not (at least not directly). A "grand unified semantics" would need to integrate both perspectives -- not by reducing one to the other, but by providing a framework in which both can be expressed and their relationship made precise. Category theory, once again, is the most promising candidate for such a framework, but the integration remains a programme rather than an accomplished fact.

## 6. Implications

### 6.1 For Philosophy: Is "Meaning" One Concept or Three?

The eight bridges documented in Section 3 provide strong evidence that meaning, as studied in logic, computer science, and natural language semantics, is not three unrelated concepts that happen to share a name. The bridges are too numerous, too structural, and too productive to be dismissed as mere homonymy. At the same time, the breakdowns documented in Section 5 show that the three traditions are not studying exactly the same thing: pragmatics, concurrency, and distributional meaning all represent genuine asymmetries among the three domains.

The philosophical conclusion suggested by this evidence is that "meaning" is a *cluster concept* -- a concept with a central core (compositional, type-theoretic, model-theoretic meaning) that is shared across all three traditions and a periphery (pragmatic meaning, distributional meaning, interactive meaning) that is distributed unevenly. The central core is unified; the periphery is not. This is analogous to the way "number" is a cluster concept: the natural numbers, the integers, the rationals, the reals, and the complex numbers share a core structure (algebraic operations, ordering) but differ in their periphery (closure properties, completeness, topological structure).

The philosophical implications extend to the debate between *truth-conditional* and *inferentialist* theories of meaning. The truth-conditional tradition (Frege, Tarski, Davidson, Montague) treats meaning as a relationship between expressions and the world (models). The inferentialist tradition (Gentzen, Dummett, Brandom) treats meaning as a relationship among expressions (inferential roles). The bridges of Section 3 show that both traditions have counterparts in all three domains, and that the two approaches are not competitors but complementary perspectives on the same phenomenon -- different functors from the same syntactic category to different (but related) semantic categories.

### 6.2 For Computer Science: What PL Design Learns from NL Semantics

Programming language design has historically proceeded with limited engagement with natural language semantics, despite the shared formal infrastructure. The bridges documented here suggest several areas where NL semantics could inform PL design.

First, *dynamic semantics* offers a model of how context can be systematically threaded through computation. The DRT notion of a discourse referent -- an entity introduced by an expression and available for subsequent reference -- is closely related to the notion of a mutable variable in a programming language, and the DRT framework for managing discourse referents could inform the design of languages for dialogue systems, chatbots, and interactive narrative.

Second, *type-theoretic natural language semantics* has developed sophisticated type systems for modeling phenomena (gradability, coercion, co-predication, plurality) that have analogues in programming but have received less attention from PL designers. Pustejovsky's generative lexicon, which models type coercion in natural language, offers a model for flexible type systems that accommodate coercion without sacrificing type safety.

Third, the game-theoretic perspective on meaning -- meaning as interaction rather than static assignment -- offers a paradigm for the design of reactive and interactive systems, in which the "meaning" of a system component is defined by its interactive behaviour rather than by its input-output function.

### 6.3 For Linguistics: What NLP Learns from PL Semantics

Conversely, natural language processing (NLP) and computational linguistics can benefit from the formal methods and conceptual frameworks of programming language semantics. Formal verification techniques -- model checking, theorem proving, abstract interpretation -- could be applied to the analysis of natural language arguments, detecting logical fallacies, verifying the consistency of legal documents, or checking the soundness of mathematical proofs written in natural language. The denotational approach -- assigning mathematical meanings to expressions and reasoning about programs by reasoning about their denotations -- offers a model for computational semantics that goes beyond the current pattern-matching approaches of neural NLP.

The full abstraction problem in programming language semantics -- the problem of constructing denotational models that distinguish exactly the operationally distinguishable programs -- has an analogue in natural language semantics: the problem of constructing semantic representations that distinguish exactly the communicatively distinguishable utterances. This analogue suggests that game-theoretic methods, which solved the full abstraction problem for PCF, might also illuminate problems of semantic granularity in natural language.

### 6.4 For System Design: Applied Unified Semantics in Multi-Representational Systems

The practical implications of a unified semantics extend to the design of complex software systems that must manage multiple representations of the same entities. A system that maintains a registry of entities (in a database), a set of schema contracts (in a specification language), a set of event routing rules (in a configuration format), and a set of user-facing descriptions (in natural language) is, in effect, implementing multiple semantic interpretations of the same syntactic objects. The bridges documented in this dissertation suggest that the relationships among these representations can be formalized as functors between categories, and that the coherence conditions that ensure consistency across representations can be stated as naturality conditions on transformations between functors.

The compositional, type-driven approach that unifies formal semantics across domains also applies to the design of naming conventions, schema contracts, and ontological layers in multi-representational systems. When an entity is identified by a name in a registry, by a schema contract in an automation system, and by a description in a user interface, the relationships among these identifications can be understood as instances of the same compositional, type-driven meaning assignment that operates in logic, programming languages, and natural language. A unified semantic framework provides the theoretical foundation for ensuring that these identifications are consistent and that changes propagate correctly across representations.

## 7. Discussion

The eight bridges documented in this dissertation vary in structural strength from isomorphism (compositionality, lambda calculus, model theory for classical fragments) through homomorphism (type theory, game semantics, proof-theoretic semantics, the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence extended to natural language) to analogy tending toward homomorphism (dynamic semantics). No bridge is a mere metaphor; all have substantive structural content that can be formalized mathematically.

This finding supports a positive answer to the dissertation's overarching question: the three traditions of semantics are not merely homonymous but are connected by deep structural bridges that reflect a genuine unity of subject matter. However, the unity is not total: pragmatics, concurrency, and distributional meaning represent genuine asymmetries that limit the scope of unification.

The role of category theory in this unification merits particular comment. Category theory provides the most perspicuous language for describing the bridges and for identifying their structural strengths and limitations. The compositional bridge is a functor; the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence is a categorical equivalence; type theory is a fibration; model theory is a functor category. These categorical descriptions are not mere redescription; they reveal structural connections (the equivalence of CCCs and typed lambda calculi, the internal language of a topos) that are invisible in the original formalisms. Nevertheless, categorical unification has limits: it is most successful for the classical, compositional, typed fragments of each tradition and least successful for the dynamic, concurrent, distributional extensions.

Returning to the three research questions:

**RQ1.** The Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence provides genuine structural unification for the classical, compositional core of each tradition. The extension to natural language, via the type-logical tradition and combinatory categorial grammar, is a homomorphism rather than an isomorphism: it captures the compositional, type-driven aspects of natural language meaning but does not extend to pragmatics, context-dependence, or distributional meaning.

**RQ2.** Dynamic semantics provides a natural unifying principle -- meaning as state transformation -- that is more naturally computational than truth-conditional semantics. However, it does not subsume truth-conditional semantics; rather, the two approaches are complementary, with truth-conditional semantics providing the static foundation and dynamic semantics providing the mechanism for context update. The dynamic perspective is more naturally suited to bridging natural language and programming language semantics; the truth-conditional perspective is more naturally suited to bridging natural language and logical semantics.

**RQ3.** Distributional semantics cannot be fully reconciled with the formal traditions in the sense of being reduced to them or derived from them. However, partial reconciliation is achievable through categorical frameworks (compositional distributional semantics), grounding approaches, and probing studies. The relationship between distributional and formal semantics is one of complementarity rather than unification.

---

## 8. Conclusion

This dissertation has undertaken a systematic investigation of the structural relationships among three traditions of formal semantics: logical semantics, programming language semantics, and natural language semantics. Through the identification and formal analysis of eight cross-domain bridges -- compositionality, lambda calculus, the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence, type theory, model theory, game semantics, dynamic semantics, and proof-theoretic semantics -- we have argued that the three traditions are not merely homonymous but are instances of a single mathematical framework, with category theory providing the most perspicuous unifying metalanguage.

The central finding is that the classical, compositional, typed fragment of each tradition admits genuine structural unification. Compositionality, lambda calculus, and model theory constitute isomorphic bridges: the same mathematical structures and the same structural relationships appear in all three traditions. Type theory, game semantics, the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence (extended to natural language), and proof-theoretic semantics constitute homomorphic bridges: the structural parallels are genuine and formally precise, but the cross-domain maps are not invertible. Dynamic semantics constitutes a bridge that is currently best characterized as an analogy tending toward homomorphism, with active research strengthening the formal connections.

The unification breaks down at the boundaries of the classical core. Pragmatics and context-dependence are pervasive in natural language but marginal in logic and programming. Concurrency and non-determinism are central to modern computation but poorly integrated with the Curry-Howard paradigm. Distributional semantics captures aspects of meaning that are absent from formal traditions and resists reduction to them. These breakdowns are not failures of the unification programme but boundary conditions that delimit its scope and identify directions for future research.

The implications of this investigation extend beyond the three traditions themselves. For philosophy, the findings suggest that "meaning" is a cluster concept with a unified core and a distributed periphery. For computer science, natural language semantics offers models of context-threading, flexible typing, and interactive meaning that could inform programming language design. For linguistics, programming language semantics offers formal verification techniques and denotational methods that could enhance computational semantics. For system design, the unified semantic framework provides a theoretical foundation for ensuring consistency across multiple representations of the same entities.

The prospect of a "grand unified semantics" -- a single formal framework that encompasses truth-conditional meaning, dynamic context change, computational behaviour, interactive meaning, inferential role, and distributional similarity -- remains, at the time of writing, an aspiration rather than an achievement. But the eight bridges documented here provide substantial evidence that this aspiration is not utopian. The three traditions of semantics are not three different subjects that happen to share a name; they are three perspectives on a single subject -- the formal structure of meaning -- and the bridges between them are load-bearing.
