# Measuring Actants: Toward a Flat Ontology of Sociotechnical Assessment

**Studium Generale ORGANVM -- Synthesis Paper SYN-04**

---

## Abstract

Psychometric measurement theory and actor-network theory (ANT) rest on incompatible ontological commitments. Psychometrics assumes stable latent constructs, fixed boundaries between instrument and object, and a separation of measurement from the measured. ANT assumes relationally constituted entities, performed boundaries, and the inseparability of observation from intervention. This paper confronts the collision directly: what happens when you apply validity theory -- with its apparatus of latent variables, construct validity, and measurement invariance -- to actants whose very existence is relationally constituted? We develop three resolution strategies. First, *performative measurement*, drawing on Karen Barad's agential realism, reconceives validity as consequential adequacy rather than correspondence to a pre-existing construct. Second, *network-relative constructs* redefine measurement invariance as invariance across network configurations rather than across populations of fixed entities. Third, *relational psychometrics* reimagines item response theory with relationally constituted "items" and "examinees," treating the ability parameter as network position rather than intrinsic property. These strategies are applied to the domain of automated software quality assessment, where repository "quality" is shown to be a relational property performed by CI/CD pipelines, not an intrinsic attribute discovered by neutral instruments. The paper concludes with five design principles for flat-ontology assessment systems that measure relations rather than entities, track network configuration alongside scores, build consequential validity into feedback loops, and accept that measurement constitutes its object.

**Keywords:** actor-network theory, psychometrics, flat ontology, measurement validity, construct validity, agential realism, performativity, sociomateriality, software quality, automated assessment

## 1. Introduction

Every measurement rests on a metaphysics, whether or not the measurer acknowledges it. When a psychometrician administers an intelligence test, the act presupposes that intelligence exists as a stable attribute of the person, that the test taps into this attribute without fundamentally altering it, and that the resulting score corresponds -- imperfectly but meaningfully -- to a quantity that the person possesses independent of the measurement occasion. These are not mere methodological conveniences. They are ontological commitments: claims about what exists, how it exists, and how its existence relates to the instruments that purport to detect it.

These commitments have been spectacularly productive. The psychometric tradition, from Spearman's (1904) formulation of classical test theory through Messick's (1989) unified validity framework to contemporary item response theory (IRT), has built an elaborate and empirically tested apparatus for evaluating the quality of measurements. The resulting technology of assessment -- educational testing, clinical diagnosis, personnel selection, program evaluation -- is among the most consequential applications of social science. Its power derives precisely from its ontological commitments: the assumption of stable latent constructs enables the mathematics of factor analysis; the assumption of separability between instrument and object enables the concept of measurement error; the assumption of correspondence between score and construct enables the very notion of validity.

Yet a parallel tradition in the social sciences has spent four decades systematically dismantling each of these commitments. Actor-network theory (ANT), developed by Bruno Latour, Michel Callon, and John Law within science and technology studies (STS), posits that entities do not possess stable intrinsic properties but are relationally constituted through the networks in which they participate. An entity *is* what it *does* in the network -- the translations it performs, the associations it enters into, the differences it makes. Boundaries between entities are not given but performed: they are achieved through ongoing practices of association-building and must be continuously maintained or they dissolve. And observation is never neutral; it is always an intervention that reshapes the network it purports to describe. ANT's defining commitment -- the principle of generalized symmetry, which requires that human and nonhuman entities be described using the same analytical vocabulary -- flattens the ontological hierarchy that Western philosophy has traditionally imposed between subjects and objects, observers and observed, measurers and measured.

The collision between these two traditions is not merely academic. It becomes operationally consequential wherever sociotechnical systems employ quantitative assessment. Consider automated software governance: continuous integration pipelines compute code coverage percentages, linting tools count style violations, complexity analyzers assign numerical scores, and promotion gates aggregate these signals into composite readiness indicators. These measurement activities presuppose -- without examination -- that "software quality" is a stable construct, that automated checks tap into it without fundamentally altering the development process, and that the resulting scores correspond to a property that repositories possess independent of the assessment apparatus. Every one of these presuppositions is contestable, and the tradition best equipped to contest them -- ANT and its allied frameworks in STS -- has rarely engaged with the psychometric tradition that formalizes them.

This paper attempts the engagement. It poses a single overarching question: *Can you measure something whose boundaries are relationally constituted?* The question is not rhetorical. Psychometrics provides formal tools -- latent variable models, validity frameworks, measurement invariance tests -- that presuppose fixed entities. ANT provides analytical vocabulary -- actants, translation, enrollment, blackboxing, obligatory passage points -- that dissolves fixed entities into relational processes. If the two traditions are genuinely incompatible, then every quantitative assessment of a sociotechnical system is either psychometrically naive (ignoring the relational constitution of its object) or ontologically conservative (smuggling in fixed entities through the back door of mathematical formalism). If, however, the traditions can be productively bridged, the result would be a richer theory of measurement -- one that acknowledges the relational, performed, and constitutive character of its objects while retaining the formal rigor that makes psychometric assessment trustworthy.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 maps the tension between psychometric and ANT assumptions, identifying the precise points of collision. Section 3 develops three resolution strategies: performative measurement (drawing on Barad's agential realism), network-relative constructs, and relational psychometrics. Section 4 applies these strategies to automated software quality assessment. Section 5 distills the analysis into design principles for flat-ontology assessment systems. Section 6 concludes.

## 2. The Tension

The incompatibility between psychometric measurement theory and actor-network theory is not a surface disagreement about methods. It is a deep ontological divergence about the nature of the entities that measurement purports to assess. This section maps the divergence by examining the core assumptions of each tradition and identifying the points at which they collide.

### 2.1 What Psychometrics Assumes

Psychometric measurement theory, as codified by Lord and Novick (1968) and unified by Messick (1989), rests on three interlocking assumptions that are so fundamental they are rarely made explicit.

**First, latent variables exist.** The central posit of psychometrics is that observed test performance is caused by an unobservable latent trait -- intelligence, depression, writing ability, software quality. This latent variable is real in the sense that it has causal power: it generates patterns in observable data. The entire mathematical apparatus of psychometrics -- classical test theory's X = T + E, item response theory's logistic item characteristic curves, structural equation modeling's path diagrams -- presupposes that latent variables exist as attributes of entities (persons, artifacts, systems) and that observed scores are noisy indicators of these attributes. Without this assumption, the mathematics is meaningless: factor analysis finds factors, but the factors are nothing if they do not correspond to real attributes. As Borsboom, Mellenbergh, and van Heerden (2004) argued, validity in its deepest sense requires that the latent variable exist and that variations in the latent variable causally produce variations in observed scores.

**Second, constructs are stable.** Psychometric models assume that the latent construct being measured is sufficiently stable to support meaningful measurement. Classical test theory defines the true score as the expected value of observed scores across hypothetical repeated measurements -- a definition that presupposes that the attribute being measured does not change between measurement occasions (or at least changes slowly relative to the measurement cycle). Item response theory's claim to "specific objectivity" -- the invariance of item parameters across populations and of person parameters across item sets -- requires that the construct maintain a consistent meaning across contexts. Measurement invariance testing, which checks whether the same factor structure holds across groups, assumes that the construct exists in the same form in each group. Without stability, there is nothing to be invariant.

**Third, measurement is separable from the measured.** The concept of measurement error -- the difference between the observed score and the true score -- presupposes that the act of measurement does not alter the true score. The test measures intelligence; it does not create, constitute, or fundamentally transform intelligence. This separability assumption is built into the very architecture of classical test theory: E (error) is uncorrelated with T (true score), which means the measurement process and the attribute being measured are independent. If the act of measuring changed the thing being measured, error and true score would be correlated, and the entire mathematical framework would collapse. This is not to say that psychometricians are naive about reactivity -- they recognize that test-taking can produce anxiety, practice effects, and strategic behavior. But these are treated as threats to validity, not as constitutive features of measurement. The aspiration is a measurement procedure that captures the construct without constituting it.

These three assumptions are not optional features of the psychometric approach. They are the load-bearing structure on which construct validity, reliability estimation, and measurement invariance testing all depend. Remove any one of them, and the psychometric edifice requires fundamental reconstruction.

### 2.2 What ANT Assumes

Actor-network theory, as articulated by Latour (2005), Callon (1986), and Law (2004), makes three counter-assumptions that directly challenge the psychometric foundations.

**First, entities are relationally constituted.** In ANT's flat ontology, an entity does not possess intrinsic properties that it carries from one network to another. An entity *is* its network position -- the set of associations it currently maintains, the translations it currently performs, the differences it currently makes. Latour's principle of irreduction holds that "nothing is, by itself, either reducible or irreducible to anything else" (Latour, 1988, p. 158); entities are defined by what they do, not by what they are "in themselves." A language model prompted as a coding assistant is a different actant than the same model prompted as a creative writer -- not merely because its outputs differ, but because its network position, its enrollment conditions, and its constitutive relations are fundamentally different. The entity does not have a fixed essence that manifests differently in different contexts; it *is* differently constituted in different contexts.

Karen Barad's agential realism radicalizes this commitment. For Barad (2007), entities do not pre-exist their relations; they are produced through "intra-action" -- a term she distinguishes from the ordinary notion of interaction, which presupposes pre-existing relata. "Relata do not precede relations" (Barad, 2007, p. 140). The apparatus of observation and the object of observation are not independent entities that come into contact; they are mutually constituted through the apparatus's enacted "agential cut" -- the local, contingent, and always revisable determination of what counts as the observer and what counts as the observed. There is no entity apart from its intra-actions; there is no "latent variable" waiting to be discovered behind the veil of measurement.

**Second, boundaries are performed, not given.** In ANT, the boundary between one entity and another -- between the instrument and the object, between the measurer and the measured, between the system and its environment -- is not a natural fact but a social and material accomplishment. Latour's concept of *punctualization* describes how a complex network comes to function as a single point -- how "the laboratory" becomes a single actant in a larger network, hiding the heterogeneous labor of technicians, instruments, reagents, and practices that constitute it. Boundaries are sustained through ongoing work; when the work ceases, the boundary dissolves, and the punctualized entity is *depunctualized* -- revealed as the network it always was. Mol's (2002) concept of ontological multiplicity extends this: the "same" entity may be enacted differently in different practices, producing not different perspectives on a single object but genuinely different objects that are partially connected.

For measurement, the consequence is radical. The boundary between the measuring instrument and the object being measured is not a given of the measurement situation but an achievement of the measurement practice. The test does not encounter a pre-existing construct; the test participates in constituting the construct it purports to measure. This is not an unfortunate confound to be controlled for; it is a constitutive feature of all measurement, obscured by the psychometric assumption of separability.

**Third, observation is intervention.** ANT's concept of translation -- the process by which actors define problems, interest other actors, enroll them in roles, and mobilize them as allies -- implies that any act of inquiry is simultaneously an act of world-making. To study a network is to participate in it, to trace associations is to strengthen or weaken them, and to produce a description is to create an artifact (an "immutable mobile" in Latour's terminology) that circulates through the network and reshapes it. The observer is always already an actant.

This resonates with, but is distinct from, the observer effect in physics, where measurement disturbs the system being measured. The quantum mechanical measurement problem concerns the physical interaction between apparatus and system; ANT's point is more fundamental. It is not merely that measurement *disturbs* its object (a practical complication that better instruments might minimize) but that measurement *constitutes* its object (an ontological condition that no instrument can escape). Ian Hacking's (1983) analysis of "making up people" -- the way that classificatory systems do not merely sort pre-existing kinds but bring new kinds into being -- captures a closely related insight: psychiatric categories do not discover pre-existing mental illnesses; they participate in constituting the very conditions they classify, through what Hacking calls "looping effects" in which the classified person responds to the classification and thereby changes the reality the classification was meant to capture.

### 2.3 The Collision: Validity Theory Meets Actants

When the psychometric framework is applied to entities whose existence is relationally constituted, several specific collisions occur.

**The latent variable problem.** If entities are relationally constituted, what is the ontological status of a latent variable? In psychometrics, the latent variable is an intrinsic attribute of the entity being measured -- a property it possesses independent of the measurement occasion. In ANT, there are no intrinsic attributes; all properties are relational. A "latent variable" would have to be reconceived as a feature of the network configuration, not of the entity. But if the latent variable is a network property, it changes every time the network changes -- every time an association is added, removed, or modified. This means the "true score" is not stable but is continuously constituted by the network's ongoing activity. Classical test theory's X = T + E becomes incoherent if T is not a fixed value but a moving target constituted by the very process (including measurement) that produces X.

**The construct stability problem.** If boundaries are performed, the "construct" being measured is not a fixed domain to be sampled but an enacted reality to be performed. Content validity -- the question of whether the measurement procedure samples the full domain of the construct -- presupposes that the construct has a fixed domain that exists prior to and independent of the measurement procedure. If the construct is constituted in part by the measurement procedure itself, then content validity becomes circular: the measurement defines the construct it claims to measure. This is precisely the "construct-operationalization collapse" that RP-07 identified as a validity threat in automated software governance: when "quality" is defined as whatever the quality metrics measure, there is no independent standard against which to evaluate the metrics.

**The measurement invariance problem.** Measurement invariance testing asks whether the same construct is being measured in the same way across groups. But if entities are relationally constituted, then entities in different network configurations are *different entities*. A repository in a Python microservices ecosystem and a repository in a Rust embedded systems context are not the same kind of entity measured under different conditions; they are differently constituted entities participating in different networks. The question of whether a quality metric "means the same thing" across these contexts is not a question about the stability of a fixed construct across populations but a question about the commensurability of differently constituted entities -- a much harder problem that measurement invariance testing, as currently formulated, is not designed to address.

**The observer effect problem.** If observation is intervention, then the act of measuring software quality changes the quality being measured. This is not merely a practical concern (developers game metrics) but an ontological one (the measurement apparatus participates in constituting the quality it purports to assess). A CI/CD pipeline that computes code coverage does not discover a pre-existing coverage level; it *enacts* coverage as a specific kind of quality indicator, and in doing so, it reshapes the development practices, the code structure, and the meaning of "quality" in that organizational context. Goodhart's Law -- "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure" (Goodhart, 1984) -- is the empirical surface manifestation of an ontological condition that ANT makes explicit: measurement constitutes its object, and the constituted object is never identical to the construct the measurement was designed to capture.

The question, then, is whether this collision is terminal -- whether psychometrics and ANT are simply incommensurable -- or whether productive resolution strategies exist that preserve the formal rigor of psychometric assessment while accommodating the relational ontology of ANT. The next section argues for the latter and develops three such strategies.

## 3. Three Resolution Strategies

The collision between psychometrics and ANT admits of at least three productive resolution strategies, each drawing on distinct intellectual resources and each yielding different implications for the practice of assessment. These strategies are not mutually exclusive; they address different aspects of the collision and can be combined in a comprehensive approach to what we term *flat-ontology assessment*.

### 3.1 Strategy 1: Performative Measurement

The first strategy draws on Karen Barad's agential realism and Michel Callon's performativity thesis to reconceive measurement as a performative practice rather than a representational one. On this view, measurement does not *discover* pre-existing qualities of pre-existing entities; it *performs* qualities into existence through specific material-discursive practices.

Barad's concept of the "agential cut" is central. In agential realism, every measurement involves an agential cut -- a local, contingent, and material determination of where the boundary falls between the measuring apparatus and the measured object. This cut is not a human decision imposed on a pre-existing reality; it is a material-discursive practice that constitutes both the apparatus and the object simultaneously. "It is through specific agential intra-actions that the boundaries and properties of the 'components' of phenomena become determinate and that particular embodied concepts become meaningful" (Barad, 2007, p. 139). The agential cut produces, rather than discovers, the entity being measured and the property being assessed.

Applied to psychometrics, this means that the act of constructing a test, selecting items, and administering the assessment is not a neutral process of tapping into a pre-existing latent variable. It is a performative practice that enacts a specific version of the construct it purports to measure. An intelligence test does not discover intelligence; it performs a specific version of intelligence into existence -- one that is constituted by the particular items chosen, the particular administration conditions, the particular scoring procedures, and the particular social context in which the test is embedded. This is not a criticism of the test (as though a "better" test could discover "real" intelligence); it is an ontological redescription of what testing does.

The implications for validity theory are substantial. If measurement is performative rather than representational, then the central question of validity shifts. Classical validity asks: does the measurement correspond to a pre-existing construct? Performative validity asks: does the measurement *produce* consequences that are adequate for the purposes for which the measurement is used? This is a shift from *correspondence adequacy* to *consequential adequacy*.

Messick (1989) himself opened the door to this reconception when he included "consequential validity" -- the social consequences of score use -- as a component of his unified validity framework. But Messick treated consequential validity as one type of evidence among many, subordinate to the master concept of construct validity, which retained its correspondence structure. A fully performative approach would invert this hierarchy: consequential adequacy becomes the master concept, and correspondence evidence (convergent validity, discriminant validity, factor structure) becomes one type of evidence bearing on the question of whether the measurement produces adequate consequences.

This inversion is not merely philosophical. It has concrete methodological implications. A performative approach to validation would ask not "Does this quality score correlate with the latent construct of software quality?" (a question that presupposes the construct's independent existence) but rather "Does this quality score, when used in governance decisions, produce organizational outcomes that the stakeholders consider desirable?" The validation criterion shifts from correspondence to a theoretical construct to consequential adequacy for a practical purpose. Cronbach and Meehl's (1955) nomological network -- the web of theoretical relationships in which a construct is embedded -- is replaced by a *consequential network* -- the web of practical effects that the measurement produces when deployed in its intended context.

This strategy preserves the formal apparatus of psychometrics (factor analysis, IRT, measurement invariance testing) but reinterprets its findings. A factor analysis that reveals a clean single-factor structure is not evidence that a unitary construct *exists*; it is evidence that the measurement procedure *performs* a unitary construct -- that the items, taken together, produce a coherent pattern that functions as though it measured one thing. Whether this performance is adequate depends not on its correspondence to reality but on its consequences for the practices in which it is embedded. The measurement is valid not because it is *true* but because it *works* -- and "works" is defined relative to the network of practices, purposes, and stakeholders in which the measurement circulates.

The risk of this strategy is relativism: if validity is consequential adequacy, then any measurement that produces desirable consequences is valid, regardless of its relationship to the world. The corrective is Haraway's (1988) concept of *situated knowledges*: the insistence that all knowledge claims are produced from a particular position, and that the relevant question is not "Is this true from nowhere?" but "Is this adequate from somewhere -- and whose somewhere?" Situated validity would demand that the measurement's consequences be evaluated from multiple positions, especially from the positions of those most affected by the measurement and least powerful in shaping it.

### 3.2 Strategy 2: Network-Relative Constructs

The second strategy retains the psychometric concept of the latent construct but relativizes it to a network configuration. Rather than assuming that a construct exists absolutely -- that "software quality" is a single thing across all contexts -- this strategy defines constructs relative to the network in which they are enacted. "Software quality" in a Python microservices ecosystem is a different construct from "software quality" in a Rust embedded systems context, because the networks are different: different tools, different practices, different standards, different actants, different translations.

This is not the same as the standard psychometric concern with measurement invariance. Measurement invariance testing asks whether the *same* construct is measured in the *same way* across groups. Network-relative constructs deny the premise: the constructs may be genuinely different across network configurations, and the question is not whether the instrument achieves invariance but whether the instrument's variation across configurations is *informative* about the differences between configurations.

The formal apparatus for implementing this strategy already exists in psychometrics, though it is not typically interpreted in network-relational terms. Multi-group confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) can be used to test whether the factor structure, factor loadings, and intercepts are equivalent across groups. When invariance fails -- when the factor structure differs across groups, or when loadings are non-equivalent -- the standard psychometric interpretation is that the measurement is biased and needs revision. The network-relative interpretation is different: non-invariance is not necessarily bias; it may be informative evidence that the construct is genuinely different across network configurations.

Consider the concrete case of test coverage as a quality indicator. RP-07 demonstrated that 80% test coverage in Python, TypeScript, Rust, and Go may not indicate the same underlying level of testing diligence, because the difficulty of achieving coverage varies with language features, type systems, and testing conventions. The standard psychometric response is to seek invariance -- to adjust the metric or the threshold until it means the same thing across languages. The network-relative response is to accept non-invariance as evidence that "testing quality" is constituted differently in each language ecosystem and to measure accordingly: different instruments (or different calibrations of the same instrument) for different network configurations, with explicit acknowledgment that the resulting scores are not directly comparable.

This strategy requires a formal account of "network configuration" that is precise enough to serve as the grouping variable in multi-group analyses. ANT's concept of the *network* is notoriously resistant to formalization -- Latour himself argued that ANT is a method for tracing associations, not a theory of network structure. But for the purposes of measurement, a pragmatic operationalization is possible: a network configuration can be characterized by the set of actants present (tools, languages, frameworks, human practices, organizational structures), the translation relationships among them (dependencies, workflows, governance procedures), and the obligatory passage points that structure interaction (protocol layers, review gates, promotion criteria). Two repositories are in the "same" network configuration if they share the same actants, translations, and OPPs to a specified degree of similarity. This operationalization is imperfect -- any boundary drawn around a network configuration is itself an agential cut -- but it is sufficient to support the multi-group analyses that the strategy requires.

The deeper insight of this strategy is that *measurement invariance is a network property, not a construct property*. A measurement achieves invariance not because the construct is the same everywhere but because the network configurations across which it is applied are similar enough that the measurement procedure performs the construct in the same way. When network configurations diverge -- when the tools, practices, and associations that constitute the measured entity are substantially different -- invariance fails, and it fails informatively: the failure tells us something about the differences between networks, not just about the inadequacy of the instrument.

This reconception has a precedent in Mol's (2002) work on ontological multiplicity. Mol argues that "the body" is not a single entity measured from different perspectives but *multiple* bodies enacted through different practices -- the body in the clinic, the body in the laboratory, the body in daily life. These are not different views of the same thing but different things that are partially connected. Analogously, "software quality" is not a single construct viewed through different metrics but multiple constructs enacted through different assessment practices, partially connected by shared vocabulary and overlapping indicators but not reducible to a single underlying reality.

### 3.3 Strategy 3: Relational Psychometrics

The third strategy is the most technically ambitious: it proposes a reconceptualization of item response theory in which both the "items" (assessment criteria) and the "examinees" (assessed entities) are understood as relationally constituted, and the "ability parameter" is reinterpreted as a network position rather than an intrinsic property.

In standard IRT, the model specifies the probability that examinee *j* responds correctly to item *i* as a function of the examinee's ability parameter theta_j and the item's difficulty and discrimination parameters (b_i, a_i). The ability parameter is an intrinsic property of the examinee -- a position on a latent continuum that the examinee occupies independent of the specific items encountered. The miracle of specific objectivity, in the Rasch tradition, is that the ability estimate is item-independent: different sets of items yield the same ability estimate, because the ability is a property of the person, not of the person-item encounter.

Relational psychometrics would reinterpret theta_j not as an intrinsic property of examinee *j* but as a characterization of *j*'s position in the assessment network -- the network constituted by *j*'s relations to the items, to the assessment apparatus, to the governance context, and to the other examinees. This is a subtle but consequential shift. In standard IRT, theta_j is a *cause* of the response pattern: the examinee's ability causally generates the pattern of correct and incorrect responses. In relational IRT, theta_j is a *summary* of the examinee's network position: it describes where the examinee sits in the network of relations constituted by the assessment, without claiming that this position is an intrinsic property that the examinee carries from one assessment to another.

The formal mathematics of IRT need not change. The logistic model P(X_ij = 1 | theta_j, a_i, b_i) = 1 / (1 + exp(-a_i(theta_j - b_i))) can be retained as a useful approximation, even if the ontological interpretation of theta_j shifts from intrinsic ability to network position. What changes is the *interpretive framework* surrounding the model: the claims made about what theta_j *means* and the conditions under which it can be meaningfully compared across examinees.

In particular, relational psychometrics would predict that theta_j is *not* invariant across assessment contexts -- that the "ability" of an examinee depends on the specific items, the specific apparatus, and the specific network configuration in which the assessment occurs. This is precisely what standard IRT treats as a pathology (differential item functioning, lack of measurement invariance) and what relational psychometrics treats as an expected consequence of the relational constitution of ability. The examinee does not have a fixed ability that is distorted by context; the examinee's ability *is* constituted by context, and variation across contexts is informative, not pathological.

The practical implication is that comparisons of theta estimates across different assessment contexts (different item sets, different governance systems, different network configurations) require explicit theoretical justification. One cannot assume that theta = 1.5 in context A and theta = 1.5 in context B indicate the same underlying quality, because theta is a context-relative quantity. Comparisons are possible only when the contexts are sufficiently similar -- when the network configurations share enough actants, translations, and OPPs that the assessment performs the construct in comparable ways. This is a more demanding requirement than standard measurement invariance testing, because it requires not just statistical equivalence of item parameters but *network-theoretic equivalence* of the contexts in which the assessment is deployed.

A further implication concerns the item parameters themselves. In standard IRT, item difficulty (b_i) and discrimination (a_i) are properties of the item -- intrinsic characteristics that the item possesses regardless of who encounters it (given adequate model fit). In relational psychometrics, item parameters are properties of the *item-in-context* -- characterizations of how the item functions within a specific network configuration. An item may be easy in one network (where the tools, practices, and conventions make passing it straightforward) and hard in another (where different tools, practices, and conventions make the same criterion more demanding). This is, again, what standard IRT calls DIF and treats as bias. Relational psychometrics reinterprets DIF as evidence of the relational constitution of item properties -- evidence that the item's difficulty is not an intrinsic feature but a network effect.

The most radical implication concerns the latent continuum itself. In standard IRT, the latent continuum -- the theta dimension -- is assumed to represent a single, unidimensional trait along which examinees can be ordered. In relational psychometrics, the continuum is not a pre-existing dimension along which entities are distributed but a *constructed* dimension produced by the assessment apparatus. The assessment does not discover the continuum; it enacts it. Different assessments may enact different continua -- not merely different scales for measuring the same thing, but genuinely different dimensions of variation. This is the measurement-theoretic equivalent of Mol's ontological multiplicity: different assessment practices enact different realities, and the question of whether two assessments "measure the same thing" is not a question about a fixed underlying reality but a question about the partial connections between differently enacted realities.

## 4. Implications for Software Quality Assessment

The three resolution strategies developed in the preceding section are not merely theoretical exercises. They have direct implications for the design and interpretation of automated software quality assessment systems -- the domain in which the psychometric tradition (as developed in RP-07) and the ANT tradition (as developed in RP-05) most concretely collide.

### 4.1 Repository "Quality" as Relational Property

The first and most fundamental implication is that repository quality is not an intrinsic property of a repository but a relational property constituted by the network in which the repository participates. A repository does not "have" quality in the way that a physical object has mass. Its quality is constituted by its relations to its test suite, its CI/CD pipeline, its dependency graph, its development team, its users, its organizational context, its governance framework, and the assessment instruments that purport to measure it.

This reconception challenges the foundational assumption of RP-07's psychometric framework: that "software quality" is a latent variable -- an unobservable attribute of the repository that causally generates observable indicators (coverage scores, lint counts, build stability). The ANT-informed view suggests instead that quality is *performed* -- enacted through the practices of testing, linting, building, reviewing, and deploying that constitute the repository's participation in its network. The observable indicators are not effects of a latent cause; they are constitutive elements of the quality they purport to indicate.

The practical difference is significant. If quality is a latent variable, then the appropriate response to a low quality score is to improve the underlying quality, and the score should improve as a consequence. If quality is a relational performance, then the quality score is not a transparent window onto an underlying reality but a participant in constituting that reality. Improving the score and improving quality are not the same thing, because the score is part of the network that constitutes quality, not a neutral observer of an independent construct.

This does not mean that quality scores are useless. It means they must be interpreted differently -- as characterizations of a repository's *network position* rather than measurements of an *intrinsic attribute*. A high quality score indicates that the repository is well-integrated into a network of assessment practices, not that it possesses a large quantity of some substance called "quality." The distinction matters because it shapes how organizations respond to scores: not by "raising quality" (as though quality were a thermostat to be adjusted) but by strengthening the network of practices that constitute quality in their specific context.

### 4.2 CI Checks as Performative Measurements

Continuous integration checks -- automated tests, linting rules, coverage thresholds, security scans, build stability assessments -- are typically understood as neutral instruments that *detect* properties of the code. The code either has 80% coverage or it does not; the linter either finds violations or it does not; the build either passes or it does not. On this view, CI checks are intermediaries in Latour's sense: they transport information about the code without transforming it.

The ANT-informed view reveals CI checks as mediators: they do not merely detect code properties but actively participate in constituting them. A CI pipeline that enforces a coverage threshold does not simply measure coverage; it *performs* coverage into a consequential quality indicator -- one that shapes development practices, code structure, and organizational attention. Before the threshold was imposed, coverage was one metric among many, attended to or ignored at the developer's discretion. After the threshold, coverage becomes an obligatory passage point: all code must pass through the coverage check to advance in the promotion pipeline. The check transforms coverage from a descriptive statistic into a governance instrument, and in doing so, it changes what coverage *means* -- from "the proportion of code executed during testing" to "a constraint that must be satisfied for deployment."

This transformation is an instance of Callon's translation: the CI pipeline problematizes quality (framing it in terms of specific, checkable criteria), interests the repository in passing the checks (by making advancement conditional on passage), enrolls the code in the assessment framework (by executing the checks and recording the results), and mobilizes the results as immutable mobiles (scores, badges, reports) that circulate through the organization. Each step is a performative act that constitutes the quality it purports to measure.

Barad's agential realism provides the sharpest formulation. The CI check is an apparatus that enacts an agential cut -- a determination of where the boundary falls between "quality" and "not quality." This cut is material (it depends on the specific tools, configurations, and thresholds deployed), local (it applies to this repository in this context), and consequential (it determines whether the code advances). The cut does not discover a pre-existing boundary between quality and not-quality; it *produces* this boundary through the specific material-discursive practice of automated assessment. Different CI configurations -- different tools, different thresholds, different coverage engines -- would produce different boundaries, enacting different versions of quality. There is no "true" boundary waiting to be discovered; there are only the boundaries that specific assessment apparatuses enact.

### 4.3 The Observer Effect in Automated Assessment

The observer effect in automated assessment operates at multiple levels, each corresponding to a different mechanism by which the act of measurement changes the thing measured.

**Direct behavioral modification.** The most obvious effect is Goodhart's Law: when a metric becomes a target, developers optimize for the metric rather than the construct it is meant to indicate. Coverage thresholds produce tests that execute code without testing meaningful behavior. Lint compliance produces code that satisfies the linter's rules without necessarily being readable. Complexity thresholds produce functions decomposed into small units that may be individually simple but collectively harder to understand than the original monolithic function. These are not merely practical problems to be solved by better metrics; they are structural consequences of the performative character of measurement. The measurement constitutes its object, and the constituted object is the one that developers actually optimize.

**Construct drift.** More subtly, the repeated application of automated assessment shifts the meaning of the construct being assessed. "Quality" in an organization that has used coverage-based assessment for five years means something different from "quality" in an organization that has never measured coverage -- not because the organizations have different *theories* of quality but because their quality constructs have been differently *constituted* by their assessment practices. The measurement does not merely detect quality; it shapes the organizational understanding of what quality is. This is Hacking's (1983) "looping effect" applied to artifacts rather than persons: the classification system (the quality assessment) changes the classified thing (the code and the development practices), which changes the meaning of the classification, which further changes the thing, in an ongoing recursive loop.

**Network reconfiguration.** At the deepest level, the introduction of automated assessment reconfigures the actor-network in which the assessed entity participates. Before assessment, the repository's quality is constituted by its relations to its developers, its users, and its deployment context. After assessment, quality is additionally constituted by relations to CI tools, coverage engines, lint configurations, and governance pipelines. These new actants are not neutral observers; they are mediators that transform the network. The repository-after-assessment is a different entity from the repository-before-assessment, because it participates in a different network with different actants, different translations, and different obligatory passage points. The measurement has not merely observed the entity; it has *changed what the entity is*.

The practical consequence is that automated assessment systems cannot be designed as though they were neutral instruments applied to a fixed object. They must be designed as participants in the networks they assess -- actants that shape the entities they purport to measure. The design question is not "How can we measure quality without disturbing it?" (an impossible aspiration, given the performative character of measurement) but "How can we measure quality in ways that constitute it desirably?" -- ways that shape development practices, code structures, and organizational attention in directions that the stakeholders consider beneficial.

### 4.4 Goodhart's Law Through the ANT Lens

Goodhart's Law -- "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure" -- is among the most widely cited observations in the social sciences. But the standard interpretation treats it as a practical problem: people game metrics, and the gaming undermines the metric's validity. The solution, on this view, is better metrics -- measures that are harder to game, more robust to strategic behavior, more tightly coupled to the "real" construct.

ANT provides a deeper interpretation. Goodhart's Law is not a practical problem but an ontological condition -- a necessary consequence of the performative character of measurement. When a measure becomes a target, it does not merely *fail to track* an independent construct; it *reconstitutes* the construct. The measured entity adapts to the measurement, and in adapting, it changes what the construct *is*. The construct is no longer what it was before the measurement was imposed; it is now a hybrid of the original construct and the measurement procedure. Goodhart's Law, on this reading, is not about gaming; it is about the constitutive entanglement of measurement and reality.

Callon's (1998) analysis of the performativity of economics provides a parallel. Callon argued that economic models do not merely describe markets; they participate in constituting the markets they describe. The Black-Scholes options pricing model, for example, did not simply discover the correct price for options; it *created* a market infrastructure (trading platforms, hedging strategies, regulatory frameworks) in which options were priced according to the model's predictions. The model's success was not a matter of correspondence to a pre-existing market reality but a matter of performative efficacy: the model reshaped the world until the world matched the model.

Similarly, a governance system that defines quality in terms of specific metrics does not merely describe quality; it participates in constituting a version of quality in which those metrics are central. The quality construct that exists *after* the governance system is deployed is different from the quality construct that existed *before* -- not because the governance system measured incorrectly but because measurement is constitutive. The implication is that Goodhart's Law cannot be solved by better metrics alone. It can only be managed by designing assessment systems that are aware of their constitutive effects and that incorporate feedback mechanisms for monitoring and adjusting those effects over time. This is the domain of consequential validity -- the component of Messick's unified framework that asks whether the social consequences of score use are defensible -- reinterpreted through the ANT lens as a question about the network effects of performative measurement.

## 5. Design Principles for Flat-Ontology Assessment

The preceding analysis yields five design principles for assessment systems that take the flat ontology of ANT seriously while retaining the formal rigor of psychometric methodology. These principles do not replace the psychometric framework; they supplement it with ontological awareness drawn from STS. Together, they constitute what we call *flat-ontology assessment* -- an approach to sociotechnical measurement that acknowledges the relational constitution of its objects, the performative character of its instruments, and the constitutive entanglement of measurement and reality.

### 5.1 Measure Relations, Not Entities

The first principle follows directly from ANT's relational ontology: the primary objects of assessment should be *relations* -- the associations, translations, and dependencies that constitute sociotechnical networks -- rather than *entities* conceived as self-contained bearers of intrinsic properties.

In software governance, this means shifting the focus of assessment from repository-level attributes (coverage, complexity, lint score) to relational properties: the quality of the repository's integration with its test suite, the stability of its dependency relationships, the coherence of its interfaces with other repositories, the reliability of its translation chains (from human intent through prompting through tool use through code generation through review). These relational properties cannot be captured by metrics applied to the repository in isolation; they require assessment of the network in which the repository participates.

Concretely, relational assessment would supplement entity-level metrics (which remain useful as partial indicators) with network-level metrics: dependency health not as a count of outdated packages but as a characterization of the repository's position in a dependency network; test quality not as coverage percentage but as a measure of the alignment between test structure and production use patterns; documentation adequacy not as the presence of a README but as a measure of the repository's communicative integration with its users and contributors.

The psychometric framework supports this shift through structural equation modeling (SEM), which can model relationships among latent variables rather than treating each variable in isolation. A relational quality model would specify quality not as a single latent variable but as a network of related constructs -- maintainability, reliability, usability, security -- with the relationships among them explicitly modeled. The fit of this relational model to observed data would constitute evidence of the relational structure of quality, providing a formal basis for the claim that quality is a network property.

### 5.2 Track Network Configuration Alongside Scores

The second principle addresses the context-dependence of measurement in a relational ontology: every score should be accompanied by a description of the network configuration in which it was produced. A quality score without its network context is an uninterpretable number -- a measurement stripped of the conditions that give it meaning.

In practical terms, this means that governance systems should record not only the assessment results (pass/fail, scores, ratings) but also the assessment context: which tools were used (and at what versions), which checks were applied (and at what thresholds), what the repository's dependency network looked like at the time of assessment, what the development team's practices were, and what organizational structures governed the assessment process. This metadata is not supplementary information; it is constitutive of the score's meaning.

The psychometric parallel is the reporting of test conditions in educational assessment: the testing environment, the time limits, the accommodations provided, the form of the test administered. Psychometricians have long recognized that test scores are interpretable only in the context of the conditions under which they were obtained. Flat-ontology assessment extends this recognition to the full network context, acknowledging that the "conditions" that matter are not just the immediate circumstances of test administration but the entire network of relations in which the assessment is embedded.

For measurement invariance, this principle has a specific implication: invariance should be tested not across arbitrary groupings (all Python repositories vs. all TypeScript repositories) but across *network configurations* -- groups of repositories that share similar actants, translations, and OPPs. Two Python repositories in the same organizational context with similar dependency structures and CI configurations constitute a more meaningful comparison group than all Python repositories across heterogeneous organizations. The grouping variable for invariance testing should be network configuration, not surface characteristics like programming language.

### 5.3 Build Consequential Validity into the Feedback Loop

The third principle operationalizes the performative measurement strategy (Section 3.1) by making consequential validity an ongoing, built-in feature of the assessment system rather than an after-the-fact evaluation.

Messick (1989) argued that the social consequences of score use are part of the validity argument. But in practice, consequential validity is rarely assessed systematically. Assessment systems are designed, deployed, and used, and the consequences are evaluated (if at all) only retrospectively, after patterns of score use are established and their effects become visible. By then, the constitutive effects of the measurement are already embedded in organizational practices, and changing course is costly.

Flat-ontology assessment would build consequential monitoring into the assessment system from the start. This means designing feedback loops that track the downstream effects of assessment decisions: Do repositories that pass the governance checks actually perform better in production? Do developers who optimize for the metrics also improve the underlying quality of their code? Do the governance decisions produce the organizational outcomes they are intended to produce? And, critically: Does the assessment system introduce pathological incentives -- Goodhart-type dynamics in which the measurement distorts the practices it is meant to improve?

Campbell's Law -- "the more any quantitative social indicator is used for social decision-making, the more subject it will be to corruption pressures and the more apt it will be to distort and corrupt the social processes it is intended to monitor" (Campbell, 1979) -- is the specific pathology that consequential monitoring is designed to detect. In a flat-ontology framework, Campbell's Law is not a bug to be fixed but a structural feature of performative measurement. The assessment system must monitor for its own constitutive effects and adjust accordingly. This requires longitudinal tracking, periodic recalibration, and explicit mechanisms for revising assessment criteria when their constitutive effects become undesirable.

The IRT framework provides a natural vehicle for this monitoring. Item information functions can be tracked over time: if a governance check's discrimination parameter declines (indicating that it no longer distinguishes between high-quality and low-quality repositories), this may be evidence of Goodhart-type gaming -- developers have learned to pass the check without improving the quality it was designed to indicate. Declining discrimination is a signal to revise or replace the check, or to supplement it with less gameable alternatives. This temporal monitoring of item parameters is not standard practice in educational assessment (where items are typically used for a limited period and then retired) but is essential in governance assessment, where the same checks are applied indefinitely and the entities being assessed can adapt to them.

### 5.4 Accept That Measurement Constitutes Its Object

The fourth principle is the most philosophically demanding and the most practically consequential: assessment system designers must accept that their instruments participate in constituting the objects they measure, and they must design accordingly.

This acceptance changes the design posture from *detection* to *constitution*. The question is not "How can we accurately detect quality?" but "How can we constitute quality desirably?" -- how can we design assessment systems whose constitutive effects produce the kinds of development practices, code structures, and organizational dynamics that the stakeholders value?

This is an uncomfortable shift for engineers trained in the positivist tradition, who understand measurement as neutral detection and validity as correspondence to reality. But it is the shift that ANT, agential realism, and the sociology of quantification all demand. And it is, in fact, more honest than the alternative: every assessment system already constitutes its object; the only question is whether the designers acknowledge this and design for it, or whether they deny it and design in ignorance of their own constitutive power.

Concretely, this principle implies that assessment criteria should be chosen not only for their diagnostic value (do they discriminate between high-quality and low-quality repositories?) but also for their *constitutive* value (do they encourage desirable development practices?). A check that has high discrimination but encourages harmful practices (e.g., a coverage threshold that incentivizes trivial tests) is constitutively counterproductive, regardless of its diagnostic utility. A check that has moderate discrimination but encourages beneficial practices (e.g., a requirement for documented architecture decisions that promotes reflective design) may be constitutively superior, even if it is diagnostically weaker.

The design framework becomes a dual optimization: maximize diagnostic value (the instrument's ability to distinguish between entities of different quality levels) *and* maximize constitutive value (the instrument's ability to shape the practices it measures in desirable directions). These objectives may conflict -- a highly discriminating check may constitute its object pathologically, and a constitutively beneficial check may discriminate poorly -- and the resolution of such conflicts is a design judgment that cannot be made on purely technical grounds. It requires input from all actants in the assessment network: developers, managers, users, governance architects, and the assessment instruments themselves (insofar as their behavior feeds back into the design process).

### 5.5 Design for Ontological Pluralism

The fifth principle follows from Mol's ontological multiplicity and the network-relative constructs strategy (Section 3.2): assessment systems should accommodate multiple, partially connected versions of the constructs they measure, rather than imposing a single, universal definition.

In software governance, this means accepting that "quality" is multiple -- that it is constituted differently in different network configurations and that no single set of metrics can capture all its enactments. A Python data pipeline, a TypeScript web application, a Rust systems library, and a Go infrastructure service each participate in different networks with different actants, different translations, and different constitutive practices. "Quality" in each context is a different thing, partially connected to "quality" in the other contexts by shared vocabulary and overlapping indicators but not reducible to a single underlying construct.

The psychometric apparatus for handling this multiplicity is multi-group CFA with partial invariance: some aspects of the measurement model may be invariant across contexts (supporting partial comparability), while others may vary (reflecting genuine differences in the construct). The flat-ontology contribution is the interpretive frame: variation is not bias to be corrected but multiplicity to be respected. The assessment system should produce *profiles* rather than *scores* -- multidimensional characterizations of each entity's quality as constituted in its specific network context, rather than a single number that pretends to capture a universal construct.

This design principle resonates with the finding from RP-07 that factor analysis of software quality metrics may reveal multiple distinct factors rather than a single unitary construct. The flat-ontology framework provides the ontological justification for this finding: quality is multiple because the networks that constitute it are multiple. A multidimensional quality profile is not a second-best approximation of a "true" unitary score; it is the more adequate representation of a genuinely multiple reality.

## 6. Conclusion

This paper has confronted a collision between two of the most productive traditions in the social sciences: psychometric measurement theory, which provides the formal apparatus for evaluating the quality of quantitative assessments, and actor-network theory, which provides the analytical vocabulary for understanding how entities, properties, and boundaries are relationally constituted in sociotechnical networks. The collision is genuine: psychometrics assumes stable constructs, fixed boundaries, and separable measurement; ANT assumes relational constitution, performed boundaries, and constitutive measurement. Neither tradition can simply absorb the other.

The three resolution strategies developed here -- performative measurement, network-relative constructs, and relational psychometrics -- do not dissolve the tension. They manage it, by providing specific, actionable ways to retain the formal rigor of psychometric assessment while accommodating the ontological insights of ANT. Performative measurement shifts validity from correspondence to consequential adequacy. Network-relative constructs redefine measurement invariance as invariance across network configurations. Relational psychometrics reinterprets the ability parameter as network position rather than intrinsic property. Each strategy preserves the mathematics while transforming the interpretation.

The application to software quality assessment demonstrates that these are not merely philosophical niceties. Repository quality is a relational property constituted by CI pipelines, dependency networks, development practices, and governance frameworks. CI checks are performative measurements that constitute the quality they purport to detect. Goodhart's Law is not a practical annoyance but an ontological condition -- a necessary consequence of the constitutive entanglement of measurement and reality. And the design of assessment systems is not a neutral engineering task but a constitutive practice that shapes the entities and networks it purports to evaluate.

The five design principles -- measure relations rather than entities, track network configuration alongside scores, build consequential validity into feedback loops, accept that measurement constitutes its object, and design for ontological pluralism -- provide a concrete framework for what we have called flat-ontology assessment. This framework does not reject psychometrics; it enriches it with the ontological awareness that STS provides. And it does not reject ANT's insights; it gives them a formal framework through which they can be operationalized in the design of actual assessment systems.

The aspiration is a measurement practice that is both rigorous and reflexive -- one that applies the full apparatus of psychometric validity theory while acknowledging that the entities it measures are constituted in part by the very act of measurement. This is a demanding aspiration, and the present paper is only a first step. But it is a necessary step, because the alternative -- measurement that is rigorous but ontologically naive, or ontologically sophisticated but formally undisciplined -- is inadequate for the sociotechnical systems we now build and inhabit.
