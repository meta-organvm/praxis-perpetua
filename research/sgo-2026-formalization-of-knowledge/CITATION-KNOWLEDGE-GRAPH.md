1. **The Formal Core** (Frege, Kripke, Tarski, Turing, Godel, Lambek, Howard, Curry, Montague) -- these authors establish the logical and formal-semantic foundations that unify RP-01, RP-02, RP-06, SYN-01, and SYN-05.

3. **The Measurement Cluster** (Cronbach, Messick, Meredith, Goodhart, Campbell, Strathern) -- these authors bridge psychometrics to governance, connecting RP-07 to SYN-02 and SYN-04.

4. **The Embodied Alternative** (Varela, Lakoff, Barsalou, Brandom) -- these authors represent the programme's acknowledged boundary, connecting the formal papers (RP-01, SYN-01, SYN-05) to questions the programme explicitly brackets.

## 7. Missing Canonical Works

Important works in each relevant field that the programme probably SHOULD cite but does not.

### Mathematical Logic & Foundations
- **Godel, K. (1930)** -- Completeness theorem. The programme cites the incompleteness theorems but not the completeness theorem that establishes the contrast (complete for first-order logic, incomplete for arithmetic).
- **Hilbert, D. (1899/1928)** -- *Grundlagen der Geometrie* / Hilbert's programme. The incompleteness theorems responded to Hilbert's programme, which is discussed but never formally cited.
- **Church, A. (1936b)** -- Church's thesis (the equivalence of lambda-definability and effective computability). Cited as (1936) for the unsolvable problem paper, but the thesis itself deserves explicit citation as the foundation of the computability-semantics bridge.
- **Post, E. (1944)** -- "Recursively enumerable sets of positive integers and their decision problems." Post's parallel discovery of undecidability deserves citation alongside Turing.

### Philosophy of Language
- **Putnam, H. (1981)** -- *Reason, Truth and History*. The brain-in-a-vat argument is directly relevant to RP-02's self-reference analysis.
- **Burge, T. (1979)** -- "Individualism and the mental." Anti-individualism about meaning is relevant to RP-04's social dimension of naming.
- **Kaplan, D. (1989)** -- "Demonstratives." The theory of direct reference extends Kripke's work and is relevant to RP-04.
- **Lewis, D. (1970/1986)** -- Convention and modal realism. Convention theory is central to RP-04's analysis of naming conventions, and Lewis's possible-worlds semantics extends Kripke's work cited in RP-01.

### Category Theory & Formal Semantics
- **Awodey, S. (2010)** -- *Category Theory*. A standard textbook reference for the categorical framework central to SYN-01.
- **Mac Lane, S. (1971)** -- *Categories for the Working Mathematician*. Foundational text, co-authored by Eilenberg (who IS cited), but Mac Lane alone is not.
- **Barwise, J. and Perry, J. (1983)** -- *Situations and Attitudes*. Situation semantics is a major alternative to Montague-style semantics, relevant to RP-01.
- **Partee, B.H. (2004)** -- *Compositionality in Formal Semantics*. Foundational for the compositionality bridge in RP-01/SYN-01.

### STS & Sociology of Science
- **Jasanoff, S. (2004)** -- *States of Knowledge*. Co-production of science and social order is directly relevant to RP-05's ANT analysis.
- **Bowker, G. and Star, S.L. (1999)** -- *Sorting Things Out*. Classification as infrastructure -- directly relevant to RP-04 (naming) and SYN-03 (naming as infrastructure). Star is cited for boundary objects but not for this work with Bowker.
- **Latour, B. (2010)** -- "Networks, Societies, Spheres." Latour's own revision of ANT vocabulary is relevant to RP-05.

### Psychometrics & Measurement
- **Kane, M.T. (2006)** -- "Validation." The argument-based validity framework that extends Messick. Relevant to RP-07.
- **Borsboom, D. (2005)** -- *Measuring the Mind*. Goes beyond the 2004 paper (which IS cited) to develop a full theory of validity.
- **van der Linden, W.J. and Hambleton, R.K. (1997)** -- *Handbook of Modern Item Response Theory*. Standard reference for the IRT framework used in RP-07.

### AI, Alignment, and Governance
- **Russell, S. (2019)** -- *Human Compatible*. Directly relevant to CAPSTONE's "human as incompleteness response" argument.
- **Bengio, Y. et al. (2024)** -- International Scientific Report on the Safety of Advanced AI. Relevant to RP-05's AI governance discussion.
- **Ostrom, E. (1990)** -- *Governing the Commons*. Polycentric governance is a major alternative to both hierarchy and flat governance, directly relevant to RP-03 and SYN-02.
- **Williamson, O. (1975)** -- *Markets and Hierarchies*. Transaction cost economics provides a formal framework for when hierarchy outperforms markets, relevant to RP-03.

### Embodied Cognition
- **Thompson, E. (2007)** -- *Mind in Life*. Extends Varela et al. (1991), which IS cited extensively, but Thompson's solo work deepens the enactive framework.
- **Noe, A. (2004)** -- *Action in Perception*. Enactive perception theory is relevant to SYN-05's discussion of non-formal meaning.
- **Gallagher, S. (2005)** -- *How the Body Shapes the Mind*. Directly relevant to the embodied meaning discussion in SYN-05.

### Philosophy of Science
- **Kuhn, T.S. (1962)** -- *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*. Remarkably absent for a programme that discusses paradigm shifts, normal science, and the sociology of knowledge.
- **Lakatos, I. (1978)** -- *The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes*. The concept of research programmes is directly relevant to the SGO's self-understanding.
- **Popper, K. (1959)** -- *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*. Falsifiability is discussed in SYN-05 and CAPSTONE but Popper is never cited.

## 9. Intellectual Architecture Summary

The SGO programme's citation infrastructure reveals a research programme built on four intellectual pillars, connected by bridge authors:

```
    FORMAL FOUNDATIONS                    SOCIOTECHNICAL LENS
    ==================                    ==================
    Godel, Tarski, Turing                 Latour, Callon, Star
    Curry, Howard, Lambek                 Haraway, Barad, Suchman
    Frege, Kripke, Montague               Law, Mol, Akrich
    Chomsky, Steedman                     Scott, Deleuze & Guattari
              |                                    |
              |          BRIDGE AUTHORS            |
              |    ===========================     |
              |    Varela (cognition <-> systems)   |
              |    Brandom (inference <-> meaning)  |
              |    Coecke (categories <-> NLP)      |
              |    Star (STS <-> infrastructure)    |
              |    Goodhart/Strathern (measure <->  |
              |       governance)                   |
              |                                    |
    MEASUREMENT SCIENCE                   EMBODIED ALTERNATIVE
    ===================                   ====================
    Cronbach, Messick, Rasch              Lakoff, Varela, Barsalou
    Stevens, Guttman, Lord                Gendlin, Merleau-Ponty
    Borsboom, Meredith                    Clark, Thompson
    Campbell, Goodhart                    (bracketed but acknowledged)
```

The programme's deepest insight -- that knowledge formalizes through naming, structuring, computing, and reflecting, and that each phase encounters characteristic impossibilities -- is visible in the citation structure itself. The impossibility authors (Godel, Tarski, Turing, Rice, Arrow, Goodhart) appear most broadly across the programme, while the domain-specific authors (Chomsky, Rasch, Latour) concentrate in their home papers. The bridge authors (Varela, Star, Brandom) appear wherever the programme crosses a disciplinary boundary.

The most notable structural feature of the citation graph is its *symmetry*: the formal tradition (left pillar) and the sociotechnical tradition (right pillar) have roughly equal citation mass and comparable internal density. The programme does not privilege one tradition over the other -- it genuinely synthesizes both. The embodied alternative (bottom right) is the acknowledged gap: present enough to mark the boundary, absent enough to signal future work.
