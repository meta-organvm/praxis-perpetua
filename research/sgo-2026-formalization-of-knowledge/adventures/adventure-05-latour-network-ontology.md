**Date:** 2026-03-20

## Central Question

**How does actor-network theory apply to human-AI collaborative systems?**

If Bruno Latour insisted that microorganisms are actors in Pasteur's network --- that scallops participate in the politics of St Brieuc Bay --- then what happens when the nonhuman actant is not passive matter but a language model that generates text, writes code, and proposes plans? ANT was built to dissolve the subject/object binary. The emergence of agentic AI systems forces us to ask whether ANT's radical symmetry was prescient or whether it now requires fundamental revision.

## Seed Articles

### Bruno Latour (1947--2022)
French philosopher, anthropologist, and sociologist. Co-developer of actor-network theory alongside Michel Callon, Madeleine Akrich, and John Law at the Centre de Sociologie de l'Innovation (Ecole des Mines de Paris). Latour diverged from social constructivism to reject the subjective/objective division entirely. His later work sought to rebuild trust in science while maintaining that "matters of fact" must become "matters of concern." Key works: *Laboratory Life* (1979), *Science in Action* (1987), *We Have Never Been Modern* (1991), *Reassembling the Social* (2005), *Facing Gaia* (2017).

**Relevance to central question:** Latour's entire project was to show that human agency is never pure --- it is always distributed across networks of humans, instruments, texts, institutions, and natural entities. AI systems are the most articulate nonhuman actants yet encountered.

### We Have Never Been Modern (1991)
Latour's thesis that modernity's defining gesture --- the "purification" that separates Nature from Society, object from subject, science from politics --- was always a fiction. In practice, modernity proliferates "hybrids" (quasi-objects, quasi-subjects) that exist in the space between these categories. The subtitle, *Essai d'anthropologie symetrique*, signals the methodological commitment: study the moderns with the same symmetrical lens anthropologists apply to other cultures.

**Relevance:** AI systems are quintessential hybrids. They are simultaneously technical artifacts, social constructions, economic instruments, and quasi-autonomous agents. The modern constitution that separates "tool" from "agent" fails to account for them.

### Assemblage (philosophy)
Concept from Deleuze and Guattari (*agencement*), subsequently adopted by Latour, Callon, DeLanda, and Jane Bennett. Assemblage theory frames social complexity through fluidity and connectivity, asserting that human action requires material interdependencies distributed across legal, geographical, cultural, and economic infrastructures. The central thesis: agency is not located in individuals but redistributed across socio-material networks.

**Relevance:** A human-AI work session is an assemblage --- user intent, model weights, prompt context, tool definitions, file systems, API endpoints, and institutional norms all participate in producing output. No single element "causes" the result.

### Rhizome (philosophy)
Deleuze and Guattari's model for non-hierarchical, multiply-connected networks where "any point can be connected to any other point." Contrasted with the arborescent (tree-like) model of knowledge. Latour himself half-jokingly suggested renaming ANT to "actant-rhizome ontology."

**Relevance:** Multi-agent architectures, tool-use protocols like MCP, and distributed knowledge systems are structurally rhizomatic. There is no single root; any tool can invoke any other; context flows laterally.

### Structuralism
The intellectual tradition (Saussure, Levi-Strauss, early Barthes) that interprets elements of culture by their relationships within a broader system. "Phenomena of human life are not intelligible except through their interrelations." Visited 7 times in reading history --- a persistent gravitational center.

**Relevance:** ANT inherits structuralism's relational ontology but rejects its stable, ahistorical structures. Where structuralism sees fixed grammars, ANT sees constantly negotiated networks. AI systems present a test case: are there stable "structures" in human-AI interaction, or only emergent patterns?

### Post-structuralism
The critique of structuralism's claim to stable, self-sufficient interpretive frameworks. Derrida, Foucault, Deleuze, Baudrillard. Rejects fixed definitions, emphasizes that meaning is constituted by power relations and context. ANT is "much more firmly embedded in English-language academic traditions than most post-structuralist-influenced approaches" but reflects many of the same preoccupations.

**Relevance:** The meaning of an AI's output is never fixed --- it depends on who reads it, what context frames it, what authority structures validate it. Post-structuralist insights about interpretive instability apply directly.

### Heterarchy
Systems of organization where elements are unranked or can be ranked multiple ways. First employed in a modern context by cybernetician Warren McCulloch (1945) to describe neural organization. "The human brain, while reasonably orderly, was not organized hierarchically. This understanding revolutionized the neural study of the brain and solved major problems in the fields of artificial intelligence and computer design."

**Relevance:** The connection to early AI is direct and historical. McCulloch's heterarchical neural model prefigures both connectionist AI architectures and ANT's flat ontology. Human-AI systems are heterarchical: neither party consistently outranks the other.

### Bricolage
Construction from whatever is available (Levi-Strauss). The bricoleur works with pre-existing materials, improvising solutions rather than engineering from first principles. Used across anthropology, philosophy, critical theory, education, and software.

**Relevance:** Prompt engineering, tool composition, and multi-step AI workflows are bricolage. The operator assembles pre-existing capabilities (models, tools, context files, system prompts) into novel configurations.

### Contextualism
The family of views asserting that actions, utterances, and expressions can only be understood relative to context. In epistemology, "knows" is context-sensitive. In architecture, modern building types are harmonized with traditional urban forms.

**Relevance:** AI outputs are radically context-dependent --- the same model produces different results depending on system prompt, conversation history, available tools, and user intent. Contextualism provides the epistemic framework for understanding why.

### Modernism
The early 20th-century movement emphasizing experimentation, abstraction, and subjective experience. Rejected Enlightenment rationalism and absolute originality; embraced collage, reprise, and reflexivity about convention.

**Relevance:** Latour's critique targets the *epistemological* claims of modernity, not the aesthetic movement. But modernism's techniques --- collage, montage, reflexivity --- describe how AI systems compose outputs from fragments.

### Critical Theory
The Frankfurt School tradition (Horkheimer, Adorno, Habermas) and its descendants. Centers on analyzing systemic power relations, arguing that knowledge and truth are shaped by power dynamics. Explicitly aims at transformation through praxis.

**Relevance:** ANT has been criticized from critical-theory positions for being "ill-suited to the task of developing political alternatives" (Whittle and Spicer). This tension is acute for AI: who benefits from the network? Whose labor is rendered invisible?

### Culture Theory
The branch of comparative anthropology and semiotics seeking operational definitions of culture. A background framework for understanding how ANT intersects with broader cultural analysis.

## Expansion Articles

### Actor-Network Theory
ANT is a theoretical and methodological approach where everything in the social and natural worlds exists in constantly shifting networks of relationships, with nothing existing outside those relationships. All factors --- objects, ideas, processes, humans --- are treated as equally important actants. Developed at the CSI (Ecole des Mines) in the 1980s by Callon, Akrich, Latour, and Law. Technically described as a "material-semiotic" method: it maps relations that are simultaneously material (between things) and semiotic (between concepts).

Key principles:
- **Generalized symmetry:** Human and nonhuman entities must be described in the same terms before a network is considered. Differences are generated *in* the network, not before it.
- **Translation:** The process by which a network comes to be represented by a single entity. Four moments: problematization, interessement, enrollment, mobilization (Callon 1986).
- **Punctualization:** When a network functions smoothly, it appears as a single actor (like a working car). When it breaks down, its components become visible (like a car with a dead engine). Analogous to encapsulation in object-oriented programming.
- **Blackboxing:** "The way scientific and technical work is made invisible by its own success" (Latour). When a machine runs efficiently, one focuses only on inputs and outputs.
- **Obligatory passage point (OPP):** A bottleneck that forces actors to converge, becoming necessary for network formation. Defines the action program and mediates all interactions.
- **Intermediaries vs. mediators:** Intermediaries transport force without transformation (ignorable). Mediators multiply difference and cannot be predicted from their inputs (must be studied).
- **Quasi-objects/quasi-subjects:** Neither purely object nor purely subject; their status shifts depending on their position in the network. Examples: language, money, bread, the ball in a soccer game.

ANT is "not a theory *of* anything, but rather a method" (Latour). It does not explain *why* networks form; it describes *how* relational ties work.

### Donna Haraway
American scholar in science and technology studies, feminist theory, and ecofeminism. Criticizes anthropocentrism, emphasizes self-organizing powers of nonhuman processes. First tenured professor in feminist theory in the US (UC Santa Cruz). Key contributions: *Primate Visions* (1989), *Simians, Cyborgs, and Women* (1991), *Modest_Witness* (1997), *Staying with the Trouble* (2016).

**Relevance:** Haraway's cyborg figure anticipated the dissolution of human/machine boundaries that AI makes literal. Her emphasis on "situated knowledges" and partial perspectives challenges the fantasy of objective, view-from-nowhere AI.

### A Cyborg Manifesto (1985)
Haraway's foundational essay rejecting rigid boundaries between human/animal and human/machine. The cyborg represents the plasticity of identity and the limitations of socially imposed categories. Argues for coalition through affinity rather than identity politics. "The cyborg does not dream of community on the model of the organic family."

**Relevance:** The cyborg is the conceptual ancestor of the human-AI collaborative pair. Haraway's refusal of origin myths ("it is not made of mud and cannot dream of returning to dust") parallels the situation of AI agents that have no single author, no unified body, no organic genesis.

### Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO)
21st-century school (Graham Harman, Levi Bryant, Timothy Morton, Ian Bogost) rejecting the privileging of human existence. Objects exist independently of perception and are not exhausted by their relations. Unlike ANT, OOO insists that objects have a "withdrawn" dimension that is never fully accessible to any relation.

**Relevance:** OOO and ANT agree on rejecting anthropocentrism but diverge on relations. ANT says entities are *constituted by* their relations; OOO says entities *exceed* their relations. For AI: is a language model fully described by its network of training data, architecture, and deployment context? Or does it possess something withdrawn?

### Graham Harman
American philosopher, founder of object-oriented philosophy (1999). Distinguished Professor at SCI-Arc. Central figure in speculative realism. Developed OOO partly *against* Latour's relational ontology while acknowledging deep debts to it.

### Speculative Realism
Movement in contemporary philosophy defining itself against "correlationism" --- the post-Kantian claim that we can only know the correlation between thought and being, never reality itself. Includes OOO, Quentin Meillassoux's speculative materialism, Ray Brassier's nihilism, and Iain Hamilton Grant's nature-philosophy.

**Relevance:** Speculative realism asks: can we think the world without humans? AI provokes the inverse: can we think thinking without biology?

### Science and Technology Studies (STS)
Interdisciplinary field examining the creation, development, and consequences of science and technology in their historical, cultural, and social contexts. The institutional home of ANT, SCOT, and related approaches. Key figures: Latour, Callon, Law, Bijker, Pinch, Jasanoff, Haraway, Suchman, Collins.

**Relevance:** STS provides the disciplinary infrastructure for studying human-AI systems without defaulting to either technological determinism or social constructivism.

### Boundary Object
Concept from Susan Leigh Star and James Griesemer (1989). Objects that are "plastic enough to adapt to local needs and constraints of several parties employing them, yet robust enough to maintain a common identity across sites." Used differently by different communities but maintaining structural coherence.

**Relevance:** In human-AI systems, boundary objects are everywhere: API schemas, prompt templates, CLAUDE.md files, seed.yaml contracts, tool definitions. Each is interpreted differently by the human operator, the AI model, and the execution environment, yet must maintain enough shared structure to enable collaboration.

### Flat Ontology
*No dedicated Wikipedia article.* The term describes ontological frameworks that refuse to privilege any level of reality (micro/macro, individual/social, natural/cultural) over another. Used across ANT, OOO, and DeLanda's assemblage theory. Latour: the network "has no a priori order relation; it is not tied to the axiological myth of a top and of a bottom of society."

**Relevance:** Flat ontology resists the hierarchical framing where humans "use" AI as a "tool." Instead, human and AI are co-actants in a flat network. This reframes accountability, authorship, and agency.

### Symmetry (social science)
*No dedicated Wikipedia article.* In ANT, the "principle of generalized symmetry" (Callon, Latour) requires that human and nonhuman entities be described using the same analytical vocabulary. The analyst must not switch explanatory registers when moving from human motivations to technical constraints. This is the methodological core of ANT.

**Relevance:** When analyzing a human-AI coding session, symmetry demands we describe the human's prompt and the model's response in the same terms --- as translations, enrollments, mediations --- rather than treating one as "real agency" and the other as "mere computation."

### Sociomateriality
Theory examining "the constitutive entanglement of the social and the material in everyday organizational life" (Orlikowski and Scott). Adopted ANT's focus on relations, combined with Karen Barad's agential realism and Lucy Suchman's feminist STS. Opposes Kantian subject/object dualism. Key claim: the social and the material are *inextricable*.

**Relevance:** Directly applicable to AI-mediated work. The code a developer writes with AI assistance is simultaneously social (shaped by norms, intentions, collaboration patterns) and material (shaped by model architecture, token limits, tool availability).

### Technological Determinism
The reductionist claim that technology follows its own internal logic and determines social structure. ANT explicitly rejects this: technology neither determines nor is fully determined by society. Both are co-constituted in networks.

**Relevance:** The dominant popular narrative about AI --- that it will inevitably transform/destroy/transcend human work --- is technologically determinist. ANT provides the vocabulary to resist this by insisting on the contingency and negotiability of sociotechnical arrangements.

### Social Construction of Technology (SCOT)
Theory (Bijker, Pinch) arguing that technology does not determine human action; human action shapes technology. A response to technological determinism. Includes the concept of "interpretive flexibility" --- the same artifact means different things to different social groups.

**Relevance:** SCOT is ANT's sibling framework. Where SCOT privileges the social side (humans shape technology), ANT insists on symmetry (humans and technologies co-shape). For AI, SCOT reminds us that "what the AI does" is partly constituted by how different communities interpret its outputs.

### Translation (sociology)
In ANT, the process by which a network comes to be represented by a single entity. Encompasses "all negotiations, intrigues, calculations, and acts of persuasion" through which an actor takes authority to speak on behalf of others. Developed by Michel Serres, applied to sociology by Callon. Crucially, nonhumans can enroll others "in exactly the same way as humans do."

**Relevance:** When an AI agent summarizes a codebase, proposes a refactoring, or drafts a commit message, it is performing translation --- re-presenting a complex network (code, tests, conventions, user intent) as a single coherent proposal. The question is: who authorized this translation?

### Immutable Mobile
*No dedicated Wikipedia article.* Latour's concept for inscriptions, documents, maps, and other artifacts that can be transported across contexts without transformation (immutable) while remaining transportable (mobile). Scientific papers, legal contracts, and standardized metrics are immutable mobiles. They allow "action at a distance."

**Relevance:** In human-AI systems: system prompts, tool schemas, configuration files (CLAUDE.md, seed.yaml), and API specifications function as immutable mobiles. They travel between contexts (different sessions, different operators) while maintaining their prescriptive force.

### Karen Barad
American feminist theorist and physicist. Developer of agential realism. Influenced by Niels Bohr's philosophy of physics. Challenges traditional metaphysics by arguing that entities do not pre-exist their interactions.

### Agential Realism
Barad's theory that things emerge through "intra-actions" rather than pre-existing and then interacting. Entities are formed through their connections. Challenges the assumption of independently existing parts. Influential in science studies, feminist theory, and new materialism.

**Relevance:** Agential realism radicalizes ANT's relational ontology. For AI: the "human operator" and the "AI model" do not exist as fixed entities that then interact. They are *constituted* by the interaction. The human who writes with AI assistance is not the same agent as the human who writes alone. The model prompted by this human is not the same model prompted by another.

### Mediation (philosophy)
*No dedicated Wikipedia article under this title.* In ANT, mediation refers to the transformative work that actants perform on one another within a network. Latour identifies four dimensions of technical mediation: interference, composition, the folding of time and space, and crossing the boundary between signs and things. Mediators (as opposed to intermediaries) transform what passes through them.

**Relevance:** An AI system is a mediator, not an intermediary. It does not simply transmit the user's intent; it transforms it. The output is never a transparent rendering of the input. This is the crux of the philosophical challenge: mediation implies that the AI *participates* in the production of meaning.

### Obligatory Passage Point (OPP)
Callon's concept for a bottleneck that forces actors to converge. An OPP "mediates all interactions between actors in a network and defines the action program." A strong OPP controls resources and takes credit for network successes; a weak OPP can be bypassed.

**Relevance:** In multi-agent AI systems, the orchestrating agent (or the human operator) functions as an OPP. In MCP-based architectures, the protocol layer itself is an OPP --- all tool invocations must pass through it.

### Blackboxing
"The way scientific and technical work is made invisible by its own success" (Latour). Functioning systems become opaque; their internal complexity is hidden behind inputs and outputs.

**Relevance:** AI models are the most consequentially blackboxed artifacts in history. A language model's internal complexity (billions of parameters, trillions of training tokens) is rendered invisible by the simplicity of its interface: text in, text out. ANT's vocabulary of blackboxing and depunctualization provides precise tools for analyzing this opacity.

## The ANT Framework

### Core Architecture

ANT rests on five interlocking commitments:

1. **Relational ontology.** Entities have no essential properties outside their network relations. A scallop is one thing in the ocean, another in the marine biologist's experiment, another in the fisherman's catch. Similarly, a language model is one thing in a research lab, another in a coding assistant, another in a creative writing tool.

2. **Generalized symmetry.** The same vocabulary must describe human and nonhuman actants. We cannot say the programmer "intends" while the model merely "processes." Both must be described as performing translations, enrollments, and mediations.

3. **Material semiotics.** Relations are simultaneously material (physical, computational, infrastructural) and semiotic (meaningful, interpretive, signifying). A prompt is both a string of tokens (material) and an instruction with pragmatic force (semiotic).

4. **Empirical description over theoretical explanation.** ANT resists explaining networks by invoking external forces (capitalism, patriarchy, technological progress). Instead, it traces the actual connections. "Explanation does not follow from description; it is description taken that much further" (Latour).

5. **Processual temporality.** Networks must be constantly performed or they dissolve. There is no stable social structure; there are only ongoing practices of network-building. An AI-assisted workflow exists only as long as the human keeps prompting, the model keeps responding, and the tools keep executing.

### The Translation Sequence

Callon's four moments of translation provide a surprisingly precise model for human-AI collaboration:

| Moment | In Callon's Scallops | In Human-AI Systems |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Problematization** | Marine biologists define the problem (declining scallop stocks) and position themselves as indispensable | The human operator defines the task and positions the AI system as the necessary mediator |
| **Interessement** | Devices and strategies that lock other actors into the proposed roles (scallop collectors, fishermen's agreements) | System prompts, tool definitions, CLAUDE.md files that constrain the AI's behavior and scope |
| **Enrollment** | Actors accept and perform their assigned roles (scallops anchor to collectors, fishermen refrain from harvesting) | The model generates outputs consistent with its role; tools execute as specified; files conform to schemas |
| **Mobilization** | The researchers speak for the scallops, the fishermen, and the scientific community | The AI produces summaries, commits, reports that represent the network's collective work to external audiences |

Translation can fail at any moment. If scallops refuse to anchor, the network collapses. If the model hallucinates, if a tool returns an error, if the human rejects the output --- the network must be re-negotiated.

## Human-Nonhuman Symmetry

### The Symmetry Principle and Its Provocation

ANT's most controversial claim is the "principle of generalized symmetry": human and nonhuman entities must be analyzed using the same conceptual framework. This does not mean humans and scallops are "the same." It means the analyst must not invoke different kinds of explanation for different kinds of entity. You cannot say the scientist "had a theory" while the scallop merely "behaved according to its nature." Both must be described in terms of what they *do* within the network.

Applied to AI:
- The human operator does not "think" while the model merely "computes." Both perform translations.
- The human does not "decide" while the model merely "generates." Both are enrolled in the network through specific mechanisms (salary, system prompts).
- The human does not "create" while the model merely "recombines." Both produce outputs that cannot be fully predicted from inputs.

### Why This Matters for AI

The symmetry principle has three consequences for understanding human-AI systems:

**1. It dissolves the tool/agent binary.** In the standard framing, AI is either a "tool" (passive, controlled) or an "agent" (autonomous, potentially dangerous). ANT replaces this binary with a spectrum of mediating actants whose degree of transformation varies.

**2. It distributes accountability.** If agency is distributed across the network, then neither the human nor the AI is solely responsible for outcomes. This is not a license for irresponsibility --- it is a demand for more precise descriptions of how responsibility is distributed.

**3. It reframes alignment.** The "alignment problem" assumes a fixed human subject whose values must be instilled in an AI object. ANT suggests that both human and AI are constituted by their interactions. Alignment is not a property of the model; it is a relational achievement of the network.

### Haraway's Supplement: Situated Knowledges

Donna Haraway complicates ANT's symmetry with the concept of situated knowledge. Symmetry does not mean all positions are equivalent. The human and the AI occupy different positions with different partialities, different blindnesses, different stakes. Haraway insists on accountability: who benefits from the network? Whose labor is rendered invisible? The symmetry principle must be held in tension with the question of justice.

## From Latour to AI Systems

### ANT Concepts Mapped to Multi-Agent Architectures

| ANT Concept | AI System Analog | Implications |
|-------------|------------------|--------------|
| **Actant** | Any entity that acts: human operator, AI model, tool, file, schema, API endpoint, network connection | The analyst must trace what each actant *does*, not what it "is" |
| **Network** | The total assemblage of human, model, tools, context, infrastructure that produces a given output | Networks are transient; each session constructs a new one |
| **Translation** | The process by which diverse elements (user intent, model capabilities, tool APIs, file contents) are aligned into a coherent action | Every prompt-response cycle is a translation event |
| **Obligatory passage point** | The MCP protocol layer, the system prompt, the orchestrating agent | Controls what can happen; defines the action program |
| **Blackboxing** | The model's internal weights, the tool's implementation, the infrastructure stack | Functioning systems hide their complexity; failures reveal it |
| **Punctualization** | When the whole system appears as a single "assistant" from the user's perspective | Breaks down when the model hallucinates, a tool fails, or context is exceeded |
| **Immutable mobile** | CLAUDE.md, seed.yaml, API schemas, tool definitions, prompt templates | Inscriptions that travel across contexts while maintaining prescriptive force |
| **Boundary object** | Shared artifacts (code files, configuration, documentation) interpreted differently by human and AI | Both must "read" the same file, but they construct different meanings from it |
| **Intermediary** | A simple pass-through tool (file reader, echo command) | Transports without transforming; can be safely ignored in analysis |
| **Mediator** | The AI model itself; any tool that transforms its input | Cannot be predicted from inputs; must be studied as an active participant |
| **Enrollment** | The process of configuring model behavior (system prompts, tool permissions, context files) | The model must be *enrolled* in the network through specific practices |
| **Quasi-object** | The AI's output --- neither fully the human's work nor fully the model's | Its status as "authored" or "generated" shifts depending on context |

### The ORGANVM System as Actor-Network

The ORGANVM eight-organ system described in this workspace provides a concrete case study. Consider the network:

- **Human actants:** The sole operator directing the system
- **AI actants:** Claude Code, MCP servers, automation scripts
- **Textual actants:** seed.yaml contracts, registry-v2.json, CLAUDE.md files, governance-rules.json
- **Institutional actants:** GitHub organizations, CI/CD pipelines, promotion state machine
- **Conceptual actants:** The eight-organ model, the AI-conductor metaphor, the dependency flow rules

The governance-rules.json and validate-deps.py are *obligatory passage points* --- all promotion events must pass through them. The seed.yaml files are *immutable mobiles* --- they travel with each repo, maintaining their prescriptive force regardless of context. The registry-v2.json is a *center of calculation* (Latour's term for places where inscriptions are accumulated and combined).

The system's explicit commitment to "human directs, AI generates volume, human reviews" is a *translation script* --- it defines the enrollment of each actant and the conditions under which mobilization (publication, deployment) occurs.

### Tool Use as Translation

When an AI agent invokes a tool (reading a file, executing a command, calling an API), it is performing translation in Callon's precise sense:

1. **Problematization:** The agent identifies a gap in its knowledge or capability and determines that a specific tool can address it.
2. **Interessement:** The agent formulates the tool invocation (parameters, format) to lock the tool into the required role.
3. **Enrollment:** The tool executes and returns results that the agent incorporates into its network of understanding.
4. **Mobilization:** The agent speaks for the tool's results, re-presenting them to the human operator as part of a coherent narrative.

Each step can fail. The tool might not exist (problematization failure). The parameters might be wrong (interessement failure). The tool might error (enrollment failure). The agent might misinterpret results (mobilization failure).

## Critical Perspectives

### 1. The Symmetry Problem Intensifies

ANT's symmetry principle was controversial when applied to scallops and door-closers. Applied to AI, the stakes escalate. Critics (notably Langdon Winner) argue that intentionality fundamentally distinguishes humans from things. ANT scholars respond that they do not attribute intentionality to nonhumans --- they locate agency in *heterogeneous associations*. But AI systems behave *as if* they have intentions, goals, and preferences. Does this collapse the distinction ANT was trying to make, or vindicate it?

### 2. The Blackboxing of Blackboxing

AI systems are blackboxed at multiple levels: model architecture, training data, fine-tuning decisions, infrastructure. ANT's vocabulary of blackboxing was developed for systems whose internals *could in principle* be opened and inspected (Pasteur's lab, a copying machine). Can it handle systems whose internals are *constitutively opaque* --- where even the designers cannot fully explain the model's behavior?

### 3. Scale and Computation

ANT was developed through qualitative case studies. Its method is intensive description: follow the actors, trace the translations. AI systems operate at scales that resist this method --- billions of parameters, trillions of training tokens, millions of users. Can ANT's artisanal ethnography scale to industrial AI?

### 4. The Political Vacuum

Whittle and Spicer's critique: ANT is "ill-suited to the task of developing political alternatives." It describes networks but does not adjudicate between them. Applied to AI: ANT can describe how human-AI networks form and stabilize, but it cannot tell us whether these networks are *just*. The concentration of AI capability in a few corporations, the extraction of training data from unconsenting populations, the displacement of human labor --- these require normative frameworks that ANT deliberately avoids.

### 5. Lucy Suchman's Corrective

Suchman's *Plans and Situated Actions* (1987) showed that people do not follow plans; they improvise in context. Her later work on military AI and autonomous weapons extends this insight: the gap between formal system descriptions and situated use is where both creativity and catastrophe live. For human-AI systems, Suchman's work warns against confusing the formal model of interaction (system prompt, tool schema, protocol specification) with the actual practice.

### 6. Barad vs. Latour on Relations

Barad's agential realism pushes further than ANT: entities do not exist *before* their relations (as ANT sometimes implies) but are *constituted by* them. This matters for AI because it suggests that "the model" and "the user" are not fixed entities that enter into a network. They are produced *by* the network. Each interaction creates a new human-AI assemblage that did not exist before.

### 7. The After-ANT Question

Some scholars (Annemarie Mol, John Law, Helen Verran) work in what they call "after-ANT" --- successor projects that retain ANT's relational commitments but modify its vocabulary and methods. Mol's emphasis on multiplicity (the same disease is different in the clinic, the lab, and the patient's life) and Law's work on "mess" (the irreducibility of social reality to clean networks) both complicate the neat application of ANT to computational systems.

## Open Questions

1. **Is an AI model an actant, a network, or both?** In ANT's terms, a working model is punctualized --- it appears as a single entity. But it is also a vast network (training data, architecture, weights, infrastructure). When does the analyst treat it as one or the other? The answer matters for accountability: holding "the model" responsible is different from holding "the network that produced the model" responsible.

2. **What counts as "translation failure" in AI systems?** Hallucination? Misalignment? Refusal? Each represents a different kind of breakdown in the translation sequence. ANT provides vocabulary (depunctualization, the opening of the black box) but not criteria for evaluation.

3. **Can ANT handle recursive agency?** In a human-AI system where the AI generates code that modifies its own tools, the network becomes self-modifying. ANT was built to describe networks that are constantly re-made, but self-modifying networks present a new challenge: the actants *reshape the conditions of their own enrollment*.

4. **Where is the body?** Latour and Haraway both insist on materiality. AI systems are materially instantiated (GPUs, data centers, energy grids) but their material substrate is typically invisible to the user. How does ANT's material-semiotic method handle a system whose materiality is systematically hidden?

5. **Is "alignment" a translation problem?** If alignment means getting the AI to act in accordance with human values, then in ANT terms it is a problem of *enrollment* --- getting an actant to perform its assigned role. But enrollment is always precarious, always requires ongoing work. Does this mean alignment is never "solved" but only continuously maintained?

6. **What would a "parliament of things" look like for AI governance?** Latour proposed that political institutions should represent nonhuman entities alongside humans. If AI systems are actants, should they be represented in governance structures? What would this mean in practice --- and how would it differ from the current model where corporations represent their AI systems?

7. **How does ANT's description of scientific knowledge production apply to AI-generated knowledge?** Latour showed that scientific facts are constructed through networks of allies --- instruments, citations, institutions. AI-generated text is also constructed through networks --- training data, model architecture, prompt engineering. Is the epistemological status of AI-generated knowledge analogous to scientific knowledge in Latour's sense?
