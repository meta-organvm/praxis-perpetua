# The Metaphysics of Flux: A Unified Framework for Perpetual Transformation

The principle that **"everything is change"** represents not merely a philosophical position but a convergent recognition across physics, cosmology, philosophy, systems theory, and performance practice. For an Omni-Performative Engine grounded in perpetual transformation, this convergence provides both theoretical legitimacy and practical vocabulary. Reality, according to this framework, consists not of substances that undergo change but of processes that constitute what we mistakenly perceive as stable things.

This report synthesizes findings across five major domains to establish that transformation and relationality are not incidental features of existence but its fundamental nature—and demonstrates how this understanding can ground a multimedia performance system where audience, performer, environment, and technology exist in constant mutual constitution.

---

## Physics reveals a universe without fixed substances

Modern physics has systematically dissolved the notion of matter as stable substance, revealing instead a world of dynamic fields, relational properties, and emergent order arising from flux.

**Quantum field theory provides the most radical reformulation of physical reality.** Cambridge physicist David Tong states the essential insight: "The fundamental building blocks of Nature are not discrete particles at all. Instead they are continuous fluid-like substances, spread throughout all of space. We call these objects fields." Every particle—every electron in the human body, every photon of light—is a "tiny ripple of the underlying field." The vacuum itself is not emptiness but dynamic activity: it "froths and boils, a bubbling soup of particles and anti-particles, constantly created and destroyed." This means that even apparent stability conceals continuous transformation; what we call "particles" are momentary patterns in perpetually fluctuating fields.

**Carlo Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics extends this dissolution to all physical properties.** In Rovelli's formulation, properties exist only in relation: "The state of a quantum system is the relation between the observer and the system." There is no observer-independent state of affairs. A particle's position, momentum, or spin exists only relative to another system interacting with it—echoing how a performance exists only in the encounter between performer, audience, and environment. Rovelli explicitly links this to process philosophy: reality consists of events and relations, not substances with inherent attributes.

**Ilya Prigogine's work on dissipative structures demonstrates that order emerges from flux.** His Nobel Prize-winning research showed that systems far from equilibrium—continuously exchanging energy with their environment—spontaneously generate complex, organized structures. A living cell, a hurricane, a performance system: all maintain coherence precisely through continuous material exchange. "To escape the tyranny of equilibrium," Prigogine wrote, "dissipation must exceed some threshold so that matter acquires these self-organizing capabilities that we call life." Structure does not resist change but arises through it.

**Complexity theory reveals creativity at the edge of chaos.** Stuart Kauffman's research at the Santa Fe Institute demonstrated that complex systems evolve toward a transition zone between rigid order and chaotic disorder—the "edge of chaos"—where emergence and adaptation flourish. Christopher Langton found that systems at this boundary exhibit maximum computational complexity and can support information processing impossible in either purely ordered or purely random regimes. This provides a rigorous framework for understanding how performances can be simultaneously structured and spontaneous: the most generative creative space lies at the boundary, not in the extremes.

**Quantum entanglement reveals interconnection as fundamental.** Bell's theorem (1964) proved that quantum mechanics requires non-local correlations: particles once interacting remain correlated regardless of separation, exhibiting relationships that cannot be explained by any theory treating them as independently existing things. This challenges substance metaphysics at its root—the universe cannot be decomposed into separately existing parts.

## Western process philosophy articulates becoming over being

The Western philosophical tradition contains a powerful counter-current to substance metaphysics, one that affirms change as primary and being as derivative.

**Heraclitus (c. 535-475 BCE) provides the foundational statement.** His river fragments articulate identity-through-change: "On those stepping into rivers staying the same, other and other waters flow." Crucially, the river remains the same *precisely because* its waters change. As the Stanford Encyclopedia notes, "We call a body of water a river precisely because it consists of changing waters; if the waters should cease to flow it would not be a river, but a lake or a dry streambed." Heraclitus's fire serves as cosmic metaphor—not merely an element but the transformative process itself, "ever-living fire, kindling in measures and being quenched in measures." The *logos* is not static truth but the pattern of transformation that unifies opposites.

**Alfred North Whitehead's process philosophy (1929) provides systematic metaphysical grounding.** Whitehead replaces substances with "actual occasions"—moments of experience that perish and are succeeded by new occasions that incorporate them. His principle of process states that "how an actual entity becomes constitutes what that actual entity is... Its 'being' is constituted by its 'becoming.'" Every actual occasion "prehends" (feels, appropriates) the entire past universe, making relationality ontologically fundamental. Whitehead's critique of "the fallacy of misplaced concreteness"—treating abstractions as if they were concrete realities—directly applies to performance: treating a "work" as fixed object rather than living event commits this fundamental error.

**Henri Bergson's concept of *durée* (duration) distinguishes lived time from spatialized abstraction.** Duration is "the continuous progress of the past which gnaws into the future and which swells as it advances." Scientific clock-time falsely imposes spatial concepts on temporal flow; genuine experience is qualitative, heterogeneous, and indivisible. Bergson's *élan vital* (vital impulse) drives creative evolution without predetermined goals—"Like a river that carves new pathways as it flows, life invents its own future." Bergson's method of intuition grasps what conceptual analysis cannot: the continuous flowing that concepts inevitably freeze into static snapshots.

**Gilles Deleuze radicalizes these insights through the concept of difference-in-itself.** Traditional philosophy derives difference from prior identity (x differs from y). Deleuze inverts this: identity is produced by prior relations between differentials. "Becoming is the pure movement evident in changes between particular events... Rather than a product, final or interim, becoming is the very dynamism of change." His rhizome concept (with Félix Guattari) describes systems with "no beginning or end"—any point can connect to any other, creating non-hierarchical networks of transformation. This offers a model for performance systems without fixed centers or predetermined paths.

## Buddhist philosophy and Indigenous cosmologies provide non-Western foundations

The insight that reality is processual and relational is not uniquely Western but appears across diverse philosophical traditions.

**Buddhist philosophy centers on three marks of existence: impermanence (*anicca*), suffering (*dukkha*), and non-self (*anattā*).** Everything conditioned is transient—nothing stays the same "even for a millisecond." The doctrine of dependent origination (*pratītyasamutpāda*) states: "If this exists, that exists; if this ceases to exist, that also ceases to exist." Nothing exists independently; all phenomena arise through interrelated causes. Thich Nhat Hanh's modern term "interbeing" captures this mutual constitution.

**Nāgārjuna's Madhyamaka (Middle Way) philosophy deepens this analysis through the concept of emptiness (*śūnyatā*).** Emptiness does not mean nonexistence but that things lack *svabhāva* (inherent existence, essence, substance). "Whatever arises dependently is as such empty," Nāgārjuna wrote. Even emptiness is empty—it is not a hidden substance behind appearances but the recognition that appearances have no hidden substance. The parallels to Whitehead are striking: both reject substance metaphysics without falling into nihilism; both propose relational, interdependent existence as the middle way.

**Indigenous relational ontologies ground knowing in kinship and reciprocity.** Robin Wall Kimmerer (Citizen Potawatomi Nation) articulates Traditional Ecological Knowledge as "a way of knowing" based on relationship rather than extraction. "When we see and experience plants as beings, it changes our relationship to plants, to nature." Indigenous languages often recognize land and species as kin through animate grammar—pronouns mark relationships rather than merely labeling objects. African Ubuntu philosophy expresses similar insight: "A person is a person through other people" (*Umntu ngumntu ngabantu*). Human-nature unity, three-dimensional relationships (living dead, the living, yet-to-be-born), and emphasis on harmony characterize these ontologies.

**The convergence is significant**: across millennia and continents, diverse traditions recognize that reality is constituted by processes and relations rather than independent substances.

## Systems theory provides frameworks for dynamic interconnection

Cybernetics and systems theory developed rigorous frameworks for understanding circular causality, self-organization, and emergence—providing both conceptual vocabulary and practical tools for performance systems.

**Norbert Wiener's cybernetics (1948) introduced feedback as a fundamental principle.** Unlike linear causation (A→B→C), feedback loops create self-regulating systems where effects influence subsequent causes. Negative feedback maintains stability through counteraction; positive feedback amplifies change. The helmsman steering a ship—continuously adjusting based on deviation from course—provided Wiener's foundational metaphor. For performance, this dissolves the notion of fixed transmission from sender to receiver: performers and audiences form feedback loops where each response modifies subsequent action.

**Second-order cybernetics incorporates the observer into the observed system.** Heinz von Foerster distinguished "the cybernetics of observed systems" (first-order) from "the cybernetics of observing systems" (second-order). "Objectivity is the delusion that observations could be made without an observer." Every observation is itself an action that shapes what is observed. This directly supports participatory performance: there is no "objective" performance independent of its observers; meaning emerges through recursive interaction. Von Foerster's ethical imperative—"Act always so as to increase the number of choices"—suggests design principles for open systems.

**Autopoiesis (Maturana and Varela, 1972) explains how systems maintain identity through continuous change.** An autopoietic system is "a network of inter-related component-producing processes such that the components in interaction generate the same network that produced them." The cell paradigmatically illustrates this: chemical reactions bounded by a membrane that those same reactions produce and maintain. Identity is organizational pattern, not material substance—which is continuously exchanged with the environment through "structural coupling." Varela's later development into enactivism extends this: cognition is not information processing but "the bringing forth of an interdependent world in and through embodied action."

**Chaos theory reveals how deterministic systems generate genuine novelty.** Edward Lorenz discovered (1963) that simple nonlinear equations produce apparently random behavior—the famous "butterfly effect" of sensitive dependence on initial conditions. Strange attractors (Ruelle and Takens, 1971) capture trajectories that are simultaneously bounded and never-repeating. This provides rigorous grounding for understanding how performance systems can be rule-governed yet inexhaustible: following the same rules never produces identical outcomes.

## Performance practice has operationalized these principles

Avant-garde performance has developed practical techniques embodying perpetual transformation—providing both precedent and method for the Omni-Performative Engine.

**John Cage dissolved boundaries through indeterminacy and chance operations.** His use of I Ching hexagrams to make compositional decisions removed personal taste from the creative process. *Music of Changes* (1951) used charts where 32 of 64 fields were silences; *4'33"* (1952) made environmental sound the composition itself. "There's no such thing as silence," Cage observed. "What they thought was silence, because they didn't know how to listen, was full of accidental sounds." Influenced by Zen Buddhism through D.T. Suzuki, Cage understood composition as discipline comparable to meditation: "Quiet mind requires ego- and thought-free attentiveness to environment." The result dissolves composer/performer/listener boundaries: "The performance should make clear to the listener that the hearing of the piece is his own action."

**Allan Kaprow's Happenings blurred art and life.** "The line between art and life should be kept as fluid, and perhaps indistinct, as possible." His rules for Happenings explicitly embrace non-repeatability: "Perform the happening once only. Repeating it makes it stale." *Fluids* (1967)—building walls of ice that melted—literally embodied impermanence. Kaprow's trajectory moved from gallery to street to everyday life, eventually proposing "lifetime Happenings" where art becomes indistinguishable from living.

**Augusto Boal's Theatre of the Oppressed transforms spectators into "spect-actors."** In Forum Theatre, audience members stop the action and intervene, replacing the protagonist to enact alternative solutions. The form originated when a woman, frustrated with an actor's failure to understand her suggestion, came onstage and showed what she meant—"the birth of the spect-actor." Every performance differs because spect-actors determine outcomes. Boal called this "rehearsal theater"—practicing responses to oppression that transfer to real action. "What looks like destiny turns out to be a problem with different possible outcomes."

**Nicolas Bourriaud's relational aesthetics defines art as social encounter.** "The role of artworks is no longer to form imaginary and utopian realities, but to actually be ways of living and models of action." Rirkrit Tiravanija cooking pad thai in a gallery (*Untitled (Free)*, 1992) creates communal space; the work exists only in the moment of social relation. Claire Bishop's critique—that such relations are "not intrinsically democratic"—introduces productive tension: genuine democracy requires sustained conflict, not just convivial gathering.

**Improvisation theory provides principles for real-time emergence.** Keith Johnstone's work on spontaneity identifies the "internal censor" that blocks creative response; his techniques ("Be obvious," "Accept offers") train performers to respond rather than plan. Free jazz—Ornette Coleman's *Free Jazz: A Collective Improvisation* (1960)—demonstrated non-hierarchical collective composition. Derek Bailey distinguished "idiomatic" improvisation (within a style) from "non-idiomatic" improvisation with "no stylistic or idiomatic commitment"—characteristics established only by "the sonic musical identity of the person or persons playing it."

**Peggy Phelan articulates performance's ontology of disappearance.** "Performance's only life is in the present. Performance cannot be saved, recorded, documented... Performance becomes itself through disappearance." This is not loss but resistance to commodification: "Performance resists the balanced circulations of finance. It saves nothing; it only spends." Philip Auslander's response complicates the live/mediatized binary, while Rebecca Schneider argues that performance continues and multiplies historically—but all agree that performance fundamentally involves temporal passage rather than spatial persistence.

## Toward a unified conceptual vocabulary

These diverse frameworks converge on several key principles that can ground the Omni-Performative Engine:

**Process primacy**: Reality consists of events, not things. What appears stable is constituted through continuous transformation. Heraclitus's river, Whitehead's actual occasions, Buddhist impermanence, autopoietic systems, and performance's ontology of disappearance all articulate this insight. *Vocabulary*: events, occasions, processes, flows, becoming, emergence.

**Relationality as constitutive**: Properties exist only in relation; entities emerge through connections rather than prior to them. Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics, dependent origination, prehension, structural coupling, and network theory converge here. Nothing has inherent, independent existence; everything is constituted through mutual determination. *Vocabulary*: relations, encounters, mutual constitution, co-arising, entanglement, coupling.

**Circular causality**: Effects loop back to influence causes; observer and observed are mutually implicated. Feedback systems, second-order cybernetics, and participatory performance dissolve linear transmission models. *Vocabulary*: feedback, recursion, reflexivity, participation, co-creation.

**Edge-of-chaos creativity**: Maximum generativity occurs at the boundary between order and chaos—neither fully determined nor purely random. Complexity science provides rigorous grounding; improvisational practice embodies it. *Vocabulary*: emergence, self-organization, constraints-enabling-freedom, bounded openness.

**Identity through difference**: The river remains the same by changing its waters. Autopoiesis, Deleuze's difference-in-itself, and Buddhist emptiness all articulate how identity arises through transformation rather than despite it. *Vocabulary*: differentiation, variation, becoming-other, unfixity.

## Conceptual framework for funders, collaborators, and academics

The Omni-Performative Engine can be articulated across registers appropriate to different audiences:

**For academic contexts**: The system instantiates insights from process metaphysics (Whitehead), relational quantum mechanics (Rovelli), autopoietic theory (Maturana/Varela), and complexity science (Kauffman, Langton) in a performance architecture where transformation and relationality are not incidental but constitutive. Drawing on established avant-garde precedents (Cage, Kaprow, Boal, Bourriaud) while extending them through computational and interactive technologies, the system creates conditions for emergence rather than predetermined outcomes. Theoretical grounding spans physics (quantum field theory, dissipative structures), philosophy (Bergson, Deleuze, Nāgārjuna), and systems theory (cybernetics, network science).

**For funders and practitioners**: The Omni-Performative Engine creates performance environments where every moment is genuinely new—not through randomness but through designed conditions for emergence. Audience, performers, technology, and environment continuously shape each other in real time. This reflects cutting-edge scientific understanding: modern physics reveals a universe of dynamic fields rather than static particles; complexity science shows how rich patterns emerge from simple rules at the "edge of chaos"; systems theory demonstrates how feedback loops create self-organizing systems. The result is performance that cannot be repeated because it is constituted by the unrepeatable moment of encounter—embodying the insight, shared across scientific and contemplative traditions worldwide, that reality is process, not substance.

**For artistic collaborators**: We build systems for transformation. Everything changes—physics knows this, Buddhist philosophy knows this, Indigenous wisdom knows this. Our materials are not objects but processes; our compositions are not fixed works but conditions for emergence. We design feedback architectures where small gestures ripple into large effects, where audiences are always already participants, where the boundary between performer and environment dissolves. The system accepts offers, amplifies differences, and trusts that order emerges from constraint-guided freedom. Following Cage: "Music as affirmation of life—not an attempt to bring order out of chaos." Following Kaprow: keep the line between art and life "as fluid, and perhaps indistinct, as possible."

## Core principles for system design

Based on this theoretical synthesis, the Omni-Performative Engine would incorporate:

- **Feedback architectures**: Multiple nested loops linking performers, audiences, technologies, and environments in continuous mutual adjustment—not transmission from source to receiver but circular causality where every element both shapes and is shaped.

- **Observer-inclusion**: Recognition that there is no external vantage point; all participants are observers whose observations actively construct the performance reality. The system designs for this reflexivity rather than treating it as contamination.

- **Autopoietic dynamics**: The performance maintains coherence through continuous self-production; it is not a fixed structure being executed but an ongoing process maintaining identity through material transformation.

- **Edge-of-chaos parameters**: Operating in the productive zone between rigid determination (dead) and pure randomness (meaningless). Constraints enable rather than prevent emergence; rules create the conditions for genuine novelty.

- **Non-repeatability by design**: Each realization is unique because the system's dynamics make exact repetition impossible. Documentation captures traces, not the work itself—which exists only in the unrepeatable present.

- **Relational encounter as medium**: The work is not object but relation. Following Bourriaud while heeding Bishop's critique: designing for encounters that include productive tension, not just convivial harmony.

This framework provides both rigorous theoretical grounding and practical design principles. The insight that "everything is change" is not mysticism but the convergent recognition of physics, philosophy, systems theory, and contemplative traditions worldwide. The Omni-Performative Engine makes this recognition performatively present—creating conditions where transformation is not represented but enacted, where interconnection is not described but embodied, where the ceaseless flux that constitutes reality becomes artistically manifest.
