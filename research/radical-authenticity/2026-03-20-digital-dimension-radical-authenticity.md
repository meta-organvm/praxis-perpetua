## 1. Authenticity as a Social Media Construct: Key Scholarship

### 1.1 Foundational Theoretical Frameworks

The academic study of digital authenticity rests on several foundational pillars, beginning with Erving Goffman's dramaturgical framework and extending through contemporary platform-specific scholarship.

**Erving Goffman, *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1959)**
The bedrock text. Goffman conceptualizes all social interaction as performance, with individuals managing impressions across "frontstage" (public) and "backstage" (private) regions. His central insight -- that persona is "imputed to individuals despite their own awareness or acceptance of the performance" -- becomes indispensable for understanding digital self-presentation. Social media collapses the frontstage/backstage distinction that Goffman theorized, creating conditions no prior theory anticipated.

**danah boyd and Alice Marwick, "I tweet honestly, I tweet passionately: Twitter users, context collapse, and the imagined audience" (2011)**
- *New Media & Society*, vol. 13, pp. 114--133.
- Introduces two concepts now central to digital authenticity scholarship: **context collapse** (multiple social contexts merging into a single digital environment) and **imagined audience** (users' conception of who will encounter their content).
- Key finding: users are not passive victims of context collapse but "strategic agents who employ various techniques to manage multiple audiences simultaneously, though these strategies inevitably result in compromises." The result is "authenticity averaging" -- versions of self palatable to the broadest possible audience.
- danah boyd traces the coinage of "context collapse" in a 2013 blog post, noting the term emerged from her collaborative work with Marwick studying Twitter practices.

**Nathan Jurgenson, *The Social Photo: On Photography and Social Media* (2019, Verso Books)**
- Jurgenson, co-founder of the Theorizing the Web conference and sociologist at Snap Inc., coined the term "digital dualism" to describe the false belief that the internet constitutes a separate virtual sphere.
- Argues that social media photography has transformed lived experience into performative documentation, creating the "hi-fi selfie" -- content that self-consciously acknowledges its own mediation.
- Proposes that authenticity in social media might be better understood as "honest acknowledgment of the filter" rather than unfiltered expression. If the distinction between authentic and inauthentic collapses through recognition that all presentation is filtered, then authenticity becomes primarily a question of whether that performance is consciously acknowledged or strategically concealed.
- Observes that people increasingly remember life events through the lens of how they posted about them rather than how they actually experienced them.

### 1.2 The Authenticity Industries

**Michael Serazio, *The Authenticity Industries: Keeping It "Real" in Media, Culture, and Politics* (2023, Stanford University Press)**
- Winner of the 2025 Erving Goffman Award for Outstanding Scholarship in the Ecology of Social Interaction.
- Argues that authenticity is "nothing short of the central moral framework of our time."
- Based on dozens of interviews with campaign consultants, advertising executives, tech leadership, and entertainment gatekeepers.
- Traces how authenticity has been transformed from a philosophical concept into a commercial commodity systematically manufactured across reality television, music, social media, and politics.
- Central paradox: "the very industries claiming to value and promote authenticity are themselves engaged in elaborate processes of constructing and controlling what appears authentic."
- Key review: Ellie Homant, *Journalism & Mass Communication Quarterly* (2025). doi:10.1177/15274764241287497.

**Emily Hund, *The Influencer Industry: The Quest for Authenticity on Social Media* (2023, Princeton University Press)**
- Hund is a research affiliate at the Center on Digital Culture and Society, University of Pennsylvania's Annenberg School for Communication.
- Based on eight years of research, analysis of 2,000 press articles, participant observation at industry events, and dozens of in-depth interviews.
- Reveals that "the people we think of as 'real' are merely those who have learned to exploit the industry's ever-shifting constructions of authenticity."
- Argues "authenticity" emerged as a metric to distinguish between influencers with comparable quantitative metrics, reflecting "a growing sense that platform metrics cannot be relied upon to measure influence."

### 1.3 Empirical Research on Performed Authenticity

**Bailey et al., "Quantified Authenticity and Well-Being" (2020)**
- *PNAS* / PMC: PMC7538578
- Used computational methods comparing users' self-reported personality traits with algorithmic personality judgments from Facebook likes and status updates.
- Finding: authentic self-expression on social media is correlated with greater life satisfaction and subjective well-being.
- Experimental arm: participants randomly assigned to post authentically vs. in a self-idealized manner reported higher positive affect during authentic weeks.
- Caveat: effects on long-term cognitive measures of life satisfaction were less pronounced.

**Social media exposure and self-presentation desire (2025)**
- PMC: PMC12255000
- Finding: increased social media exposure triggers upward social comparison and FOMO, which motivate self-idealized presentation rather than authentic expression.
- Moderated by network type: friend-dominated networks vs. stranger-dominated networks produce different self-presentation patterns.
- Implication: authenticity and self-idealization are dynamic responses to specific social environments, not stable traits.

**Cross-cultural Instagram authenticity study (2025)**
- *Journal of Computer-Mediated Communication*, vol. 30, no. 4, zmaf008.
- Examined authenticity perceptions across 15 Instagram image genres and multiple cultural groups.
- Highest authenticity ratings: cancer diaries, coffee/cake images, selfies with dogs, family vacations, graduation portraits.
- Lowest authenticity ratings: gym mirror selfies, green smoothies, nail art, perfume advertisements, travel influencer content.
- Italian respondents systematically judged images as less authentic; Korean respondents gave higher ratings to typically low-authenticity genres.
- Key implication: authenticity is not an objective property of content but a culturally constructed judgment.

**Audrezet, de Kerviler, and Guidry Moulard, "Authenticity management strategies of social media influencers" (2020)**
- *Journal of Business Research*, vol. 117, pp. 557--569.
- Identifies two primary strategies: **passionate authenticity** (emphasizing intrinsic motivation and genuine enthusiasm) and **transparent authenticity** (prioritizing clarity about commercial relationships).
- Finding: these strategies pull in opposite directions -- clear FTC-compliant disclosure enhances transparent authenticity but undermines passionate authenticity.

**David Kasch, "The Curated Self and the Commodified Self" (dissertation)**
- University of California eScholarship.
- Identifies three layers of the "curated self": personal curation (for oneself), social curation (for intimate connections), and spectacle curation (for strangers and potential audiences).
- The "commodified self" represents identity understood as "a resource and object for production, consumption, and distribution."
- Users do not perceive curation as corruption of authentic self but as "a legitimate, necessary, and ongoing practice through which digital identity is continuously constructed."

### 1.4 The Paradox of "Authentic Inauthenticity"

The fundamental paradox animating contemporary scholarship: the very act of attempting to be authentic online inevitably undermines authenticity itself. As one synthesis puts it:

> "The more deliberately one tries to be authentic online, the more performative the authenticity becomes."

This manifests as "Instagram authenticity" -- carefully composed images of apparently messy bedrooms, strategically positioned acne cream, casually composed selfies with perfect lighting. These posts signal authenticity while maintaining aesthetic appeal, threading the needle between genuineness and engagement in ways that purely spontaneous content cannot.

The emergence of AI-generated content has paradoxically intensified this. By 2026, only 26% of consumers express preference for generative AI content (down from 60% in 2023). Brands now explicitly request that creators "avoid over-polished, overmanicured aesthetic presentations and instead embrace imperfections as markers of authenticity." Imperfection becomes strategically curated as an authenticity signal -- the authenticity paradox at its most recursive.

## 2. The "Building in Public" Movement

### 2.1 Origins

The building in public methodology emerged as a recognizable practice in the early 2010s, though its roots extend into open-source software culture and earlier entrepreneurial transparency practices. The term was popularized primarily through social media discourse rather than academic literature.

**Early pioneers (2013--2016):**
- **Buffer** (Joel Gascoigne, founded ~2010): Made all employee salaries publicly visible in 2013, based on a transparent formula accounting for role, experience, and geography. Grew from 17 team members (average salary $100,812) to 75 (average $151,113), demonstrating sustained radical transparency.
- **Product Hunt** (Ryan Hoover): Became one of the first large-scale enablers of public builders, providing infrastructure for sharing projects in development.
- **Indie Hackers** (Courtland Allen, founded 2016): Created community infrastructure for the movement; was itself built in public and acquired by Stripe within 10 months.

**Antecedent figure:** Patrick McKenzie (patio11), who shared earnings online from small projects like appointment reminders for barbershops in the early 2010s. Pieter Levels has cited McKenzie as a primary inspiration: "In 2013--2014, when Pieter entered the startup world, there was barely any indie hacking, there was no bootstrapping, almost, except Patrick McKenzie."

### 2.2 Key Figures

**Pieter Levels** -- The movement's most iconic practitioner. In March 2017, Levels live-coded Hoodmaps from scratch on Twitch while simultaneously posting updates to Twitter. He tweeted on March 31, 2017: "I'm live streaming on @twitch coding a new thing called Hood Maps from zero." Completed the first prototype in 2 hours and 31 minutes. The project went from zero to 300,000+ users. This was his first experience building "100% in public" and demonstrated the complete startup lifecycle in compressed, observable form. Levels was based outside Silicon Valley as a digital nomad, proving geographic irrelevance in the building-in-public model. His earlier project, "12 Startups in 12 Months" (2014), had already established his approach to radical public iteration.

**Arvid Kahl** -- The movement's theorist. Co-founded FeedbackPanda with Danielle Simpson; after selling it, devoted himself to systematizing the practice. Books: *Zero to Sold* (documenting his journey) and *The Embedded Entrepreneur* (frameworks for audience-first business development). Over 65,000 Twitter followers. Crucially, Kahl wrote one of the most self-aware internal critiques of the movement: "The Performative Nature of Building in Public: A View From the Inside" (thebootstrappedfounder.com), in which he acknowledges:

> "It's a performance that I believe to be worthwhile and empowering, but nonetheless artificial and strategic."

And: "It's not you *being* authentic that matters, but the perception of those who *find* you authentic." Authenticity becomes reputation earned through demonstrated consistency, not an essential quality.

**Sahil Lavingia (Gumroad)** -- Key case study in vulnerability and honesty about failure. After KPCB (Kleiner Perkins) offered to sell their stake back for one dollar, Lavingia chose transparency over retreat. Began sharing Gumroad's financial metrics publicly in April 2018, including numbers that revealed the company's modest size. His essay "Reflecting on My Failure to Build a Billion-Dollar Company" (sahillavingia.com) is a foundational text on the relationship between entrepreneurial vulnerability and audience trust.

**Jon Yongfook (Bannerbear)** -- Scaled from zero to $240,000 ARR in under two years through deliberate building in public, positioning the brand around his personal journey.

**Pat Walls (Starter Story)** -- Began as a side project in 2017; grew into a media company eventually acquired by HubSpot Media (2026). Documented every experiment, pivot, and strategic decision publicly.

### 2.3 The Vulnerability Tension

The movement's power derives from its promise of authentic vulnerability. Practitioners argue that sharing failures, revenue setbacks, and emotional struggles establishes trust in ways that polished corporate communication cannot. The logic: if someone will share negative information, audiences infer that positive information must also be credible.

**But the internal critiques are revealing:**

Arvid Kahl, despite being the movement's evangelist, candidly addresses the paradox:
> "When one types updates into a Twitter prompt with awareness of a global audience watching, one is necessarily performing, regardless of stated intentions toward authenticity."

He describes the cognitive dissonance: founders "begin asking themselves 'Is this act of mine an authentic act?' while simultaneously questioning 'Would I do this if I was me?'" -- a formulation that reveals deep alienation.

Simran (web developer/blogger) described the "invisible pressure" of building in public:
> "I spent forty minutes crafting a tweet about a feature that took only ten minutes to code... The more you think about what to share, the less you actually build."

**Hampton Law's structural critique:** The audience interested in watching someone build a product is almost never the audience with money to buy the product. Most engaged followers are other bootstrapped founders -- people building their own projects with limited disposable income. Building in public optimizes for the wrong metrics (engagement, follower growth) rather than the one that matters (customer revenue).

### 2.4 Performed Vulnerability vs. Genuine Vulnerability

Ash Riley distinguishes between vulnerability that represents "a genuine relinquishment of control" and vulnerability "strategically performed to maintain or consolidate power and status." When coaches and founders post "vulnerable" content to demonstrate emotional intelligence or evolved consciousness, they are "using vulnerability as a tool for control rather than relinquishing control."

Applied to building in public: when a founder shares a failure story but frames it as a learning opportunity that made them a better entrepreneur, the narrative ultimately reinforces competence. The failure is presented in a way that invites audiences to admire the founder's resilience and growth -- reputation management disguised as vulnerability.

## 3. Criticism of Vulnerability-as-Brand: Commodification of Authenticity

### 3.1 The Personal Branding Literature

Personal branding scholarship has explicitly embraced vulnerability as strategy. Key claims from the literature:
- "86% of consumers say authenticity is important when deciding what brands they like and support."
- "When we share our vulnerabilities, we activate the part of the brain responsible for empathy in our listeners."
- "That perfect image? It's pushing people away."

The literature teaches individuals to identify, cultivate, package, and deploy vulnerabilities toward professional advantage. Frameworks include: raising self-awareness, needs analysis and positioning, constructing brand architecture, self-reflection and feedback-seeking, and sense-making (Gorbatov et al., "Personal Branding: Interdisciplinary Systematic Review," *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2018, PMC6258780).

**The structural problem:** when visibility and personal brand development become prerequisites for employment -- when individuals must maintain LinkedIn profiles, generate content, and project vulnerability-as-authenticity -- the burden falls disproportionately on those in precarious positions. The personal branding literature frames this as empowerment ("everyone is a brand") but it represents the extension of market logic into the last territories of privacy.

### 3.2 Brene Brown and the Vulnerability Industry

**Brene Brown, "The Power of Vulnerability" (TEDxHouston, 2010)** -- One of the most-viewed TED talks in history. Brown's research on shame resilience positioned vulnerability as essential to connection and courage.

**Criticisms have emerged along several axes:**
- **Privilege critique:** Brown's framing of vulnerability as "a choice" exposes what critics call "a privilege-dependent and white-centric perspective." As one critic asked: "What about the vulnerability of those who do not have a choice in whether their weakness is laid bare before the world?" (Reina Gattuso, *Literary Hub*, "Why Brene Brown's Gospel of Vulnerability Fails the World's Most Vulnerable.")
- **Corporate capture:** Brown's quiet rebrand from "the face of vulnerability and shame resilience" to leadership consultant -- "no longer selling healing in the language of therapy but selling transformation in the language of systems, organizations, and culture" (Chris Hoff, Substack). The *New Yorker* profiled her work under the heading "Vulnerability Inc."
- **Regulatory gap:** Corporate coaching is an unregulated industry. Critics argue that coaches "push people into emotional spaces they are not equipped to handle under the guise of 'leadership development.'"

### 3.3 Mark Fisher and the Impossibility of Authenticity Under Capital

**Mark Fisher, *Capitalist Realism: Is There No Alternative?* (2009, Zero Books)**

Fisher argues capitalism operates not through explicit propaganda but through "the systematic elimination of alternatives to capitalist logic." Key concepts applicable to vulnerability-as-brand:

- **Market Stalinism:** Fisher's term for systems that "value symbols of achievement over actual achievement." Applied to authenticity: the *image* of vulnerability -- the performance of struggling, the narrative of overcoming -- becomes more valuable than actual experience of vulnerability. Someone who documents their mental health journey beautifully on Instagram may be more professionally successful than someone genuinely experiencing the same struggles but unable or unwilling to aestheticize them.

- **The demand for constant hustle:** "Capital demands that we always look busy, even if there's no work to do. If neoliberalism's magical voluntarism is to be believed, there are always opportunities to be chased or created; any time not spent hustling and hassling is time wasted." Applied to confessional content: one must not simply experience growth; one must document and share it. One must not simply recover from trauma; one must narrate that recovery for audiences.

- **Mental health as market effect:** "Capitalist realism insists on treating mental health as if it were a natural fact, like weather (but, then again, weather is no longer a natural fact so much as a political-economic effect)." When individuals share genuine mental health struggles on platforms, those struggles have already been shaped by capitalist conditions, and the decision to share has been shaped by platform economics and algorithmic incentives.

### 3.4 Lauren Berlant and Cruel Optimism

**Lauren Berlant, *Cruel Optimism* (2011, Duke University Press)**
- Winner of the Rene Wellek Prize (American Comparative Literature Association) and the Alan Bray Memorial Book Award (MLA).
- Core concept: "A relation of cruel optimism exists when something you desire is actually an obstacle to your flourishing."
- Describes how people have remained attached to "unachievable fantasies of the good life -- with its promises of upward mobility, job security, political and social equality, and durable intimacy -- despite evidence that liberal-capitalist societies can no longer be counted on to provide opportunities for individuals to make their lives 'add up to something.'"
- Applied to vulnerability-as-brand: the optimistic attachment to the idea that "sharing my authentic story will produce genuine connection and career success" may be precisely the attachment that prevents flourishing, because it requires continuous self-commodification and emotional labor that erodes the very selfhood it promises to liberate.

### 3.5 Sarah Ahmed and the Happiness Imperative

**Sarah Ahmed, *The Promise of Happiness* (2010, Duke University Press)**

- Central argument: "Happiness (or the desire for it) is used as a social control to steer individuals towards specific choices, lifestyles, and realities."
- "Happiness is promised as a reward for certain ways of life (whether that happiness ever arrives or not)."
- Applied to digital confession: the promise that vulnerable self-disclosure will produce happiness -- through community, recognition, healing, or professional success -- becomes a mechanism to direct behavior in profitable directions.
- Ahmed's concept of the **"feminist killjoy"**: the figure who refuses happiness imperatives and questions why certain emotional states are demanded. In the context of vulnerability-as-brand, the killjoy refuses the framework that positions strategic disclosure as empowerment.

## 4. Strategic Vulnerability in Personal Branding

### 4.1 The Concept

"Strategic vulnerability" describes the deliberate, calculated deployment of personal disclosure for professional or commercial advantage. While the exact coinage is difficult to pin to a single source, the concept pervades contemporary personal branding literature and has been examined from multiple angles:

**Ogston, "Vulnerability: The Missing Piece in Your Personal Brand Strategy"** (ogston.com/impact):
- Frames vulnerability as activating empathy in listeners, creating "a shared experience, a moment of 'me too' that forms the foundation of genuine connection."
- Recommends integrating vulnerability into LinkedIn content through sharing "your journey, including the setbacks and learnings" and "balancing insights with personal anecdotes, focusing on lessons learned and growth."

**Gorbatov, Khapova, and Lysova, "Get Noticed to Get Ahead: The Impact of Personal Branding on Career Success" (2018)**
- *Frontiers in Psychology*, vol. 9, article 2152 (PMC6258780).
- Identifies personal branding processes: raising self-awareness, needs analysis and positioning, constructing brand architecture, self-reflection and feedback-seeking.
- Notes that individuals must "identify individual characteristics prior to engaging in positioning of their personal brands to the outer world" -- first determine authentic self, then calculate how to present it to specific audiences.

### 4.2 The Critique from Within

The literature itself reveals the contradiction. When vulnerability is recommended as a "strategy" or "tactic" for audience connection, it has been absorbed into market logic. What was a human need for connection becomes a professional tool. The vulnerability that remains visible under this regime is only vulnerability that has been processed into a redemptive narrative -- struggle that proves something positive about the person.

**The gendered dimension:** Tolentino documents that "publications were mostly publishing women who would get paid $150 to write something really personal." Women face disproportionate expectation to perform vulnerability, to make themselves emotionally available, to commodify their psychological experience. The confessional mode -- emotional vulnerability, trauma narrative, intimate self-disclosure -- became systematically undercompensated precisely because it appeared nonprofessional, voluntary, or therapeutic.

**HBR's framing:** "A New Approach to Building Your Personal Brand" (2023) explicitly positions personal branding as professional necessity: "everyone is a brand, and you need to develop yours and get comfortable marketing it." From an alternative perspective, this represents the extension of market logic into the last territories of privacy and inner life.

## 5. Jia Tolentino's *Trick Mirror* and the Internet Self

### 5.1 Publication and Structure

**Jia Tolentino, *Trick Mirror: Reflections on Self-Delusion* (August 2019, Random House)**

Nine essays. The key essays for this inquiry:
1. **"The I in the Internet"** -- the foundational essay on digital selfhood
2. **"Always Be Optimizing"** -- on the optimization imperative applied to the body and self
3. **"The Story of a Generation in Seven Scams"** -- on the scam as the definitive cultural form of the era

### 5.2 Core Arguments

**The internet as architecture of self-commodification.** Tolentino's central formulation:

> "A world in which selfhood has become capitalism's last natural resource, a world whose terms are set by centralized platforms that have deliberately established themselves as near-impossible to regulate or control."

**The trajectory of digital selfhood.** Tolentino traces her own progression:

> "At ten, I was clicking around a web ring to check out other Angelfire sites full of animal GIFs and Smash Mouth trivia. At twelve, I was writing five hundred words a day on a public LiveJournal. At fifteen, I was uploading photos of myself in a miniskirt on Myspace. By twenty-five, my job was commissioned to make people bitch."

This progression illustrates the transformation from voluntary creative expression to obligatory performance: what began as youthful play became professional necessity.

**Context collapse and the panopticon.** Tolentino argues that the internet introduces "nightmarish metaphorical structures: the mirror, the echo, the panopticon." The mirror creates algorithmic feedback loops of self-reflection. The echo amplifies certain performances while silencing others. The panopticon produces conditions of constant potential observation. Individuals must accommodate "a self that can be viewed simultaneously by their mom, their boss, their potential future bosses, their eleven-year-old nephew, their past and future sex partners, their relatives who loathe their politics, as well as anyone who cares to look for any possible reason."

**The loss of freedom.** A key formulation:

> "Where we had once been free to be ourselves online, we were now chained to ourselves online, and this made us self-conscious."

**Performance as constitutive of self.** Drawing on Goffman, Tolentino emphasizes that the self is "a product of the circumstances we put it in" rather than an essential entity. If selfhood emerges through performance -- if it is "an effect that comes off" rather than a fixed truth -- then the demand for authenticity within performance becomes paradoxical.

**The essay economy and gendered exploitation.** Tolentino documents the systematic underpayment of confessional writing, particularly by women: "Publications were mostly publishing women who would get paid $150 to write something really personal." The demand for authentic vulnerability paired with refusal to adequately compensate it.

**The impossibility of simple refusal.** Tolentino does not advocate simple rejection of digital culture, recognizing that opting out is primarily available to the economically privileged. The question is not whether to participate but how to participate while maintaining integrity.

### 5.3 Critical Reception

- Los Angeles Review of Books: "Identity Shaping on Social Media" (review essay)
- *Slate* review (August 2019): describes the book as "penetrating" on feminism, identity, and the internet
- *Brooklyn Rail* (September 2019): extended critical engagement
- *The White Review*: assessment of Tolentino's arguments about performed selfhood

## 6. Parasocial Relationships and Confessional Content

### 6.1 Theoretical Foundation

**Donald Horton and Richard Wohl, "Mass Communication and Para-social Interaction: Observations on Intimacy at a Distance" (1956)**
- Published in *Psychiatry*, vol. 19, no. 3, pp. 215--229.
- Coined the term "parasocial interaction" to describe one-sided relationships in which audiences feel intimate connection with media personalities despite no reciprocal relationship.
- The foundational insight: media creates conditions for "intimacy at a distance" -- felt closeness without actual reciprocity.

### 6.2 Extension to Social Media

The concept has been substantially updated for the social media era:

**Key distinction:** social media introduces what appears to be reciprocity (commenting, liking, direct messaging, live interaction) but maintains fundamental asymmetry. The parasocial bond intensifies because "social media provides more opportunities for intimate, reciprocal, and frequent interactions between the user and persona."

**Hartmann and Goldhoorn (2011)** established that parasocial interaction "can lead to the development of parasocial relationships that resemble real-world social bonds," with social media amplifying this through the illusion of bidirectional communication.

**The Parasocial Relationships in Social Media Survey** -- developed by researchers at the University of Tennessee's Educational Leadership and Policy Studies department to measure parasocial dynamics specifically in social media contexts, addressing limitations of Horton and Wohl's broadcast-era framework.

**Liebers and Schramm (2019)** published an updated meta-analysis demonstrating that parasocial interaction theory requires substantial revision for social media contexts where the one-to-many broadcast model has been replaced by many-to-many networked communication with asymmetric visibility.

### 6.3 Confessional Content Creators and Their Audiences

**"Trans-parasocial interaction"** -- a term from recent scholarship (BYU Scholars Archive, 2022) describing heightened parasocial intimacy generated by confessional video formats. When creators address cameras as if speaking to friends, they activate mechanisms of felt reciprocity despite the relationship remaining one-sided.

**The exploitation of intimacy:** The content creator benefits through audience engagement, algorithmic amplification, and potential monetization. The audience experiences feeling seen and understood. Both parties believe themselves to be participating in authentic connection while actually participating in a system designed for profit extraction.

**Key finding on persistence of parasocial bonds:** Research (CUNY Academic Works, 2021) found that "influencers were deemed inauthentic by their audiences, yet still had power over a part of their following to stay" engaged. Parasocial relationships persist despite audiences recognizing performance -- indicating the power of vulnerability signals even when perceived as strategic.

**Mental health amplification loops:** "Generation Z shows unprecedented rates of self-diagnosis, often through social media platforms like TikTok. When confession culture combines with algorithmic amplification of mental health content, users can find themselves in feedback loops that reinforce pathological self-perception rather than promoting genuine understanding or healing." (fracturedself.com, "Digital Confession Culture")

### 6.4 The "Authenticity Arms Race"

Digital confession operates according to competitive escalation:

> "An authenticity arms race -- a competitive escalation where individuals must share increasingly intimate details to maintain attention and engagement."

What felt shocking last year becomes mundane. What generated sympathy yesterday generates yawns today. The system creates systematic pressure toward disclosure of increasingly intimate material, particularly damaging for individuals whose psychological struggles genuinely require private processing.

## 7. Digital Self-Disclosure and the Confessional Tradition

### 7.1 The Key Paper

**Norm Friesen, "Confessional Technologies of the Self: From Seneca to Social Media" (2017)**
- *First Monday*, vol. 22, no. 6 (June 5, 2017).
- DOI: https://firstmonday.org/ojs/index.php/fm/article/view/6750
- Friesen, based at Boise State University, traces confessional practices from earliest Western examples through to contemporary digital self-disclosure.
- Uses Foucault's concept of "technologies of the self" -- "reflected and voluntary practices by which men not only fix rules of conduct for themselves but seek to transform themselves" -- as a framework for investigating confessional practices across historical periods.
- **Core argument:** social media disclosure constitutes a "confessional technology of the self" where people use platforms "not only to disclose about themselves, but also to disclose for themselves as a means of finding or reworking their identity."
- Traces a genealogy: Seneca's epistolary self-examination --> Augustine's *Confessions* --> medieval Catholic confession --> Rousseau's secular confessions --> psychoanalytic confession --> digital self-disclosure.

### 7.2 Foucault on Confession

**Michel Foucault, *The History of Sexuality, Volume 1: An Introduction* (1976, English trans. 1978)**

Foucault's analysis of confession as technology of power provides the theoretical backbone:

- Confession is "one of the West's most highly valued techniques for producing truth."
- The modern West is characterized by an "incitement to discourse" -- systematic encouragement to speak about oneself, to articulate inner states, to make public what was once private.
- Confession has been positioned throughout modern history as a path to truth and liberation, yet actually functions as "a mechanism for subjecting populations to observation, knowledge, and control."
- For the Christian confessional specifically, Foucault highlights "a correlation between disclosure of the self, dramatic or verbalized, and the renunciation of the self."
- The "inauguration of penance in the thirteenth century" marks an important step in the rise of dramatic self-disclosure.
- Crucial Foucauldian insight applied to digital platforms: "the insistence on confession produces knowledge -- but knowledge that serves power's interests, not the confessor's."

### 7.3 Does Digital Self-Disclosure Extend or Corrupt the Confessional Tradition?

The scholarship divides on this question:

**The extension argument (Friesen, 2017; Fesshole study, 2024):**
- Digital self-disclosure represents a contemporary reconfiguration of ancient confessional practices, adapted to new media contexts but serving similar functions of identity construction and community bonding.
- A study of @Fesshole on X (ScienceDirect, 2024, doi:10.1016/j.dcm.2024.100849) found that users "establish rapport through confessions about topics of varying degrees of seriousness, seeking to bond in place of absolution and offering inspiration or commonality in place of guilt."
- The confessional function persists but transforms: absolution is replaced by community; guilt is replaced by solidarity; the priest is replaced by the algorithm and the audience.

**The corruption argument (Foucault applied; Tolentino; Fisher):**
- Digital confession fundamentally corrupts the confessional tradition because it eliminates the structural conditions that made confession potentially liberatory:
  - **Privacy:** traditional confession occurred in private (to priest or therapist); digital confession is public, permanent, and algorithmically recirculated.
  - **Bounded authority:** the priest or therapist had specific, limited authority over the confessed material; platforms have unlimited authority to harvest, analyze, and monetize.
  - **Non-commercial relationship:** traditional confession was not designed to generate profit from the confessor's vulnerability; digital confession is structurally oriented toward engagement metrics and data extraction.
  - **Confidentiality:** the seal of confession was absolute; digital "confessions" are permanently recorded, searchable, and weaponizable through screenshots and archives.
- Tolentino's panopticon argument: when one speaks knowing that speech may be observed by parents, employers, and hiring committees, "the conditions for genuine vulnerability collapse."

**The ambivalent position (Jurgenson; contemporary affect theory):**
- Digital confession is neither simple extension nor simple corruption but a fundamentally new formation that cannot be adequately understood through either traditional confessional models or pure critique of commodification.
- The therapeutic benefits are real (empirical evidence shows disclosure of negative events on social media reduces negative affect, stress, and anxiety -- PMC12222736, 2025).
- The exploitation is also real (platforms harvest vulnerability data for behavioral targeting and advertising).
- Both can be true simultaneously. The question is not whether digital confession is authentic or commodified but how individuals navigate a system that is structurally both.

## 8. Synthesis: Implications for Radical Authenticity as Creative Practice

The literature reveals several key tensions that any theory of radical authenticity must address:

### 8.1 The Constitutive Paradox
All authenticity in digital spaces is performed. But not all performance is equally inauthentic. The meaningful distinction is not between authentic and performed but between performance that acknowledges its own performativity (Jurgenson's "honest acknowledgment of the filter") and performance that denies it.

### 8.2 The Commodification Trap
Any authenticity that gains visibility within platform architectures has already been captured by commercial logic (Serazio, Fisher). But refusing visibility entirely is available primarily to the privileged (Tolentino). Radical authenticity must therefore navigate within commercial systems while maintaining critical consciousness about those systems.

### 8.3 The Confessional Transformation
Digital self-disclosure follows the confessional tradition in structure but corrupts it in three ways: it eliminates privacy, it serves commercial rather than spiritual/therapeutic ends, and it produces permanently recorded material that can be weaponized (Foucault applied via Friesen). Any radical authenticity practice must account for these structural differences.

### 8.4 The Vulnerability Asymmetry
Strategic vulnerability reinforces existing power structures (Ahmed, Brown critique). The demand to perform vulnerability falls disproportionately on women and marginalized populations (Tolentino). Radical authenticity as creative practice must attend to who bears the costs of disclosure.

### 8.5 The Parasocial Problem
Confessional content creates one-sided bonds that feel mutual but are not (Horton and Wohl, updated). The authenticity of the creator's disclosure does not produce authentic relationship with audiences. Any practice of radical authenticity must be honest about this asymmetry rather than exploiting it.

### 8.6 The Building-in-Public Model
The most self-aware practitioners (Kahl, Lavingia) acknowledge that building in public is "artificial and strategic" even when genuine. This acknowledgment itself may be a form of meta-authenticity -- authenticity about the impossibility of authenticity -- that points toward a productive model for radical creative practice.
