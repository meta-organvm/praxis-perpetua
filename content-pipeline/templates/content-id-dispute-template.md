# Content ID Dispute Template -- Object Lessons

Reusable template for disputing YouTube Content ID claims on Object Lesson episodes. The channel uses film clips under fair use (17 U.S.C. Section 107) for transformative commentary and critical analysis.

---

## Pre-Upload Checklist

Complete every item before publishing. Prevention is cheaper than dispute.

### Clip Discipline

- [ ] **Individual clips kept under 10 seconds** where analytical point permits. Shorter is safer -- 3-7 seconds is ideal for Content ID avoidance.
- [ ] **No continuous clip playback.** Every clip is interrupted by narration, text overlay, or a cut to a different source. Never play more than one clip from the same film back-to-back without narration between them.
- [ ] **Audio stripped or replaced on all film clips.** Use ETCETER4 original scoring throughout -- never use the film's soundtrack, score, or licensed music. Music labels are often MORE aggressive than film studios on Content ID.
- [ ] **No music from film soundtracks.** Even brief use of a recognizable score (Hans Zimmer, John Williams, Ennio Morricone) will trigger claims from the music rights holder, not the studio. This is the most common Content ID trigger for video essays.
- [ ] **Dialogue isolated from score.** If a clip includes essential dialogue (e.g., Sharon Stone's "What are you gonna do? Arrest me?" in Cigarettes cold open), strip the background music and keep only the dialogue track. If they can't be separated, use a text overlay with the quote instead.
- [ ] **Clips visually modified where possible.** Cropping, zooming, picture-in-picture, side-by-side comparisons, and text overlays all strengthen the transformative argument and reduce Content ID fingerprint matches.

### Upload Strategy

- [ ] **Upload as Unlisted first.** Do not publish immediately.
- [ ] **Wait 24-48 hours** for Content ID claims to surface. Most automated claims appear within the first 24 hours; some take up to 72.
- [ ] **Screenshot all claims before disputing.** Document: claimant name, claim type (audio/visual), claimed duration, content policy (monetize/block/track). These screenshots are evidence if disputes escalate.
- [ ] **Check claim details.** Expand each claim to see the exact timestamp range flagged. This tells you which clip triggered it and whether the match is accurate or a false positive.
- [ ] **Dispute all claims simultaneously** if multiple claims appear. Don't wait for one dispute to resolve before filing the next -- the 30-day counter runs independently for each.

### Pre-Dispute Documentation

- [ ] **Note each clip's purpose** in the episode. Write a one-sentence explanation of the analytical point the clip illustrates. This becomes the FACTOR 1 argument.
- [ ] **Calculate clip percentage.** For each claimed clip: [clip duration in seconds] / [full film runtime in seconds] * 100 = percentage of original work used. This is the FACTOR 3 argument.
- [ ] **Verify no market substitution.** Confirm the video does not allow the viewer to watch the film instead of purchasing/renting it. A 5-second clip with narration overlay does not substitute for viewing the full film.

## Dispute Letter Template

Copy, fill in bracketed fields, paste into YouTube's Content ID dispute form. One dispute per claim.

```
This video constitutes fair use under 17 U.S.C. Section 107.

FACTOR 1 (Purpose and Character): This video is a transformative
work of criticism, commentary, and education. The clip from
[FILM TITLE] ([YEAR]) is used to illustrate [SPECIFIC ANALYTICAL
POINT -- e.g., "how directors use milk as a symbol of corrupted
innocence in post-1970s cinema"]. The clip is accompanied by
original narration analyzing [WHAT THE NARRATION SAYS ABOUT THIS
CLIP -- e.g., "how Kubrick inverted milk's traditional associations
with purity in the Korova Milk Bar sequence"]. The clip is not
used for entertainment but as evidence supporting the video's
thesis about [THESIS -- e.g., "the evolution of milk as a loaded
cinematic symbol across a century of filmmaking"]. The work adds
new meaning, expression, and message to the original material
through critical analysis.

FACTOR 2 (Nature of the Work): [FILM TITLE] is a published,
creative work. While creative works receive stronger copyright
protection, fair use explicitly permits criticism and commentary
on such works.

FACTOR 3 (Amount Used): Approximately [X] seconds of a [RUNTIME
-- e.g., "163-minute"] film ([X]% of the total work). Only the
minimum amount necessary to support the analytical point is used.
The clip is not played in its entirety and is interrupted by
original narration and commentary.

FACTOR 4 (Market Effect): This video does not substitute for
viewing [FILM TITLE]. It is a critical analysis that discusses
the film's use of [OBJECT -- e.g., "milk as a visual motif"] in
the context of a broader cinematic survey spanning multiple films
and decades. The video may increase interest in the original work
by drawing attention to its craft and thematic depth.
```

### Dispute Variations by Claim Type

**Audio-only claim (film score or soundtrack):**

```
The claimed audio is incidental to a visual clip used for
transformative critical commentary. The audio is not the subject
of the video; it accompanies a brief visual excerpt analyzed in
the narration. The video's audio track is primarily original music
(ETCETER4) and original narration. No portion of the claimed audio
is used for its entertainment value or as a substitute for
listening to the original soundtrack.
```

**Visual match on a very short clip (under 5 seconds):**

```
The claimed visual content constitutes approximately [X] seconds
of footage used in a montage of [NUMBER] films spanning [YEAR
RANGE] to illustrate a critical argument about [OBJECT]'s role
in cinema history. The clip is not presented for entertainment
value but as one data point in a scholarly comparison across
multiple works.
```

## Studio Risk Assessment

Based on documented Content ID enforcement patterns in the video essay community. Severity reflects how aggressively the rights holder pursues claims and how likely they are to reject disputes.

### HIGH RISK -- Expect claims, prepare disputes in advance

| Rights Holder | Notes |
|---------------|-------|
| **Disney / 20th Century Studios / Fox** | Most aggressive. Claims on clips as short as 2-3 seconds. Disputes are frequently rejected at first pass. Disney-owned Alien (1979), Star Wars, Marvel, and all Fox catalog (Fight Club, Cleopatra, Sunrise) fall here. |
| **Warner Bros. / Warner Discovery** | Aggressive on both visual and audio. Owns A Clockwork Orange, The Shining, The Matrix, Contact, Joker, Harry Potter, Casablanca, Blade Runner. WB music publishing is separately aggressive. |
| **Universal / NBCUniversal** | Consistent claimer. Owns Get Out, Us, Oppenheimer, The Birds, Psycho, Schindler's List. Universal Music Group (UMG) is the most aggressive music rights holder on YouTube -- any UMG-published score will trigger instantly. |
| **Sony Pictures** | Claims aggressively. Owns Basic Instinct, A Single Man. Sony Music is separately aggressive. |

### MEDIUM RISK -- Claims likely but disputes usually succeed

| Rights Holder | Notes |
|---------------|-------|
| **Lionsgate** | Claims but less aggressive on dispute rejection. Owns some catalog titles. |
| **A24** | Generally more permissive toward video essays but still uses Content ID. Owns some newer releases. |
| **Paramount** | Variable enforcement. Owns Chinatown, Double Indemnity, Sunset Boulevard, Breakfast at Tiffany's. Some catalog titles have lapsed or inconsistent Content ID. |
| **MGM (now Amazon)** | Post-Amazon acquisition, enforcement is tightening. Owns No Country for Old Men, Raging Bull (distribution), some Bond films. |

### LOWER RISK -- Claims less common, disputes rarely escalate

| Rights Holder | Notes |
|---------------|-------|
| **Criterion Collection** | Generally supportive of film criticism and scholarship. Distributes many art house titles (Persona, Orphee, M, The Mirror). Claims may still appear from underlying rights holders. |
| **Janus Films** | Similar to Criterion -- art cinema distributor, less aggressive. |
| **Independent / arthouse distributors** | Smaller distributors often lack Content ID fingerprinting. Films from Kino Lorber, Cohen Film Collection, Milestone Films rarely trigger automated claims. |
| **Public domain films** | Pre-1928 films are public domain in the US. Some 1928-1963 films with lapsed copyright renewal are also PD. Verify status per film. Duck Soup (1933) may have lapsed copyright but Paramount may still claim. |

### CRITICAL NOTE: Music Rights vs. Film Rights

Music labels are often MORE aggressive than film studios. Even if a studio doesn't claim a visual clip, the clip's background music may trigger a separate claim from:

- **Universal Music Group (UMG)** -- most aggressive music claimer on YouTube
- **Sony Music Entertainment (SME)** -- second most aggressive
- **Warner Music Group (WMG)** -- third most aggressive
- **Independent publishers** (BMG, Concord, Kobalt) -- variable

**Mitigation:** Strip all audio from film clips and replace with ETCETER4 original scoring. If dialogue is essential, isolate dialogue from music track before editing.

## Escalation Path

Content ID disputes have a defined escalation sequence. Each step has higher stakes.

### Step 1: Standard Dispute (Low Risk)

- Filed through YouTube Studio > Content > select video > select claim > Dispute
- Claimant has **30 days** to respond
- If claimant does not respond within 30 days, the claim is automatically released
- No personal information required at this stage
- **Most video essay disputes are resolved here.** Many automated claims are released after dispute because the claimant (or their agent) recognizes fair use.
- You can continue uploading and earning revenue on other videos during the dispute

### Step 2: Appeal (Medium Risk)

- If the claimant rejects the dispute, you can file an Appeal
- Claimant has **30 days** to respond to the appeal
- If rejected again, the next step requires a DMCA counter-notification
- **Two rejected appeals on different videos = copyright strike warning.** Be strategic about which disputes to escalate.

### Step 3: Counter-Notification (Higher Risk)

- If the appeal is rejected, YouTube offers a DMCA counter-notification
- **Requires your legal name and address** -- this information is shared with the claimant
- Claimant has **10 business days** to file a federal lawsuit or the content is restored
- Most claimants do NOT file lawsuits over video essay fair use -- the cost-benefit is unfavorable
- However, Disney and Warner Bros. have legal departments that occasionally pursue
- **Do not file a counter-notification without understanding the legal exposure**

### Step 4: Consult an Attorney

Consider legal consultation if:

- [ ] A single video receives 3+ claims from the same rights holder (pattern of targeting)
- [ ] A dispute is rejected and the claimant threatens further action
- [ ] You are considering a counter-notification against Disney, Warner, Universal, or Sony
- [ ] The channel receives a copyright strike (not just a Content ID claim -- strikes are more serious)
- [ ] Revenue from the channel justifies the cost of legal counsel

**Resources:**
- Lumen Database (lumendatabase.org) -- searchable database of DMCA takedown requests
- Electronic Frontier Foundation (eff.org) -- fair use advocacy and legal guidance
- Volunteer Lawyers for the Arts (vlany.org) -- free or low-cost legal counsel for artists
- YouTube Creator Support -- accessible through YouTube Studio for Partner Program members

### When to Accept a Claim Instead of Disputing

Sometimes the strategic choice is to accept the claim:

- **"Track only" claims** that don't affect monetization or visibility -- low priority to dispute
- **Claims on videos with minimal revenue potential** -- the dispute process takes time and attention
- **Claims from rights holders known to escalate aggressively** -- if the clip isn't essential and the episode works without it, consider re-editing to remove the triggering clip rather than disputing

## Quick Reference: Fair Use Four Factors

For every clip, confirm all four factors favor fair use before including:

1. **Purpose and Character** -- Is the use transformative? Does it add new meaning through criticism, commentary, or education? (YES for all Object Lessons clips -- narration provides analytical context.)
2. **Nature of the Original** -- Is the original a published creative work? (YES for all feature films -- this factor always favors the copyright holder, but it's the least weighted factor.)
3. **Amount Used** -- Is only the minimum necessary amount used? (Keep clips under 10 seconds. Calculate percentage of total runtime.)
4. **Market Effect** -- Does the use substitute for the original? (NO for analytical video essays -- the video does not replace watching the film.)

Fair use is a BALANCING TEST, not a checklist. Strong performance on factors 1, 3, and 4 can outweigh factor 2. The key principle: the more transformative the use, the stronger the fair use argument.
