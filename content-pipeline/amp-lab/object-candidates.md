# Object Lessons -- Candidate Objects

## Evaluation Key

Priority scores are based on the criteria defined in `templates/object-research-brief.md`. A full research brief should be completed before committing any object to production.

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 5 | Immediate production -- high search, rich history, clear angle, clips accessible |
| 4 | Strong candidate -- minor gaps in one criterion, but overall compelling |
| 3 | Solid backlog -- worth doing but not urgent, or needs more research |
| 2 | Weak -- significant gaps in searchability, clip access, or narrative |
| 1 | Not viable -- too niche, too competitive, or insufficient film history |

---

## Tier 1 -- Launch Priority

These objects have the highest impact, strongest searchability, and richest film history. Any of these could anchor the V2 relaunch.

### 1. Milk

**Priority Score: 5/5**

The most culturally loaded liquid in cinema. Milk signals innocence, purity, nourishment -- and directors weaponize those associations. A Clockwork Orange (Alex's milk-plus), Get Out (the hypnosis stirring), Inglourious Basterds (Hans Landa's glass of milk during the opening interrogation), No Country for Old Men (Chigurh's glass after a murder), Mad Max: Fury Road (the Milk Mothers), Leon: The Professional (Leon's only vice).

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- innocence, purity, motherhood, domesticity. Subversion is the story.
- **Film history presence:** 70+ notable appearances spanning 1920s-present
- **Searchability:** Strong. "Milk in movies" and "milk cinema" have existing search volume. Now You See It did a video but not a definitive compilation format.
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- white liquid against varied cinematography is visually striking
- **Cross-director range:** Kubrick, Peele, Tarantino, Coens, Miller, Hitchcock, Besson, Bong Joon-ho
- **Competitive landscape:** Partial coverage exists but no definitive supercut/analysis
- **Clip sourcing:** MODERATE -- most key films widely available on streaming
- **Narrative potential:** The arc from genuine innocence (early cinema) to weaponized innocence (modern) is a complete story

### 2. Mirrors

**Priority Score: 5/5**

The most philosophically rich object in cinema. Mirrors enable self-recognition, double identity, vanity, truth-telling, portal crossing. Every major director has a mirror scene. Taxi Driver ("You talkin' to me?"), Black Swan (Natalie Portman's cracking reflection), Mulholland Drive, The Lady from Shanghai (hall of mirrors), Orpheus (Cocteau's mirror as portal to the underworld), Contact (young Ellie running to the medicine cabinet mirror).

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- self, identity, truth, vanity, the double. Mythological and psychoanalytic depth.
- **Film history presence:** 100+ -- arguably the single most used symbolic object in cinema
- **Searchability:** HIGH -- "mirror scenes in movies" is a proven search term
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- reflections, distortions, shattering. Inherently cinematic.
- **Cross-director range:** Tarkovsky (literally titled a film "Mirror"), Kubrick, Lynch, Aronofsky, Cocteau, Hitchcock, Welles, Villeneuve
- **Competitive landscape:** Several mirror compilations exist but most are listicles, not analytical essays
- **Clip sourcing:** EASY -- mirror scenes are well-documented and films are widely available
- **Narrative potential:** The evolution from mirrors as literal plot devices (silent era) to mirrors as psychological landscapes (modern) is rich

### 3. Cigarettes

**Priority Score: 4/5**

The object whose disappearance tells a cultural story. Cigarettes were ubiquitous in cinema from the 1930s through the 1990s -- glamorous, rebellious, contemplative. Their declining screen presence tracks directly with public health campaigns, studio self-censorship, and changing notions of cool. Casablanca, Breakfast at Tiffany's, Pulp Fiction, Coffee and Cigarettes, Thank You for Smoking, Inherent Vice.

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- glamour, rebellion, addiction, mortality, class. The decline is the story.
- **Film history presence:** 80+ iconic uses (literally hundreds of incidental ones)
- **Searchability:** HIGH -- "smoking in movies" has strong search volume. Nostalgia angle.
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- smoke, lighting through haze, the gesture of smoking. Film noir is built on it.
- **Cross-director range:** Hawks, Wilder, Godard, Jarmusch, Tarantino, Wong Kar-wai, the Coens
- **Competitive landscape:** Several existing videos on smoking in film, but most are nostalgic compilations. The analytical "decline" angle is underexplored.
- **Clip sourcing:** MODERATE -- older films may require physical media for quality clips
- **Narrative potential:** The arc of "most glamorous prop in cinema" to "signifier of moral failure" is a complete cultural essay

### 4. Clocks / Watches

**Priority Score: 4/5**

Time made physical. Clocks and watches represent mortality, urgency, countdown, the mechanical versus the human. Safety Last! (Harold Lloyd hanging from the clock face), High Noon (the clock as antagonist), Interstellar (the ticking watch), Hugo (the automaton's clockwork), Pulp Fiction (Christopher Walken's watch monologue), Metropolis (the factory clock).

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- time, death, industry, urgency. Universal human anxiety.
- **Film history presence:** 60+ notable uses. Clocks as both literal plot devices and metaphorical weight.
- **Searchability:** MEDIUM-HIGH -- "clocks in movies" is niche but "time in cinema" is broader
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- clock faces, ticking close-ups, time-lapse, stopped clocks
- **Cross-director range:** Hitchcock, Nolan, Lang, Scorsese, Miyazaki, Kubrick, Lloyd
- **Competitive landscape:** LOW -- no definitive video on clocks/watches as objects in cinema
- **Clip sourcing:** MODERATE -- good mix of public domain (silent era) and streaming-available films
- **Narrative potential:** The tension between human time and mechanical time runs through all of cinema

### 5. Doors

**Priority Score: 4/5**

Threshold symbolism. Every door opening or closing is a decision, a revelation, a boundary crossed. The Godfather's final shot (Kay watching the door close on Michael's world), The Shining ("Here's Johnny!"), Psycho (the shower curtain as door), 2001 (the pod bay doors), The Wizard of Oz (Dorothy opening the door from sepia to Technicolor), Blue Velvet (the ear as door into the underworld).

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- liminality, choice, inclusion/exclusion, revelation, privacy
- **Film history presence:** 60+ iconic door moments (every film has doors, but meaningful ones are distinct)
- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- "door scenes in movies" is less searched but "iconic movie doors" has potential
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- doors frame shots, divide spaces, create dramatic reveals
- **Cross-director range:** Coppola, Kubrick, Hitchcock, Lynch, Fleming, Spielberg, Bong Joon-ho
- **Competitive landscape:** LOW -- very few existing videos on doors as a cinematic motif
- **Clip sourcing:** EASY -- door scenes are well-known and films are accessible
- **Narrative potential:** Doors as architectural metaphor for cinematic language itself (the frame as door)

## Tier 2 -- Strong Candidates

These objects have strong film history and analytical potential. Some have searchability challenges or higher competitive pressure. All are viable episodes.

### 6. Guns

**Priority Score: 4/5**

The most politically charged object in American cinema. Guns carry weight that no other prop does -- they are simultaneously tools, symbols, fetish objects, and moral tests. The Good, the Bad and the Ugly, Taxi Driver, Unforgiven, Bowling for Columbine, The Deer Hunter, Heat.

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- power, violence, masculinity, American identity
- **Film history presence:** 100+ (the challenge is curation, not finding enough)
- **Searchability:** HIGH -- but also high competition
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- iconic imagery but risks being a "guns are cool" compilation
- **Cross-director range:** Leone, Scorsese, Eastwood, Mann, Tarantino, Bigelow, Kathryn Bigelow
- **Competitive landscape:** HIGH -- many existing compilations. Our angle must be analytical, not celebratory.
- **Clip sourcing:** EASY
- **Narrative potential:** Strong but requires careful framing (not glorification). The "how cinema's relationship with guns has changed" angle is the essay.

### 7. Letters / Notes

**Priority Score: 3/5**

The analogue communication object becoming extinct. Letters carry secrets, confessions, misdirections. The Shop Around the Corner, Atonement, The Age of Innocence, Her, Harry Potter (the Hogwarts letters), The Grand Budapest Hotel, 84 Charing Cross Road.

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- communication, secrets, romance, the tangible in a digital age
- **Film history presence:** 50+ meaningful uses
- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- niche but "love letters in movies" has romantic audience crossover
- **Visual potential:** MEDIUM -- paper, handwriting, opening envelopes. Elegant but not explosive.
- **Cross-director range:** Lubitsch, Wright, Scorsese, Jonze, Anderson, Spielberg
- **Competitive landscape:** LOW -- underexplored as a motif
- **Clip sourcing:** MODERATE
- **Narrative potential:** The extinction narrative (letters disappearing from cinema as they disappear from life) is compelling

### 8. Blood

**Priority Score: 3/5**

The body's confession. Blood in cinema signals violence, sacrifice, birth, menstruation, contamination, the stakes becoming real. Carrie, Kill Bill, The Shining (elevator), Psycho (shower drain), There Will Be Blood, Nosferatu, Un Chien Andalou.

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- life, death, sacrifice, horror, the body's vulnerability
- **Film history presence:** 70+ iconic uses
- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- "blood in movies" skews horror-only, needs broader framing
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- color theory (red), volume, texture. Visually dramatic.
- **Cross-director range:** De Palma, Tarantino, Kubrick, Hitchcock, Murnau, Park Chan-wook
- **Competitive landscape:** MEDIUM -- horror compilations exist but cross-genre analysis is rare
- **Clip sourcing:** EASY -- well-documented scenes
- **Narrative potential:** How cinema's relationship with showing blood has changed (Hays Code censorship to modern splatter) is a strong cultural arc

### 9. Rain

**Priority Score: 4/5**

Mood made weather. Rain in cinema is never just rain -- it cleanses, mourns, threatens, romanticizes, isolates. Blade Runner (tears in rain), Singin' in the Rain (joy), Seven (despair), The Shawshank Redemption (freedom), Rashomon (narrative frame), Oldboy (the corridor fight).

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- cleansing, sorrow, atmosphere, pathetic fallacy
- **Film history presence:** 60+ iconic rain scenes
- **Searchability:** HIGH -- "best rain scenes in movies" is a well-searched term
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- rain transforms light, reflection, texture. Extremely cinematic.
- **Cross-director range:** Ridley Scott, Donen, Fincher, Kurosawa, Park Chan-wook, Darabont
- **Competitive landscape:** MEDIUM -- compilation videos exist but most are "top 10" not analytical
- **Clip sourcing:** EASY -- rain scenes are well-known and visually identifiable
- **Narrative potential:** Rain as the director's emotional score -- how weather becomes narration

### 10. Food / Meals

**Priority Score: 3/5**

Communion, power, seduction, class. Eating together in cinema is never neutral. Babette's Feast, Tampopo, The Cook the Thief His Wife & Her Lover, Goodfellas (prison cooking), Eat Drink Man Woman, Big Night, Ratatouille, The Godfather (oranges as death omen).

- **Cultural significance:** HIGH -- community, class, pleasure, sustenance, cultural identity
- **Film history presence:** 50+ meals that matter cinematically
- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- "food in movies" has search volume but broad competition
- **Visual potential:** HIGH -- food cinematography is a genre unto itself
- **Cross-director range:** Axel, Itami, Greenaway, Scorsese, Ang Lee, Bird, Coppola
- **Competitive landscape:** MEDIUM -- food-in-film is a popular topic but usually by food channels, not film analysis
- **Clip sourcing:** EASY
- **Narrative potential:** Meals as the social contract -- what it means when characters eat together (or refuse to)

## Tier 3 -- Rich but Niche

Strong analytical material with narrower audience appeal. Best scheduled after the channel has established its audience.

### 11. Flowers

**Priority Score: 3/5**

Beauty, death, romance, the ephemeral. American Beauty (roses), Blue Velvet (the severed ear among flowers), Vertigo (the bouquet), Midsommar (the flower crown), The Secret Garden. Flowers wilt -- their symbolism is inseparable from impermanence.

- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- niche but visually compelling for thumbnails
- **Cross-director range:** Mendes, Lynch, Hitchcock, Aster, Ozu
- **Clip sourcing:** MODERATE
- **Narrative potential:** Beauty that decays -- flowers as cinema's memento mori

### 12. Keys

**Priority Score: 3/5**

Access, secrets, power, trust. Keys unlock narrative possibilities. Hitchcock was obsessed with them (Notorious, Dial M for Murder). Coraline, Bluebeard adaptations, Get Out (the teacup and the keys), Pan's Labyrinth.

- **Searchability:** LOW-MEDIUM -- niche
- **Cross-director range:** Hitchcock (dominant), del Toro, Peele, Selick
- **Clip sourcing:** MODERATE -- key scenes are often brief and require precise extraction
- **Narrative potential:** Keys as the oldest storytelling device (who has access?)

### 13. Stairs

**Priority Score: 3/5**

Ascent, descent, class, tension, the vertical axis of cinema. Parasite (the stairs between rich and poor), Vertigo (the bell tower), The Exorcist (the spider walk), Battleship Potemkin (the Odessa Steps), Joker (the bathroom stairs dance).

- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- "stairs in movies" has some volume, Parasite connection helps
- **Cross-director range:** Bong, Hitchcock, Friedkin, Eisenstein, Phillips, Kubrick
- **Clip sourcing:** EASY -- staircase scenes are well-documented
- **Narrative potential:** Stairs as class architecture -- cinema's vertical axis of power

### 14. Windows

**Priority Score: 3/5**

Voyeurism, framing, escape, the screen-within-a-screen. Rear Window (the definitive statement), A Room with a View, The Truman Show, Psycho (Norman watching through the peephole), The Lives of Others.

- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- "window scenes in movies" is niche
- **Cross-director range:** Hitchcock, Ivory, Weir, von Donnersmarck, Spielberg
- **Clip sourcing:** MODERATE
- **Narrative potential:** Windows as cinema's self-portrait -- the frame watching the frame

### 15. Water / Swimming Pools

**Priority Score: 3/5**

Wealth, danger, transformation, the subconscious. Sunset Boulevard (the opening dead body), The Graduate (Benjamin floating), Gattaca (the swimming contest), A Bigger Splash, The Swimmer, It Follows.

- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- "swimming pool movies" has search volume in summer months
- **Cross-director range:** Wilder, Nichols, Niccol, Guadagnino, Perry, Mitchell
- **Clip sourcing:** EASY
- **Narrative potential:** Pools as wealth signifiers that always harbor danger

## Tier 4 -- Experimental

These objects may lack the search volume or breadth for a traditional Object Lesson. They could work as shorter episodes, collaborative specials, or experiments with the format.

### 16. Hats

**Priority Score: 2/5**

Identity, status, disguise, era-specific. Indiana Jones, film noir fedoras, westerns, The Grand Budapest Hotel. Strong visual identity but the analytical angle is less clear.

- **Searchability:** LOW -- "hats in movies" is more fashion than film analysis
- **Cross-director range:** Spielberg, Hawks, Leone, Anderson
- **Narrative potential:** Hats as character shorthand -- how cinema uses headwear to signal identity

### 17. Photographs

**Priority Score: 3/5**

Memory, evidence, the past, the frozen moment. Blade Runner (Deckard's photos), Memento (Leonard's Polaroids), Blow-Up (the enlargement), The Omen, Amelie.

- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- "photographs in movies" has some crossover with photography audience
- **Cross-director range:** Ridley Scott, Nolan, Antonioni, Donner, Jeunet
- **Narrative potential:** Photographs as cinema's anxiety about its own medium -- the still image inside the moving image

### 18. Cars

**Priority Score: 2/5**

Freedom, masculinity, the American dream, escape, destruction. Drive, Mad Max: Fury Road, Bullitt, Thelma & Louise, Christine, The Italian Job. Strong visual material but risks being a "cool cars" compilation.

- **Searchability:** HIGH -- but dominated by car enthusiast channels, not film analysis
- **Cross-director range:** Refn, Miller, McQueen, Scott, Carpenter, Friedkin
- **Narrative potential:** Needs a strong analytical frame to avoid "best car chases" territory

### 19. Books

**Priority Score: 2/5**

Knowledge, escape, the forbidden, destruction of knowledge. Fahrenheit 451, Se7en, The Neverending Story, Beauty and the Beast, Indiana Jones, The Name of the Rose.

- **Searchability:** LOW-MEDIUM -- literary audience crossover but niche
- **Cross-director range:** Truffaut, Fincher, Petersen, Eco/Annaud, Spielberg
- **Narrative potential:** Books as objects that cinema both reveres and fears (the competing medium)

### 20. Masks

**Priority Score: 3/5**

Identity, deception, ritual, the hidden self. Eyes Wide Shut, V for Vendetta, The Phantom of the Opera, Halloween, The Dark Knight, Persona, Scream.

- **Searchability:** MEDIUM -- "masks in movies" has search volume, especially around Halloween
- **Cross-director range:** Kubrick, McTeigue, Carpenter, Nolan, Bergman, Craven
- **Narrative potential:** Masks as the paradox of cinema -- hiding the face in a medium built on faces
