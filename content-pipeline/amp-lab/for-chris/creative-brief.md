# Object Lessons V2 — Creative Brief for Chris

Hey Chris,

I've been thinking about Object Lessons a lot lately. Like, more than a lot. I keep coming back to those original compilations we made — the way a three-minute montage of milk in film could genuinely unsettle someone. We built something real with those. And I think it's time to go back.

## The Vision

Object Lessons V2 is the same soul, evolved format. The compilations were beautiful — pure visual argument, no narration, just objects speaking for themselves. That was right for where we were. But I've realized there's a layer we never made explicit: the *why*. Why does milk unsettle us? Why do mirrors terrify directors? V2 keeps the visual montage at the center but wraps it in narration that makes the implicit analysis explicit. The objects still speak — now we give them context.

## Why Now

Here's the thing: nobody is doing this. Every Frame a Painting closed years ago. Rishi Kaneria pivoted away. The video essay world grew up around us while we were away, but the "objects in film" space? That's still ours. We had it first. Six years is a long gap, and I'm not going to pretend otherwise — but the idea only got sharper while it sat.

## The First Three Episodes

**Milk.** From Hitchcock's poisoned glass in *Suspicion* to Jordan Peele's stirring scene in *Get Out*, milk is never innocent in cinema. It's purity performed, and every director who reaches for it knows exactly what they're doing.

**Mirrors.** The most philosophically loaded object in film — every director uses them differently, and the mirror is cinema looking at itself. Tarkovsky, Kubrick, Deren, Peele again. It's the one object that literally reflects the medium's obsession with seeing.

**Cigarettes.** The only object whose meaning completely reversed because society changed. Glamour to poison, rebellion to addiction. The cultural decline arc *is* the story — you can watch an entire century of moral shift through one prop.

## The Format

We open with a pure visual montage — the most striking moments, no narration, just the object and the music. Then we pull back and ask: *why?* The narration carries us through film history, and we close with another montage, but now the audience sees differently. What was invisible is now visible.
