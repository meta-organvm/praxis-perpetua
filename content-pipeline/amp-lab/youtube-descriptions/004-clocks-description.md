Cinema IS a clock. It was born as one -- a projector running at 24 frames per second, a shutter opening and closing, measuring time tick by tick. When a filmmaker puts a clock on screen, they're holding up a mirror to the apparatus itself.

From Harold Lloyd dangling from a clock face in 1923 to Nolan weaving his own pocket watch into Dunkirk's soundtrack -- from the alarm clock that traps Bill Murray in Groundhog Day to Christian Marclay's 24-hour film that functions as an actual timepiece -- cinema has never stopped confessing that its deepest subject is time. Tarkovsky called filmmaking "sculpting in time." The clock is the chisel.
