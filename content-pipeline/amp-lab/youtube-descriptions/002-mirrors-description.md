The mirror is cinema's most self-aware object. It reveals truth, enables lies, fractures identity, opens portals, weaponizes vanity, and confronts characters with themselves. No other prop has this range -- and no other prop does what cinema itself does.

From the Marx Brothers' mirror pantomime to Cocteau pouring actual mercury to Aronofsky hiding cameras millimeters from the reflected frame -- every director who films a mirror is solving two problems at once: how to point a camera at a reflective surface without revealing the camera, and how to use a device that shows reality to tell a story about illusion. This is the mirror's century-long argument with cinema.
