# SOP: Source Evaluation & Annotated Bibliography (The Refinery)

## 1. Ontological Purpose
This SOP defines the process for discovering, evaluating, annotating, and synthesizing source material at a doctoral standard. It is the upstream input to all research-dependent SOPs in the cluster. No source enters the system unexamined; no bibliography is considered complete until saturation is demonstrated.

A literature review is not a summary. It is a critical, exhaustive, and synthetic analysis that establishes expertise and justifies original work. It must move beyond "what has been done" to "how it has been done" and "what remains unknown."

**Applicable to:** All ORGANVM research, all organs. Governed by `METADOC--research-standards.md`.

**Downstream consumers:** `SOP--research-to-implementation-pipeline.md` (Stage I), `SOP--typological-hermeneutic-analysis.md` (Phase I), `SOP--market-gap-analysis.md` (Phase I).

## 2. Phase I: Source Discovery & Scope Definition

**Goal:** Define the boundaries of the search before reading a single source. Pre-defined criteria prevent convenience sampling and ensure defensibility.

### Process
1. **Define the research question** — a single sentence that every source must address directly or indirectly.
2. **Set inclusion criteria** — elements a source must have to qualify (e.g., empirical study, specific population, specific theoretical framework, peer-reviewed).
3. **Set exclusion criteria** — disqualifying factors (e.g., non-English without justification, pre-cutoff date without seminal status, non-peer-reviewed gray literature without explicit rationale).
4. **Declare source hierarchy targets:**
   - Tier 1 (Foundational/Seminal): Must include all universally cited works in the field.
   - Tier 2 (Primary Empirical): Prioritize original research over secondary sources.
   - Tier 3 (Secondary Analytical): Systematic reviews, meta-analyses, handbook chapters.
   - Tier 4 (Practitioner/Applied): Industry reports, case studies, recent dissertations.
   - Tier 5 (Contextual/Gray): Conference papers, preprints, trade press — include only with justification.
5. **Set exhaustive scope targets:** A doctoral-standard bibliography typically cites 150-200 references. Define a minimum target proportional to the research scope.
6. **Search across databases:** Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, domain-specific databases (PubMed, IEEE, JSTOR, arXiv). Track search terms and date ranges.

### Starter Research Questions
- What is the central research question, and what sub-questions does it decompose into?
- Which seminal works are cited by everyone in this field? (These are non-negotiable inclusions.)
- What are the natural temporal boundaries? (When did this field begin? What cutoff is defensible?)
- Are there adjacent disciplines whose literature might reveal isomorphisms?
- What is the expected volume of sources for this scope? (Calibrate against published dissertations in the field.)

## 3. Phase II: Source Evaluation — CRAAP Test & Methodological Rigor

**Goal:** Every source receives a quantified trust assessment before it enters the bibliography. Use the `source-evaluation-scorecard.md` template.

### Process
1. **Apply the CRAAP Test** to each source — score 1-10 on five dimensions:
   - **Currency:** Is the source timely? STEM: last 5 years preferred. Humanities: 5-10 years acceptable. Seminal works exempt.
   - **Relevance:** Does it directly address the research question or a declared sub-theme?
   - **Authority:** Evaluate author credentials, institutional affiliation, peer-review status.
   - **Accuracy:** Are findings supported by evidence? Is the methodology sound?
   - **Purpose:** Identify potential biases. Bias is not disqualifying but must be noted.
2. **Compute Trust Score** — mean of the five CRAAP dimensions.
3. **Set threshold:** Trust Score >= 6 for inclusion. Sources scoring 4-5 may be included as counterpoints with explicit justification. Below 4: exclude.
4. **Assess methodological rigor** for each included source:
   - Was the research design appropriate for its own questions?
   - Are conclusions supported by the presented data?
   - What are the stated limitations?
   - Are there unstated limitations visible to a critical reader?
5. **Classify into source hierarchy tier** (1-5 per Phase I).

### Starter Research Questions
- Does this source's methodology actually support its conclusions, or does it overreach?
- Is the author a recognized authority, or is this a one-off publication?
- If this source were removed, would the bibliography lose a unique perspective?
- Are there contradictory findings in sources of equal authority? What explains the discrepancy?
- Has this source been cited by subsequent high-quality work, or has it been ignored/refuted?

## 4. Phase III: Annotation & 5 Cs Synthesis

**Goal:** Transform individual source evaluations into a connected whole. Every source is annotated with the 5 Cs framework, then organized into a coherent synthesis.

### Process
1. **For each source passing CRAAP threshold, annotate with the 5 Cs:**
   - **Cite:** Credit the finding and establish its temporal position.
   - **Compare:** Highlight similarities in findings, methodologies, or research questions across studies.
   - **Contrast:** Identify where findings or theories disagree and explore why.
   - **Critique:** Evaluate whether the research design was sound and conclusions are supported.
   - **Connect:** Relate the source directly to the research problem and methodology.
2. **Organize the synthesis** using one or more structural approaches:
   - **Thematic:** Group by topic or concept (most common).
   - **Methodological:** Group by research approach to reveal methodological trends.
   - **Chronological:** Show the evolution of understanding over time.
3. **Build the synthesis narrative:** Sources are not listed; they are integrated. Show how they relate to one another, where they agree, where they conflict, and what patterns emerge.
4. **Identify theoretical framework:** The review must define and justify the theoretical or conceptual framework that guides the study. This emerges from the synthesis, not before it.

### Starter Research Questions
- What themes emerge when sources are grouped by finding rather than by author?
- Where do high-authority sources disagree? Is the disagreement methodological, theoretical, or empirical?
- What theoretical framework best accounts for the majority of the evidence?
- Are there chronological shifts — points where the field's consensus changed?
- Which connections between sources have not been made by previous reviewers?

---

## 5. Phase IV: Gap Identification & Iteration

**Goal:** Map the terrain of what is known to expose what is unknown. The bibliography is never finished until the research is submitted.

### Process
1. **Build a coverage map:** Matrix of research sub-questions vs. sources that address each. Identify:
   - **Well-covered areas:** Multiple high-quality sources with convergent findings.
   - **Contested areas:** Multiple sources with divergent findings — these are research opportunities.
   - **Gaps:** Sub-questions with zero or inadequate coverage — these justify original research.
2. **Perform saturation check:** Are new sources still revealing new themes, or are they confirming existing ones? Saturation = new sources add volume but not new insight.
3. **Iterate continuously:** A doctoral-standard bibliography is "never finished" until final submission. As new research publishes during the 3-5 year study period, update the bibliography.
4. **Document gaps explicitly:** Each gap becomes either a research question for the current study or a declared limitation with a recommendation for future research.
5. **Validate against peers:** Present the gap analysis to advisors or peer reviewers. Gaps that seem obvious to the researcher may be well-covered in literature they haven't encountered.

### Starter Research Questions
- Which sub-questions have zero sources? Is this a true gap or a search failure?
- Are the gaps methodological (no one has studied this *properly*) or topical (no one has studied this *at all*)?
- Has saturation been reached, or are new sources still shifting the synthesis?
- What would a hostile reviewer say is missing from this bibliography?
- Are there adjacent fields whose literature might fill identified gaps?

---

## 6. Output Artifacts

1. **Source Evaluation Scorecard** — completed `source-evaluation-scorecard.md` per research session.
2. **Annotated Bibliography** — formatted bibliography with Trust Score, tier, 5 Cs annotation, and methodology note per source. Organized by theme or chronology.
3. **Synthesis Narrative** — integrated prose (not a list) showing how the literature connects, conflicts, and converges.
4. **Gap Analysis Matrix** — sub-questions vs. sources, with gaps highlighted and severity rated (H/M/L).
5. **Saturation Report** — brief statement of whether saturation has been reached and what triggers the next iteration.
