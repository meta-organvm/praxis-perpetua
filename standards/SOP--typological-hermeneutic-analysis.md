# SOP: Typological & Hermeneutic Analysis (The Archaeology)

## 1. Ontological Purpose
This SOP defines the process for conducting Architectural Typology and Hermeneutic Research — the exhaustive historical study of a "form" to extract its deep structure and synthesize foundational knowledge. We study history not to record it but to extract the invariant rules (Covenants) that govern the future.

The assumption is that "everything already exists" as deep, underlying structures (types). The work is to strip away style, find essence, and alchemize that essence with present needs to create something both original and timeless.

**Applicable to:** All ORGANVM projects requiring historical-to-normative research. Governed by `METADOC--research-standards.md` (Pillar I: Historiography).

**Upstream dependency:** `SOP--source-evaluation-and-bibliography.md` (provides evaluated source inventory).

**Downstream consumers:** `SOP--research-to-implementation-pipeline.md` (Stage II-III), `SOP--market-gap-analysis.md` (Phase I genealogical inquiry).

## 2. Phase I: Typological Inventory

**Goal:** Enumerate all historical instances of the "type" — every previous iteration of the form being studied, across time and discipline.

### Process
1. **Define the "type"** — the abstract form being studied (e.g., "the accountability system," "the creative marketplace," "the verification engine").
2. **Enumerate all instances** — historical, contemporary, and speculative — across all relevant fields and eras. Each instance is a row in the typological matrix.
3. **Build the typological matrix:**

   | Instance | Era | Discipline | Surface Structure (Style) | Deep Structure (Essence) | Outcome |
   |----------|-----|-----------|--------------------------|--------------------------|---------|
   | *e.g., Guild system* | *Medieval* | *Economics* | *Apprentice hierarchy* | *Reputation-based trust* | *Survived 400 years* |

4. **Classify instances by lineage:** Which instances evolved from which? Where did the form branch? Where did it converge?
5. **Identify the "unkillable" elements** — features that persist across all instances regardless of era or discipline. These are candidate Covenants.

### Starter Research Questions
- How many distinct historical instances of this form can be identified?
- Which instances are universally recognized as "canonical" in their field?
- What surface features change across instances while the deep structure remains?
- Are there instances from unexpected disciplines that share isomorphic deep structure?
- Which instances failed, and did they lack a specific deep-structure element that survivors possessed?

---

## 3. Phase II: Hermeneutic Circle

**Goal:** Achieve interpretive understanding through iterative movement between the whole (the history of the form) and the parts (specific documents, instances, and artifacts) until understanding stabilizes.

### Process
1. **First pass — the whole:** Read the entire corpus of sources gathered in the typological inventory. Form a preliminary interpretation of the form's essential nature.
2. **Second pass — the parts:** Re-read individual sources with the whole-interpretation in mind. Note where specific instances confirm, refine, or contradict the whole-interpretation.
3. **Third pass — revised whole:** Incorporate part-level insights back into the whole-interpretation. Has the understanding shifted?
4. **Iterate until stabilization:** Continue whole-to-parts-to-whole cycles until new passes no longer shift the interpretation. This is the Fusion of Horizons — where the researcher's current perspective merges with the historical truth of the object.
5. **Document the stabilized interpretation** — this becomes the working definition of the form's deep structure.

### Starter Research Questions
- Does the initial whole-interpretation survive contact with the individual parts?
- Which specific instances most challenge the current interpretation?
- At what point did the interpretation stabilize? How many cycles were required?
- What was the Fusion of Horizons — how did the researcher's initial assumptions change?
- Are there parts that remain unresolved? (These are declared limitations or future research.)

---

## 4. Phase III: Genealogical Inquiry

**Goal:** Conduct a Foucauldian "history of the present" — trace the current form backward through time to expose the power structures and logic that produced it, and identify the rupture points where it might have gone differently.

### Process
1. **Identify the current form's dominant characteristics** — what does it look like today? What does it claim to do? What does it actually do?
2. **Trace backward:** For each dominant characteristic, identify when and why it emerged. What historical event, power shift, or technological change produced it?
3. **Map the logic chain:** Build a causal narrative from origin to present. At each step, note:
   - What alternatives existed but were not chosen?
   - What power structures benefited from the chosen path?
   - What was lost at each branch point?
4. **Identify rupture points:** Moments where the form's trajectory could have changed. These are intervention opportunities for the new creation.
5. **Alchemize the path:** For each rupture point, ask: "If we had taken the alternative path, what would the form look like today?" This generates candidate Covenants for the new creation.

### Starter Research Questions
- What does the current form claim to do, and what does it actually do? Where is the gap?
- What historical accident produced the current dominant form? Was it the "best" outcome or merely the surviving one?
- Who benefited from each major decision point in the form's history?
- What alternatives were available at each rupture point? Why were they not chosen?
- If we could "replay" history from a specific rupture point, what would we choose differently?

---

## 5. Phase IV: Pattern Language & Isomorphism Extraction

**Goal:** Extract the invariant patterns (Alexanderian Method) that must be present for the form to be functional. Test these patterns for transdisciplinary isomorphism — do they hold across fields?

### Process
1. **From the typological inventory and hermeneutic interpretation, extract recurring patterns.** Each pattern follows the Alexanderian format:
   - **Context:** The situation in which the pattern applies.
   - **Problem:** The recurring problem the pattern addresses.
   - **Forces:** The tensions or constraints that make the problem hard.
   - **Solution:** The invariant response that successful instances share.
   - **Resulting Context:** What the world looks like after the pattern is applied.
2. **Test each pattern for isomorphism:** Does it hold in a different discipline? (e.g., a pattern found in medieval guild systems — does it appear in biological immune systems? In open-source governance?)
3. **Classify patterns by strength:**
   - **Universal:** Appears in every successful instance across disciplines. Non-negotiable Covenant.
   - **Common:** Appears in most instances. Strong Covenant candidate.
   - **Contextual:** Appears only in specific conditions. Conditional Covenant.
4. **Build the Pattern Language:** The set of all extracted patterns, organized from large-scale (whole-system) to small-scale (individual-component), with explicit relationships between patterns.

### Starter Research Questions
- What patterns appear in every surviving instance of this form?
- Do these patterns hold when tested in an entirely different discipline?
- Which patterns are necessary (present in all survivors) vs. sufficient (present in survivors but also in some failures)?
- How do the patterns relate to one another? Does Pattern A require Pattern B as a prerequisite?
- Are there "anti-patterns" — features present in every failed instance?

---

## 6. Phase V: First Principles Reconstruction

**Goal:** Perform the "Alchemy" — deconstruct the historical forms to their First Principles and reconstruct the new creation from those absolute truths.

### Process
1. **State the Thesis** — the historical "Form" (What was). Synthesize the typological inventory and hermeneutic interpretation into a single coherent statement of what the form has been.
2. **State the Antithesis** — the current "Failure/Gap" (What is). Using the genealogical inquiry, state precisely how and why the current form fails.
3. **Perform the Synthesis** — the "New Covenant" (What must be). Using the pattern language, construct the normative theory:
   - What universal patterns must be preserved?
   - What rupture points must be addressed?
   - What new elements are required by the present context that no historical instance possessed?
4. **Express the Synthesis as Covenants** — numbered, falsifiable rules that the new creation must satisfy. Each Covenant traces to specific patterns, instances, and genealogical evidence.
5. **Validate the Covenants** against the typological matrix: Would these Covenants, if applied retroactively, have improved the failed instances? Would they have preserved the successful ones?

### Starter Research Questions
- Can the entire historical trajectory be compressed into a single Thesis statement?
- What is the most concise statement of why the current form fails?
- Do the proposed Covenants address every identified rupture point?
- If these Covenants had existed 100 years ago, would the form's history be different?
- Are there Covenants that conflict with one another? How is the tension resolved?

---

## 7. Output Artifacts

1. **Typological Matrix** — all instances of the form, classified by era, discipline, surface structure, deep structure, and outcome.
2. **Hermeneutic Interpretation Log** — record of each whole-parts-whole cycle with the interpretation at each stage and the evidence that shifted it.
3. **Genealogical Map** — causal narrative from origin to present with rupture points and alternative paths marked.
4. **Pattern Language Document** — all extracted patterns in Alexanderian format, classified by strength, with isomorphism test results.
5. **Covenants Document** — numbered, falsifiable rules for the new creation, each traced to its evidentiary basis.
