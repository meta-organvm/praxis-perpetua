## 1. Central Argument

Objects in representational art cross a threshold from functional prop to active carrier of cultural meaning through five interlocking mechanisms: **estrangement**, **accumulation**, **bifurcation**, **condensation**, and **recursion**. This process — *object-attention* — is medium-agnostic in principle but medium-inflected in practice: each medium modulates the five mechanisms differently, and these modulations are not deficiencies but *constitutive features* of how meaning is produced within each medium. The dissertation stages this claim as a dialectical investigation: Paper I argues for cinematic privilege (the thesis), Paper II demolishes each pillar of that privilege by demonstrating object-attention across non-cinematic media (the antithesis), and Paper III introduces the **medium-inflection thesis** as the synthesis — arguing that cinema's four properties (indexicality, duration, materiality-in-motion, montage) do not make it *privileged* but make it *distinctively inflected*, and that each medium's inflection reveals aspects of object-attention that no single medium can disclose alone. The combination of all inflections — not the elevation of any one — is what produces a general theory.

## 4. Paper I (Thesis): Object-Attention in Cinema — A Material History

**Disciplinary home:** Film & Media Studies
**Core claim:** Cinema is the privileged medium for object-attention because of four unique properties: indexicality, duration, materiality-in-motion, and montage. No other medium combines all four.
**Evidence base:** 444+ films across 10 objects (Cereal, Telephones, Balloons, Eggs, Milk, Mirrors, Cigarettes, Clocks, Doors, Guns), cross-object density analysis, supercut methodology, production history.
**Target journals:** *Screen*, *Cinema Journal*, *Film Quarterly*, *[in]Transition*

### Chapter 1: The Apparatus and the Object — Cinema's Formal Kinship with Things

- The chronophotographic gun (Marey, 1882): the camera literally shaped as a weapon. "We shoot films. We frame shots."
- Virilio's *War and Cinema*: cinema and targeting as parallel technologies of perception
- Bazin's ontology of the photographic image: the camera preserves the *appearance* of things — cinema is fundamentally a technology of object-capture
- The Kuleshov Effect as object-theory: meaning produced not by objects in isolation but by objects in *sequence*
- Acknowledgment of the pre-cinematic tradition: object-attention predates cinema by centuries (vanitas painting, 1580s; Chekhov's gun, 1889; Proust's madeleine, 1913). Paper I's claim is deliberately circumscribed: not that cinema *invented* object-attention, but that cinema's apparatus produces a *distinctive inflection* of it. Paper II will challenge even this circumscribed claim — and Paper III will show why both positions are simultaneously correct.
- Argument: cinema's apparatus is constitutively entangled with objects in ways that painting, literature, and theatre are not. The medium was invented to make objects visible in motion. This is a claim about cinematic distinctiveness, not cinematic supremacy — a distinction the chapter makes explicit to prepare the reader for the dialectical turn.

### Chapter 2: The Object Lessons Methodology — Following Things Through Film

- The V1 foundation: Cereal, Telephones, Balloons, Eggs (2017-2020). How the methodology was discovered empirically before being theorized.
- The V2 formalization: identification → cataloguing → taxonomy → deep analysis → narrative arc → theoretical grounding → cross-object analysis
- The supercut as scholarly method: Tohline, Grant, *[in]Transition*, videographic criticism as knowledge production
- Production design as symbolic system: the production designer as still-life composer
- Sound design and the auditory object: Murch's worldizing, Burtt's lightsaber, Nolan's pocket watch
- Quantitative methods: the Materia Collider database, density metrics, co-occurrence matrices

### Chapter 3: Milk — The Bifurcation of Innocence

- 71 films cataloged, 10 symbolic categories, 12 Tier 1 scenes
- The Hitchcock-to-Kubrick transition (1941-1971) as the definitive bifurcation event: milk's functional identity (nourishment) permanently splits from its symbolic identity (threat)
- Barthes's "Wine and Milk" as the semiotic foundation
- The villain-milk lineage: Landa, Chigurh, Rose, Homelander
- Milk and race: Get Out's deconstructed cereal, the alt-right's appropriation
- Milk and the body: Alien's white blood, Possession's subway miscarriage, Mad Max's milking machines
- Close reading: Rose Armitage's milk in Get Out — all five mechanisms demonstrated in a single scene

### Chapter 4: Mirrors — Cinema's Recursive Self-Portrait

- 85 films cataloged, 9 symbolic categories
- Lacan's mirror stage → Baudry's apparatus theory → the cinema screen AS mirror
- The formal challenge: how do you film a mirror without showing the camera? The technical problem is also the philosophical one.
- Cocteau's mercury mirrors (Orphée, 1950): the moment mirror merged with door (portal)
- The mirror-as-doppelgänger: Persona, Black Swan, Us, Perfect Blue
- Reflexivity: every mirror shot is cinema looking at itself
- Velázquez's Las Meninas → Foucault's analysis → cinematic equivalents

### Chapter 5: Cigarettes — The Most Culturally Volatile Object

- 74 films cataloged, 9 symbolic categories
- The cigarette's cultural biography: glamour (1920s-1950s) → danger (1964) → regulation (1970s-2000s) → nostalgia (2010s-present)
- Kopytoff's biographical transitions applied: each transition deposits new meaning
- Film noir: the cigarette IS the genre (smoke as atmosphere, the femme fatale's cigarette as weapon)
- The Hays Code and the cigarette as sex surrogate: Now, Voyager (1942), To Have and Have Not (1944)
- Post-regulation irony: every smoking scene after 1964 carries the ghost of mortality
- Penn's Cigarettes (1972): the photographic parallel — discarded butts as indexical portraits

### Chapter 6: Clocks — Cinema's Most Self-Referential Object

- 69 films cataloged, 10 symbolic categories
- Tarkovsky: "sculpting in time." The clock is the chisel.
- Cinema IS a time-based medium — putting a clock on screen is cinema acknowledging its own material
- High Noon (1952): the countdown as suspense architecture, Hitchcock's "bomb under the table"
- Nolan's temporal investigations: Dunkirk (pocket watch as soundtrack), Interstellar (each tick = one Earth day), Inception (nested temporal frames)
- Marclay's The Clock (2010): the ultimate synthesis — a 24-hour film of clocks, synchronized to real time
- Schaeffer's objet sonore: the tick-tock as one of the most culturally loaded sonic objects in existence

### Chapter 7: Doors — The Object That IS the Cut

- 70 films cataloged, 10 symbolic categories
- The door mirrors cinema's own grammar: the cut transports the viewer from one space/time to another; the frame defines the boundary between shown and excluded
- Benjamin's threshold vs. boundary: boundaries limit, thresholds transform
- Dorothy's farmhouse door onto Oz (1939): cinema's purest estrangement event — the door changes the film's entire visual system
- The Godfather's closing door: an entire arc of moral corruption performed in a single physical gesture
- Campbell's "crossing of the threshold": the hero's journey encoded in architecture
- HAL's pod bay doors: the door as technological adversary

### Chapter 8: Guns — The Object That IS America

- 76+ films cataloged, 10+ symbolic categories
- Latour's hybrid actor: "You are different with a gun in your hand; the gun is different with you holding it"
- The chronophotographic gun (1882) revisited: the gun and the camera as formally cognate apparatus
- The Western's foundational equation: gunfighter arrives, identifies injustice, resolves it through marksmanship
- Unforgiven (1992) as the medium's self-consciousness about guns: the "good guy with a gun" exposed as literary invention
- Violence as spectacle (John Woo, The Matrix, John Wick) vs. violence as trauma (Saving Private Ryan, Elephant)
- Chekhov's Gun as the foundational principle of dramatic economy — and its subversions

### Chapter 9: Cross-Object Analysis — The System of Cinematic Objects

- The density report: 13 films at 3+ object density, 2 films at 4/5 (A Clockwork Orange, Blade Runner)
- Co-occurrence patterns: door+mirror (most common), cigarette+milk (consumable pair), clock+door (doubled threshold), cigarette+clock (rarest — competing temporal anxieties)
- Director density profiles: Kubrick (2 films in high-density tier), Hitchcock (3 films)
- Decade distribution: the 1980s peak (investment in world-building genre films)
- Toward a network analysis of cinematic object co-occurrence
- The argument for cinematic privilege: only cinema combines indexicality, duration, materiality-in-motion, and montage in a way that makes object-attention empirically demonstrable at this scale

## 5. Paper II (Antithesis): Against Cinematic Privilege — Object-Attention Across Representational Media

**Disciplinary home:** Material Culture / Visual Culture Studies
**Core claim:** Object-attention is not cinema's invention. It predates cinema by centuries and operates across all representational media. Paper I's five mechanisms work in painting, literature, theatre, photography, music, and video games — but they work *differently*, and the differences reveal that cinema's "privilege" is a disciplinary bias, not an ontological fact.
**Evidence base:** Close readings of specific works across 6 non-cinematic media, with primary-source engagement at the level of individual artworks, novels, performances, photographs, compositions, and games.
**Target journals:** *Journal of Material Culture*, *Visual Studies*, *Oxford Art Journal*, *New Literary History*

### Chapter 10: The Vanitas Precedent — Object-Attention Before Cinema

- Object-attention has a 450-year history in Western art, beginning with the Dutch Golden Age still life (1580-1720)
- The vanitas painting as the first systematic practice of object-attention: arranging objects (skulls, candles, hourglasses, flowers, musical instruments) into compositions that produce meaning through spatial relationships
- Close readings: Pieter Claesz, *Vanitas Still Life* (1630); Willem Claesz. Heda, *Banquet Piece with Ham* (1656); Willem Kalf, *Still Life with Drinking-Horn* (c. 1653)
- The pronkstilleven's double address: celebrating abundance AND critiquing it simultaneously
- Norman Bryson's *Looking at the Overlooked* (1990): the still life as the genre that makes overlooked objects visible — anticipating the entire Object Lessons methodology by four centuries
- Argument against Paper I: estrangement and bifurcation were operating in painting before cinema existed. Cinema did not invent object-attention; it inherited it.

### Chapter 11: Chardin, Cézanne, Morandi — The Object Unmoored from Narrative

- Chardin (1699-1779): involuntary attention — the object's claim on consciousness precedes intellectual analysis. Diderot's response as phenomenological testimony.
- Cézanne (1880s-1906): the apple made present-at-hand. Schapiro's "The Apples of Cézanne" (1968). The object forced into visibility by the refusal of illusionistic transparency.
- Morandi (1920s-1964): 1,300 paintings of the same bottles. Accumulation within a single artist's career. Exhaustiveness as method — seeing the same thing a thousand times reveals more than seeing a thousand things once.
- Argument against Paper I: cinema's claim to "accumulation" assumes a cross-artist, cross-work process. Morandi proves that accumulation can operate within a single artist's sustained attention. The mechanism is not cinema-specific.

### Chapter 12: Duchamp, Warhol, Gonzalez-Torres — The Object in the Institutional Frame

- Duchamp's readymades (1913-1917): estrangement as pure reframing. The urinal proves that object-attention is a property of the *context*, not the *object*. Any object becomes meaningful in the right frame.
- Warhol's Campbell's Soup Cans (1962): the image of the commodity replaces the commodity. Sign-value supplants use-value. Baudrillard's system made visible.
- Gonzalez-Torres's candy spills (1991): object-attention through quantity — a pile of undifferentiated things as a portrait of a dying man. Object-attention without singularity.
- Boltanski's clothing installations: 50 tons of garments as mass memento mori. Object-attention at industrial scale.
- Hatoum's Homebound (2000): domestic objects wired with live electricity. Estrangement through danger — the kitchen becomes a minefield.
- Argument against Paper I: the gallery frame, the museum, the installation space are all technologies for producing object-attention. Cinema's frame is one among many, not the privileged one.

### Chapter 13: Objects in the Realist and Modernist Novel — Attention Without Indexicality

- Chekhov's gun: the foundational principle of dramatic economy. The object on the wall as narrative promise. Close reading of the letter to Lazarev-Gruzinsky (1889) and its dramaturgical implications.
- Eliot's objective correlative (1919): the "set of objects, a situation, a chain of events" that produces emotion without naming it. Objects do emotional work that language cannot.
- Barthes's "Reality Effect" (1968): the apparently insignificant detail (Flaubert's barometer) whose function is structural, not symbolic. But: accumulation transforms the reality effect into a symbolic field.
- Close readings: Flaubert's cap in *Madame Bovary* (a single object characterizing an entire personality), Proust's madeleine in *Swann's Way* (involuntary memory triggered by sensory contact — the phenomenology of object-attention from the inside), Woolf's lighthouse in *To the Lighthouse* (the object that organizes temporal experience across a decade)
- Argument against Paper I: literature achieves object-attention *without indexicality*. The reader never sees the madeleine — they construct it from language. This proves that indexicality (Paper I's first claim for cinematic privilege) is not necessary for object-attention. Imagination is sufficient.

### Chapter 14: Objects in Postmodern and Global Fiction — Proliferation, Enchantment, and the Object as Protagonist

- Pynchon's V-2 rocket in *Gravity's Rainbow* (1973): the object as protagonist — the weapon with a narrative arc (birth, career, death) more coherent than any human character's.
- DeLillo's supermarket in *White Noise* (1985): brand names as white noise of signification overwhelming human consciousness. Consumer objects replacing inner life.
- Perec's *Les Choses* (1965): an entire novel about a couple's relationship to consumer objects. Identity constituted through possession.
- García Márquez's magical realist objects in *One Hundred Years of Solitude* (1967): objects that acquire properties violating physics but feeling emotionally true. The flying carpet as mundane domestic object; blood that runs through streets to find its source.
- Close readings: 3 specific passages analyzed at the level of sentence-by-sentence formal attention to object-language
- Argument extension: literature demonstrates that object-attention can operate through *proliferation* (DeLillo, Perec) as well as *singularity* (Proust, Chekhov) — a distinction Paper I's cinema-centric model did not predict

### Chapter 15: Objects in Theatre and Performance — The Prop in Shared Space

- Sofer's *The Stage Life of Props* (2003): props acquire performative significance through stage action, not through inherent properties
- Brecht's gestus through objects: Mother Courage's wagon as embodied contradiction (survival + complicity). The object makes the system visible.
- Brook's empty space: the fewer objects, the more powerful each one. Minimalism as concentration of attention.
- Kantor's "reality of the lowest rank": damaged, decayed objects that carry history in their bodies. Indexicality without photography — the scratches, stains, and wear ARE the object's biography.
- Object theatre (Bread and Puppet, Handspring): objects as co-performers, not props. War Horse's bamboo-and-cloth horses producing genuine audience emotion despite visible artificiality.
- Argument against Paper I: theatre's objects share *physical space and time* with the audience. Cinema's objects are images — projections of light. Theatre's objects have *actual* materiality; cinema's have *represented* materiality. If anything, theatre has a stronger claim to object-attention because its objects are real, present, and tangible. Paper I's "materiality-in-motion" argument applies more powerfully to theatre than to cinema.

### Chapter 16: Objects in Photography and Sound Art — Index Without Duration, Sound Without Sight

- Weston's Pepper No. 30 (1930): the object as nude. Photography's indexicality without cinema's duration — the object frozen in a single moment of maximum presence.
- Penn's Cigarettes (1972): discarded butts as indexical portraits of absent persons. The object as trace.
- Tillmans's democratic leveling: any object is potentially significant. The question is not "which objects are important?" but "how does any object become important?"
- Evans and Agee, Let Us Now Praise Famous Men (1941): object-attention as ethical practice — attending to someone's possessions is attending to their life.
- Schaeffer's objet sonore: the sound object stripped of its referent. A train whistle without the train. Estrangement through aurality.
- Cage's 4'33" and prepared piano: ambient sound reframed as music; hardware-store materials reframed as musical agents.
- Attali's Noise (1977): instruments as technologies of social organization. The object shapes the social relationships of everyone who encounters it.
- Argument against Paper I: photography has indexicality without duration. Sound art has estrangement without visuality. Paper I claimed cinema's privilege rests on combining all four properties (indexicality, duration, materiality-in-motion, montage). Paper II shows that each property can produce object-attention *independently*. The combination is not necessary — it is merely convenient.

### Chapter 17: Objects in Video Games — Agency, Inventory, and the Mechanization of Attention

- The inventory system as ontology: the game's binary division of the world into interactive objects (significant) and environmental objects (furniture) is a mechanized version of object-attention. The system *enforces* attention through gameplay.
- Heidegger literalized: the weapon in the player's hand is ready-to-hand (transparent, used without reflection); the weapon in the inventory screen is present-at-hand (inspected, compared, considered). The menu screen IS the breakdown.
- Environmental storytelling: Gone Home (2013) — a complete narrative built entirely from picking up objects and looking at them. Object-attention as the sole gameplay mechanic.
- Dark Souls' item descriptions: narrative distributed across objects. The lore is not told through cutscenes — it is embedded in things. To understand the world, you must read your inventory.
- The Weighted Companion Cube (Portal, 2007): a featureless metal box that produces genuine emotional attachment through naming and forced cooperation. Bifurcation without intrinsic properties.
- Named weapons (Master Sword, Gjallarhorn): virtual objects generating attachment and community mythology comparable to physical objects.
- Walking simulators as phenomenological restoration: Dear Esther, What Remains of Edith Finch — the genre that insists looking is a complete activity, resisting the instrumentalization of objects.
- Argument against Paper I: games are the only medium where object-attention is *mechanically enforced*. The player MUST attend to objects because their survival depends on it. Cinema asks for voluntary attention; games *require* it. If we're ranking media by the intensity of object-attention they produce, games have a stronger claim than cinema. Paper I's "privilege" is not cinematic — it is ludic.

### Chapter 18: The Verdict Against Privilege — What the Cross-Media Evidence Shows

- Summary of contradictions: each medium challenges at least one of Paper I's four claims for cinematic privilege
  - Indexicality: literature achieves object-attention without it; imagination suffices
  - Duration: photography achieves object-attention in a single frozen moment
  - Materiality-in-motion: theatre's objects are MORE material than cinema's (actual vs. projected)
  - Montage: games produce montage through player agency, not editorial assembly
- Each mechanism (estrangement, accumulation, bifurcation, condensation, recursion) operates across all media studied — but with medium-specific variations that Paper I's cinema-centric formulation did not predict
- The five mechanisms are *medium-agnostic* in principle but *medium-inflected* in practice
- The question for Paper III: if object-attention is not cinema-specific, what IS it? And was Paper I wrong — or merely incomplete?

## 6. Paper III (Synthesis): Object-Attention — A Unified Theory of Why Things Mean

**Disciplinary home:** Cultural Studies / Interdisciplinary Humanities
**Core claim:** Both Papers I and II are right. Cinema IS the medium where object-attention is most visible and analytically productive — not because of ontological privilege but because of *empirical density* (cinema produces more object-rich works per year than any other medium) and *cultural circulation* (cinema reaches more people than gallery art, literary fiction, or experimental theatre). Object-attention itself is a general cultural process — not limited to art — that operates wherever humans encounter objects within representational or institutional frames.
**Evidence base:** Extension of the theory beyond art into politics, religion, commerce, law, domestic life. New theoretical synthesis drawing on all sources from Papers I and II plus additional frameworks (Bourdieu, Douglas, Mauss).
**Target:** University press monograph (Duke UP, Minnesota UP, MIT Press). This paper IS the book.

### Chapter 19: The Reconciliation — Why Cinema Is Special Without Being Privileged

- Paper I was right that cinema combines all four properties (indexicality, duration, materiality-in-motion, montage) in a unique configuration
- Paper II was right that none of these properties is *necessary* for object-attention — each can operate independently
- The synthesis: cinema's "privilege" is not ontological but *practical* — it is the medium where object-attention is most *empirically dense* (444 films per 6 objects in 100 years; no comparable corpus exists for painting, literature, or games) and most *culturally distributed* (a single viral film scene reaches more people than a gallery exhibition, a literary novel, or an indie game)
- Cinema is the best *laboratory* for studying object-attention — not because it invented the process but because it produces the most data
- The Object Lessons methodology is therefore *validated* by Paper II's cross-media critique: the theory works everywhere, and cinema is the most productive place to test it

### Chapter 20: Semiotic Affordance and Cultural Specificity — Why Some Objects and Not Others

- James J. Gibson's affordance extended semiotically: an object's *semiotic affordance* is the range of meanings its material properties can support
- Milk vs. water: milk has high semiotic affordance (whiteness, opacity, organic origin, maternal association, perishability, racial coding); water has low semiotic affordance (transparency, universality, cultural neutrality)
- Mirrors vs. windows: mirrors return your gaze (reflexivity, self-confrontation); windows let you look through (view, longing, barrier — but no self-confrontation)
- Guns vs. knives: guns kill at a distance (impersonal power, institutional violence, technological extension); knives require proximity (intimacy, effort, bodily engagement)
- Cultural specificity as the second criterion: objects embedded in specific cultural contradictions (innocence/corruption, justice/violence, pleasure/death) are more symbolically productive than objects with universal, uncontested meanings
- The "knot" metaphor: the most productive objects are those where cultural contradictions are tied too tightly to be pulled apart

### Chapter 21: Object-Attention Beyond Art — Politics, Religion, Commerce, Law

- **Political iconography:** The flag, the podium, the ballot, the gavel. Objects that condense institutional authority into graspable forms. The American flag's semiotic career: liberty → patriotism → imperialism → protest (flag-burning) → nostalgia → polarization. Object-attention in political campaigning: the hard hat (working class), the rolled-up sleeve (readiness), the lapel pin (allegiance).
- **Religious ritual:** The cross, the chalice, the Torah scroll, the prayer rug. Objects that condense theological systems into material forms. Marcel Mauss's *The Gift* (1925): the sacred object as gift that creates obligation. Mary Douglas's *Purity and Danger* (1966): objects that mark boundaries between sacred and profane, clean and unclean.
- **Commerce and branding:** The logo, the packaging, the store layout. Baudrillard's sign-value made architectural. Apple's product design as object-attention engineering: the unboxing experience, the weight of the device, the click of the keyboard. Objects designed to produce estrangement (the unfamiliar made desirable) and condensation (an entire lifestyle compressed into a device).
- **Legal evidence:** The murder weapon, the contract, the exhibit. Objects whose meaning is *adjudicated* — a jury must decide what an object means. Legal object-attention is coercive: you MUST attend to the exhibit, you MUST assign it significance. The courtroom is a frame (like the gallery, the cinema, the stage) that produces object-attention by institutional fiat.
- **Domestic life:** The wedding ring, the family photograph, the heirloom. Daniel Miller's *The Comfort of Things* (2008): objects as constitutive of personhood — people become themselves through things. The everyday object as site of involuntary memory (Proust) and identity maintenance (Miller).

### Chapter 22: The Five Mechanisms Revised — A General Theory

- Reassessment of each mechanism in light of Papers I, II, and III:
  - **Estrangement** is universal but the *frame* that produces it varies by medium and context: the gallery, the cinema, the stage, the page, the game screen, the courtroom, the ritual space, the brand experience
  - **Accumulation** operates at different rates in different media: cinema accumulates fastest (high production volume, rapid cultural circulation); painting accumulates slowest (one object at a time, centuries of tradition). Rate of accumulation does not determine depth — Morandi's 1,300 paintings of the same bottles are as deep as cinema's 71 milks across 85 years
  - **Bifurcation** is most intense where cultural contradictions are most acute: the gun in American cinema, the cigarette in post-regulation culture, the cross in a secular society. Bifurcation is culturally indexed, not medium-specific
  - **Condensation** is most powerful for objects that serve as portals to hyperobjects (Morton): the gun condenses American gun culture, the cigarette condenses the tobacco industry's century, the smartphone condenses the surveillance economy. Objects are meaningful when they make the ungraspable graspable
  - **Recursion** is the mechanism that makes object-attention a *living system* rather than a static taxonomy. Each new use revises all previous uses. The system never closes. This is why objects are inexhaustible — and why a dissertation about them is, in principle, unfinishable

### Chapter 23: The Medium-Inflection Thesis

- The central theoretical contribution: object-attention is a *medium-agnostic process* with *medium-specific inflections*
- Each medium modulates the five mechanisms differently:
  - Cinema: indexicality + duration + montage = the fastest accumulation, the most visible estrangement, the most empirically demonstrable patterns
  - Painting: spatial composition + material permanence = the deepest accumulation within single works, the most controlled estrangement
  - Literature: linguistic construction + reader imagination = object-attention without indexicality, bifurcation through metaphor rather than visual juxtaposition
  - Theatre: shared space + liveness + materiality = the most visceral estrangement, the most physical condensation
  - Photography: indexicality + stillness = the most intense single-moment estrangement, the most forensic accumulation
  - Music/Sound: aurality + temporal unfolding = estrangement through decontextualized sound, condensation through sonic compression
  - Games: interactivity + agency + mechanical enforcement = the most coercive object-attention, the most instrumentalized accumulation
- The medium-inflection thesis resolves the Papers I-II dispute: cinema IS special (its inflection is uniquely productive for empirical study) but NOT privileged (it is one inflection among many, each with its own strengths)

### Chapter 24: Toward a General Semiotics of Object-Attention

- Relationship to existing theoretical frameworks:
  - Extends Appadurai: objects have social lives not just in commodity circulation but in representational circulation — every appearance in art is a phase in the object's cultural biography
  - Extends Barthes: mythologies of objects are not static (as Barthes's synchronic analysis suggests) but *diachronic* — they change across time, and the change is the interesting part
  - Extends Heidegger: the ready-to-hand/present-at-hand distinction is not just a phenomenological observation but a *cultural process* that art systematically produces and that criticism can systematically study
  - Extends Harman: OOO's claim that objects withdraw is confirmed by the empirical evidence — no single film, no single analysis exhausts an object's meaning — but the *productive* consequence of withdrawal is not philosophical speculation but *recursive artistic engagement*
  - Extends Bennett: thing-power is not merely an ontological claim but an *observable cultural phenomenon* — objects in art demonstrably act on viewers, producing responses (fear, nostalgia, desire, revulsion) that exceed the artist's intention
- What object-attention adds: a *processual* account of how objects become meaningful — not a taxonomy of meanings but a theory of meaning-*production* that is testable, revisable, and applicable across media and cultural contexts

### Chapter 25: The Ethics and Limits of Object-Attention

- When is object-attention a form of *care* (Evans/Agee attending to sharecroppers' possessions) and when is it a form of *fetishism* (commodity worship, collector obsession)?
- The danger of overinterpretation: Eco's unlimited semiosis warns that once an object enters the semiotic field, meaning proliferates without limit. Not every glass of milk is significant. The methodology must distinguish between objects-as-furniture and objects-as-things without arbitrarily imposing significance.
- The danger of universalism: the theory claims medium-agnosticism, but it was developed from a Western art-historical tradition. How does object-attention operate in non-Western representational systems? In Islamic geometric art (which historically avoids figurative representation)? In Chinese scroll painting (which uses objects differently from Western still life)? In African masking traditions (where the object IS the performer)?
- The danger of presentism: the theory reads historical artworks through a contemporary theoretical framework. Did Hitchcock "do" object-attention? Or is that a retrospective imposition? The answer matters for the theory's claims about accumulation and recursion.
- Limitations acknowledged: the empirical corpus is Western-centric, cinema-heavy, and English-language-dominated. Future work should extend to non-Western cinema, non-Western art traditions, and non-English-language cultural systems.

## 7. Coda: The Unfinished Archive

### Chapter 26: Recursion as Structural Principle

- The dissertation performs what it describes: Paper I proposes a theory, Paper II revises it, Paper III revises the revision. This is recursion — each paper references and modifies all previous papers.
- The archive is unfinished by design: objects do not stop meaning. A glass of milk placed on a table in a film released tomorrow will carry the weight of everything described in this dissertation — and will deposit a new layer for everything that comes after.
- The theory of object-attention is itself an object: it will be estranged (placed in contexts the author did not intend), accumulated (cited, built upon, criticized), bifurcated (its descriptive function will split from its normative implications), condensed (summarized in ways that compress its complexity), and recursed (each new application will revise its meaning).
- Final statement: "We look at the things that no one looks at. That is the lesson."
